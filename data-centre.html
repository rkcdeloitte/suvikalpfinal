Final Recommendations / Data Centres
Data Centers
Global Precedents
Bahnhof Pionen Data Centre, Stockholm, Sweden

Data centres are specialized facilities designed to store, process, and manage digital information, forming the backbone of modern digital infrastructure and services. In the context of post-mining landscapes, reclaimed land offers a strategic opportunity for establishing data centres due to the availability of large land parcels and potential access to power and connectivity infrastructure, enabling the transformation of previously degraded areas into high-value technology hubs that support digital and economic growth.

The development of data centres in such regions can create skilled employment opportunities for local communities, particularly those transitioning from mining-based livelihoods, by generating roles in operations, maintenance, security, and technical services. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring reliable power supply, efficient cooling systems, robust connectivity, environmental sustainability, and adherence to data security and regulatory standards to ensure long-term viability and operational efficiency.

PROJECT VISION

A data centre is a capital-intensive, infrastructure-led, operator-driven repurposing option — transforming degraded mine land into high-value digital infrastructure. Sites must satisfy critical requirements for power, fiber, cooling, geotechnical stability, and security.

This concept proposes the development of a Data Centre / Digital Infrastructure Campus within a closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine area. The objective is to transform suitable mine land into a high-value digital infrastructure use that supports regional diversification, productive reuse, skilled employment, and long-term lease/concession revenue.

The project may include: data hall buildings / modular white-space blocks, disaster-recovery / backup modules, edge / regional cloud infrastructure blocks, AI / HPC-ready compute blocks, NOC/SOC facilities, substations and redundant electrical systems, cooling plant, telecom and fiber infrastructure, warehousing and logistics support, renewable-energy integration, and BESS.

For data centres, reliability and operational sustainability matter more than mere land availability. Power, connectivity, disaster risk, water/cooling strategy, and operating capability are decisive factors.

A data centre can be a strong repurposing option where a closed coal mine site offers a combination of:

  • Large contiguous land parcels
  • Existing or expandable high-voltage power infrastructure
  • Potential for dedicated renewable-energy linkage
  • Low competing urban land-use pressure
  • Controlled perimeter and industrial zoning character
  • Proximity to trunk fiber routes or feasible fiber extension
  • Adequate physical security and setback potential
  • Institutional support for post-mining repurposing

The first RFP should not go out until power, fiber, land status, and geotechnical suitability are reasonably established — those are the biggest project killers.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Before issuing the first RFP, the mine owner must establish power, fiber, land status, and geotechnical suitability. These are the biggest project killers if not resolved pre-tender. Model-wise responsibility must be clearly defined to protect uptime, security, and local livelihood obligations.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing the first RFP for a Data Centre / Digital Infrastructure Campus in a repurposed mine area, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership / possession, Mine Closure Plan linkage, post-mining land-use permissibility, and suitability of the proposed area for industrial / digital-infrastructure use.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review lease / concession rights, development rights, commercial-use permissibility, utility-right-of-way requirements, revenue-sharing permissibility, and insurance / liability allocation.
  3. Safe-zone and no-go-zone mapping — Identify stable and unstable areas, including subsidence-prone land, unsafe water edges, restricted mine infrastructure, environmental buffer zones, and utility corridors.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, drainage and flood-risk review, access-road assessment, and preliminary land-platform / grading feasibility.
  5. Power and utility feasibility assessment — Assess availability of grid power, substation connectivity, redundancy potential, water / treated-water availability, backup-power arrangement, and utility-corridor readiness. Data-centre policy discussions in India have specifically emphasized ready infrastructure and reliable power.
  6. Telecom and fibre-connectivity assessment — Identify proximity to fibre routes, telecom backhaul, route diversity, and feasibility of secure, redundant digital connectivity. TRAI's data-centre consultation framework specifically addressed data centres, CDNs, and interconnect exchanges as part of the wider data economy.
  7. Environmental and contamination screening — Assess soil condition, water quality, residual contamination risk, dust exposure, drainage behaviour, and broad environmental suitability for long-term critical infrastructure use.
  8. Functional and market suitability assessment — Determine whether the site is more suitable for edge, disaster recovery, enterprise colocation, or multi-tenant data-centre park development, based on power, connectivity, access, and likely market demand. MeitY's ongoing policy work and the existing State Data Centre guidance support formal planning for data-centre infrastructure in India.
  9. Preliminary zoning and campus layout — Prepare a basic layout showing data-hall zone, utility yard, substation area, cooling plant, admin / security block, internal circulation, parking, service access, emergency routes, and future expansion area.
  10. Approvals pathway mapping — Map likely approvals including building plan approval / building permit, Fire NOC / Fire Safety Certificate, electrical safety approvals / inspection, water and drainage permissions, and later Completion Certificate / Occupancy Certificate, as applicable under the local framework. The Model Building Bye-Laws, 2016 and fire-safety processes provide the core public-building approval logic, while CEA regulations apply to electrical installations.
  11. Cost and implementation-model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, or EPC + O&M / Facility Management.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid-evaluation framework — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, utility integration, operations, uptime management, security, compliance, local employment, and ESG obligations; and define evaluation criteria covering data-centre experience, technical capability, financial capacity, O&M plan, power / connectivity strategy, and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / data-centre operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, geotechnical investigation, hydrology / drainage assessment, utility mapping, access assessment, and detailed platform-development studies.
  2. Detailed power, cooling, and connectivity planning — Prepare detailed plans for incoming power, substation / switching arrangement, backup systems, cooling strategy, water systems, fibre entry, telecom redundancy, and utility routing. India's policy consultations on data centres have emphasized reliable power and digital connectivity as core enablers.
  3. Final master planning — Prepare the final data-centre master plan showing data halls, electrical and mechanical areas, NOC / SOC spaces, utility corridors, access control zones, parking, service areas, and future phases.
  4. Detailed engineering and design — Prepare detailed architectural, structural, electrical, mechanical, cooling, fire-safety, telecom, and security-system designs.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — Prepare the DPR including detailed surveys, final master plan, detailed engineering, financial model, approvals plan, O&M plan, implementation schedule, and phasing plan for owner approval before execution.
  6. Approvals and compliance processing — Obtain project-specific approvals including building plan approval / building permit, fire clearance, electrical inspection / approval, utility connections, and, on completion, Completion Certificate and Occupancy Certificate, as applicable. Fire authorities commonly require compliance before occupancy, and electrical installations fall under CEA's safety regulations.
  7. Procurement of systems and equipment — Procure electrical systems, cooling equipment, DG / backup systems, UPS / batteries, telecom equipment, racks / white-space systems, CCTV, access control, BMS / DCIM, and fire-detection / suppression systems.
  8. Construction and installation — Execute grading, civil works, utility works, substation and cooling infrastructure, internal roads, drainage, buildings, security systems, and data-centre technical fit-out.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for facility operations, uptime management, incident response, electrical safety, security, access control, maintenance, emergency shutdown, and business continuity.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train technical staff, security personnel, facility-management teams, utility operators, and local support staff for operations and maintenance.
  11. Trial operations and commissioning tests — Conduct integrated testing, electrical and cooling-system trials, fire-safety checks, redundancy testing, load trials, and pre-commissioning readiness verification.
  12. Commercial onboarding and phased expansion — Start customer / tenant onboarding, operationalize the first phase, and expand additional modules in phases based on demand, power readiness, network readiness, compliance, and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

For mine-area data centres, the preferred model is Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate — preserving owner land control while bringing in private financing, specialist design, and operational expertise. DBFOT and Hybrid are the next most suitable options.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (2) DBFOT, (3) Hybrid

Why preferred: Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is preferred because data centres are high-CAPEX, highly specialized and operator-driven. The mine owner can retain land control while the private developer handles financing, technology, clients, security and O&M.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be considered only for captive, sovereign, PSU or government-led data infrastructure where the owner has a clear use case.

Suggestable technical care: Power reliability, fibre connectivity, cooling, water availability, land stability, flood risk, security, fire safety, redundancy, renewable power supply and data protection requirements must be assessed.

Recommended development logic: Start with land, power and connectivity screening. Proceed only where grid capacity, fibre backbone, cooling feasibility and tenant demand are strong.
