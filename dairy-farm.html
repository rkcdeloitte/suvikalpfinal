Final Recommendations / Dairy Farming & Milk Value-Chain Clusters
Dairy Farming
Global Precedents
Dairy Farm Stable, Katehra, Punjab, India
Dairy farms function as integrated agricultural units focused on milk production, livestock management, and allied activities, contributing to food supply and rural development. Within post-mining landscapes, reclaimed land can be effectively utilized for establishing dairy farms by supporting fodder cultivation, cattle rearing, and associated infrastructure, thereby transforming previously degraded areas into productive and sustainable agro-based systems.
Such initiatives can provide stable income and employment opportunities for local communities, particularly those who were earlier dependent on mining, through activities such as animal husbandry, milk processing, and distribution. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring availability of water and fodder, maintaining animal health and veterinary support, managing waste sustainably, and developing reliable market linkages and cold-chain infrastructure to ensure efficiency and long-term viability of dairy farming operations.

PROJECT VISION

Project overview, strategic rationale, site suitability assessment, and indicative dairy scale and configuration matrix for closed coal mine areas.

  • What: Dairy farming — milch cattle and buffalo rearing, milk production, value-added processing and circular economy — on reclaimed or safe-to-use closed coal mine land
  • Why it fits mine land: Reclaimed opencast land with flat or gently sloping terrain, available water bodies, green belts and community access is well-suited for fodder cultivation, cattle housing and milk handling infrastructure
  • National alignment: Supports National Dairy Development Board (NDDB) goals, National Action Plan for Dairy Development, GOBARdhan, National Livestock Mission and PM Kisan Samridhi objectives
  • Who benefits: Land-losers, SHGs, FPOs, unemployed youth, small dairy producers, dairy cooperative members and tribal / rural households in mining-affected areas
  • Mine-closure alignment: Supports ESG commitments, just transition obligations, mine closure plan requirements and meaningful post-closure land utilisation
  • Circular economy integration: Cattle dung to biogas / CBG, vermicompost and organic manure (GOBARdhan); fodder cultivation on reclaimed land; wash-water treatment
  • Generates regular daily income through milk sales — unlike seasonal crops, revenue is year-round
  • Supports land-losers, rural households, women SHGs, youth and small producers with direct livelihood integration
  • Creates year-round employment in animal care, fodder production, milk handling, transport, feed management, dung management and processing
  • Reclaimed mine land supports fodder cultivation, silage production, grazing-control zones and green belts — productive use of otherwise idle land
  • Supports circular economy through cattle dung-based biogas, compost, organic manure and bio-slurry under the GOBARdhan scheme
  • Enables value addition through curd, paneer, ghee, khoa, flavoured milk, butter, buttermilk and packaged milk — significantly higher margins than raw milk
  • Can be integrated with existing dairy cooperatives, milk unions, FPOs and private dairy processors for assured procurement
  • Aligns with ESG commitments, just transition mandates, rural livelihood targets and mine-closure obligations

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP preparation checklist, post-award activity scope, and indicative implementation pathway for dairy-based mine repurposing.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a dairy-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of dairy farming, fodder cultivation, milk collection, milk chilling and allied livelihood use.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease / concession rights, cattle ownership, milk ownership, produce rights, user charges, revenue sharing and community participation conditions.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, mine voids, contaminated areas, unsafe water bodies and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, drainage review, slope-stability screening, access assessment and location suitability for cattle sheds, fodder plots, milk collection point and manure area.
  5. Soil, water and contamination screening — Assess soil quality, groundwater / surface-water quality, dust exposure and contamination risk before allowing fodder cultivation or cattle-related use.
  6. Fodder and water availability assessment — Estimate green fodder, dry fodder, silage potential and assured water requirement for cattle drinking, shed cleaning, fodder irrigation and milk-handling operations.
  7. Animal husbandry and veterinary linkage plan — Map nearby veterinary hospital, artificial insemination centre, vaccination support, disease-control services and livestock insurance support.
  8. Milk market and procurement assessment — Identify nearby dairy cooperative, milk union, private dairy processor, bulk milk cooler, milk route and demand for raw milk or value-added dairy products.
  9. Environmental and waste-management screening — Prepare preliminary dung, urine, wash-water and solid-waste management plan in line with CPCB / SPCB expectations for dairy farms and gaushalas. CPCB guidelines cover dung utilization, solid-waste management, wastewater management and environmental safeguards for dairy farms.
  10. Food safety and clean milk compliance note — Identify applicability of FSSAI registration / licence, clean milk production, milk testing, hygienic handling, chilling and processing requirements. FSSAI standards define requirements for milk and milk products, while NPDD supports quality and clean milk production awareness.
  11. Community and beneficiary mapping — Map land-losers, SHGs, FPOs, dairy farmers, unemployed youth, women groups, panchayats and local entrepreneurs for dairy-linked livelihood integration.
  12. Preliminary dairy zoning and carrying-capacity plan — Prepare basic layout showing cattle shed area, fodder plots, milk collection point, BMC location, feed store, veterinary room, dung yard, compost / biogas area, internal roads and buffer zones.
  13. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, EPC + O&M, DBFOT or BOOT.
  14. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, operation, cattle care, fodder management, milk handling, veterinary linkage, local employment, SHG / FPO participation, waste management and food-safety compliance.
  15. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering dairy-sector experience, animal-health management, clean milk practices, financial capacity, market linkage, O&M plan, community integration, environmental safeguards and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / dairy operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, soil testing, water testing, drainage study, access planning, fodder-land assessment and final safety verification.
  2. Detailed dairy master planning — Prepare the final dairy master plan showing cattle sheds, calf pens, fodder plots, feed store, milking area, milk collection / chilling area, dung yard, biogas / compost unit, veterinary area, service roads and buffer zones.
  3. Detailed Project Report — Prepare the DPR covering herd size, breed plan, fodder plan, shed design, water system, milk-handling system, waste-management system, financial model, implementation schedule and statutory compliance plan.
  4. Approvals, registrations and linkages — Obtain required local permissions, FSSAI registration / licence where applicable, SPCB / local-body compliance for waste and wastewater, veterinary linkages, insurance arrangements and dairy cooperative / processor tie-ups.
  5. Detailed engineering and shed design — Prepare detailed designs for cattle sheds, drainage, feed storage, silage pits, water troughs, milking area, BMC room, internal roads, fencing, lighting and sanitation facilities.
  6. Procurement of cattle and equipment — Procure approved breeds / animals, milking equipment, milk cans, testing kits, BMC if required, feed equipment, water systems, veterinary first-aid kits and manure-handling tools.
  7. Fodder development and feed planning — Develop green fodder plots, silage pits, dry fodder storage and feed-supply agreements. NPDD and dairy-development programmes emphasize clean milk production, green fodder, cattle feed and mineral-mixture adoption.
  8. Construction and installation — Construct cattle sheds, calf pens, milk room, BMC room, feed store, water points, drainage, dung collection area, composting / biogas unit, fencing and internal access roads.
  9. Biosecurity and animal-health protocol — Prepare SOPs for animal induction, quarantine, vaccination, deworming, disease reporting, artificial insemination, veterinary visits and mortality management.
  10. Clean milk production protocol — Prepare SOPs for milking hygiene, equipment cleaning, milk testing, milk chilling, storage, transport and rejection of contaminated / poor-quality milk.
  11. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train local youth, women SHGs, cattle attendants, milk handlers, fodder workers, sanitation workers and supervisors in dairy operations and hygiene practices.
  12. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, FPOs, dairy farmers, milk pourers, fodder growers, transport providers, compost users and local entrepreneurs for dairy-linked income generation.
  13. Waste-to-wealth system setup — Set up dung composting, vermicompost, biogas or CBG linkage where feasible. GOBARdhan promotes conversion of cattle dung and biodegradable waste into biogas, bio-slurry, CBG and organic manure.
  14. Trial operation and health audit — Conduct trial milking, milk testing, BMC operation, feed trials, animal-health checks, waste-management checks and safety inspection before full operation.
  15. Market linkage and branding — Finalize milk procurement agreements, cooperative / processor linkage, local branding, value-added product plan and digital record system for milk yield and payments.
  16. Commissioning and regular operation — Start regular dairy operations after owner approval, with daily milk recording, animal-health monitoring, fodder tracking, waste-management reporting and community livelihood monitoring.
  17. Monitoring and phased expansion — Expand herd size, fodder area, BMC capacity, milk processing, compost / biogas operations and SHG participation based on milk yield, market demand, animal health, environmental compliance and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward recommendations, key references, and abbreviations for dairy-based mine repurposing projects.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) EPC + O&M / Facility Management, (4) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because dairy projects need land, fodder, veterinary services, producer participation and market procurement. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner land + SHG / FPO dairy producers + cooperative / private dairy buyer + veterinary and fodder support agency.

Secondary option: BOOT may be used where commercial chilling, processing and eventual asset transfer are desired.

Suggestable technical care: Fodder availability, water quality, animal housing, veterinary support, chilling, disease control, waste management, biogas, market procurement and animal welfare must be planned.

Recommended development logic: Start with a dairy-fodder livelihood cluster and milk collection / chilling facility. Add processing, biogas and branded products only after supply and procurement are stable.
