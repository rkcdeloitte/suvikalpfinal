Industrial Hub / Plug-and-Play Industrial Estate
Global Precedents
Zeche Zollverein Industrial Hub, Essen, Germany
In India, industrial hubs are planned clusters of manufacturing, processing, and allied industrial units that drive regional economic growth by leveraging infrastructure, logistics connectivity, and skilled labour availability. Post-mining landscapes across coal-bearing states present a strategic opportunity to develop such hubs by utilizing large parcels of reclaimed land, existing road and rail networks, and power infrastructure, thereby channelling investment into industrial activities that support regional diversification and economic transition.
The establishment of industrial hubs can generate significant employment and business opportunities for local communities, particularly those previously dependent on mining, through roles in manufacturing, logistics, technical operations, and ancillary services. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring land readiness and environmental clearances, developing robust connectivity and utility infrastructure, attracting anchor industries and investors, and establishing effective governance and management frameworks to ensure the long-term competitiveness and sustainability of industrial hub operations.

PROJECT VISION

Concept for Industrial Hub / Plug-and-Play Industrial Estate on closed coal mine land — covering MSME manufacturing, light engineering, warehousing, logistics, common facility centres, and skill development. Site suitability, scale planning, and strategic rationale are covered here.

This concept proposes the development of an Industrial Hub / Plug-and-Play Industrial Estate on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land into a productive industrial ecosystem supporting: MSME manufacturing units; light engineering and fabrication units; warehousing and logistics facilities; repair, maintenance, and service workshops; green-industry units; local enterprise zones; common facility centres; skill development and training centres; and worker amenities and support infrastructure.

Unlike conventional greenfield industrial development, closed mine land offers large contiguous parcels, existing road access, nearby labour settlements, power evacuation corridors, and scope for structured land-use zoning. As per Government of India information, more than 4,500 industrial parks are mapped on the India Industrial Land Bank, with 306 plug-and-play parks and 20 NICDC-led parks/smart cities already operational — providing benchmark standards for mine-repurposed industrial hubs.

Industrial Hub development is suited for closed coal mine areas where land is safe, accessible, legally available, and close to markets, roads, rail links, industrial clusters, or labour settlements.

  • Converts closed mine land into productive economic assets
  • Creates long-term non-mining employment for mine-dependent communities
  • Supports MSMEs, local contractors, mining-service firms, and new entrepreneurs
  • Enables just transition for mine-dependent communities
  • Can reuse existing approach roads, power corridors, workshops, yards, and administrative buildings where safe
  • Supports plug-and-play industrial development with ready infrastructure
  • Reduces pressure on fresh greenfield land acquisition
  • Generates lease rent, user charges, maintenance fees, and possible revenue share
  • Can be phased according to demand and land safety clearance
  • Supports skill development and local enterprise incubation
  • Can integrate green infrastructure such as solar rooftops, wastewater recycling, stormwater harvesting, and green buffers
  • Aligns with IPRS 3.0 benchmarks: sustainability, logistics, digitalization, skill linkages, and tenant feedback

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP readiness for Industrial Hub development on closed mine land requires land safety confirmation, geotechnical investigation, environmental screening, market demand assessment, and community and MSME consultation. Post-award activities include detailed planning, statutory approvals, trunk infrastructure, plug-and-play shed development, tenant mobilisation, and phased industrial estate operations led by the selected developer, concessionaire, or operator.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an Industrial Hub-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and mine-closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, approved mine-closure linkage, and permissibility of industrial / MSME / logistics / plug-and-play estate use under the applicable mine-closure plan.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land conversion requirement, lease / concession rights, allotment powers, revenue-sharing permissibility, industrial estate operation rights, and liability for third-party industrial users.
  3. Safe and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, abandoned workings, restricted infrastructure, waterlogged areas, and areas unsuitable for industrial loading.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, bearing-capacity assessment, drainage review, road-access assessment, and broad suitability for plots, sheds, utilities, parking, and truck movement.
  5. Environmental and contamination screening — Assess soil, groundwater, dust, legacy waste, mine drainage, ecological sensitivity, and suitability for industrial reuse before allowing any plot allotment or construction.
  6. Industrial demand and sector suitability assessment — Identify feasible sectors such as MSME fabrication, repair workshops, warehousing, logistics, agro-processing, renewable-energy assembly, service units, or low-pollution manufacturing based on local demand and site risk.
  7. Utility and infrastructure readiness assessment — Assess power, water, internal roads, stormwater drainage, sewage, solid waste, street lighting, firefighting, digital connectivity, and common infrastructure requirements for industrial estate development.
  8. Preliminary zoning and layout plan — Prepare a basic master layout showing industrial plots, plug-and-play sheds, common facility centre, utilities, green buffer, truck circulation, emergency access, parking, worker amenities, and no-go areas.
  9. Policy and scheme alignment — Check alignment with industrial-park benchmarks, plug-and-play industrial park models, MSME Cluster Development Programme, Common Facility Centre support, and Industrial Park Rating System parameters such as sustainability, logistics, digitalization, and tenant services.
  10. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult district administration, state industrial development corporation, industries department, pollution control board, MSME / DIC office, panchayats, local communities, SHGs, youth groups, and potential industrial users.
  11. Cost, revenue and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX, 5-year O&M cost, lease / rental / maintenance fee assumptions, and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, BOOT, or EPC + O&M.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid evaluation framework — Prepare bidder scope covering design, finance, construction, utility development, statutory compliance, estate management, tenant facilitation, local employment, ESG safeguards, and evaluation criteria for technical capability, industrial-park experience, financial capacity, O&M plan, and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / industrial estate developer can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, contamination assessment, drainage study, road-capacity assessment, and final demarcation of safe development zones.
  2. Detailed Project Report and final master plan — Prepare the DPR covering final land-use plan, plot layout, plug-and-play sheds, utilities, roads, drainage, power, water, waste systems, common facilities, financial model, approvals plan, and implementation schedule.
  3. Statutory approvals and institutional tie-ups — Obtain applicable land-use, industrial-estate, environmental, building, fire, electricity, water, factory-related and local-body approvals, and establish coordination with state industrial agencies and MSME / DIC institutions.
  4. Detailed engineering and infrastructure design — Prepare detailed designs for internal roads, stormwater drains, water supply, power distribution, street lighting, sewage / STP, solid waste management, fire network, ICT systems, boundary, and worker amenities.
  5. Common facilities and MSME-support planning — Design the Common Facility Centre, testing / tool room, training centre, raw material depot, warehouse, canteen, first-aid centre, and other need-based facilities in line with MSME cluster-development principles.
  6. Construction of trunk and plot-level infrastructure — Develop land grading, roads, drains, compound wall, utilities, power systems, water systems, sewerage, fire-safety systems, green buffers, signage, and access-control infrastructure.
  7. Development of plug-and-play and common facilities — Construct ready sheds, flatted factory units, warehouses, logistics yards, CFC, admin office, worker amenities, parking, truck bays, and digital estate-management systems.
  8. Tenant mobilization and allotment framework — Prepare plot / shed allotment rules, lease terms, user charges, maintenance charges, eligible industry list, environmental conditions, local-employment clauses, and tenant onboarding process.
  9. Local livelihood and skill integration — Onboard local youth, contractors, SHGs, service providers, transporters, and training institutions for construction jobs, estate services, maintenance contracts, MSME support, and industrial skilling.
  10. Trial operation and compliance validation — Test power, water, drainage, STP, fire systems, access control, estate-management software, emergency routes, and facility services before allowing full industrial occupation.
  11. Commissioning and industrial operations — Commission the hub after safety, utility, and statutory readiness, and begin phased tenant occupation, estate management, billing, security, maintenance, and compliance monitoring.
  12. Regular O&M, reporting and tenant support — Carry out routine maintenance, utility management, environmental monitoring, waste management, grievance redressal, tenant facilitation, safety audits, and performance reporting to the mine owner.
  13. Phased expansion and upgradation — Expand plots, sheds, logistics areas, common facilities, worker amenities, renewable-energy systems, and digital services in phases based on tenant demand, revenue performance, safety record, environmental compliance, and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

The Hybrid Industrial Hub model is the most recommended approach for industrial-hub-based mine repurposing. This section outlines the preferred implementation models, key references, and abbreviations used throughout the PRISM framework.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because industrial hubs need owner control over land use, environmental safeguards and local employment while attracting private units and operators. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner land / trunk infrastructure + private estate operator + MSME tenants + skill / incubation support.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable for smaller owner-controlled industrial estates with strong welfare or local employment objectives.

Suggestable technical care: Land suitability, access roads, utilities, zoning, pollution category, fire safety, waste treatment, stormwater, worker facilities and local employment conditions must be defined.

Recommended development logic: Start with low-to-moderate pollution, employment-intensive sectors such as fabrication, repair, warehousing, agro-processing and renewable-component assembly. Avoid hazardous or heavy-polluting industries unless statutory safeguards are strong.
