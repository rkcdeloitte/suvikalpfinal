Final Recommendations / Physics Lab / R&D Facility
Physics Lab
Global Precedent
India-based Neutrino Observatory (INO) — ICAL Detector, Bodi Hills, Theni, Tamil Nadu

A Physics Lab / Laboratory / R&D Facility is a knowledge-based mine-closure repurposing option that converts suitable closed or abandoned mine infrastructure into a science, testing, training, innovation, and applied research asset. It can take the form of a teaching laboratory, applied physics and instrumentation lab, NABL-accredited calibration lab, mine-environment R&D facility, underground geoscience lab, or a hybrid science park — depending on site conditions, regional demand, and funding model.

Unlike heavy industrial repurposing, a laboratory-led reuse model creates a knowledge-based post-mining economy. It can support technical education, reskilling of local youth and mine workers, applied research, NABL calibration services, environmental monitoring, mine-closure data collection, renewable-energy testing, and STEM outreach — while productively reusing existing mine buildings, workshops, power systems, and communication infrastructure with lower land intensity than most alternative uses.

PROJECT VISION

Post-mining context, Physics Lab / Science and Innovation Campus configurations, site suitability, and indicative scale benchmarks for education, STEM, and science outreach repurposing.

This concept proposes the development of a Physics Lab / Science and Innovation Campus within or around a closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine area as part of a broader post-mining land-use, education, scientific-capacity, and local livelihood strategy.

The objective is to transform suitable mine land, reclaimed industrial land, existing administrative buildings, workshops, training centres, warehouses, unused colonies, and selected legacy infrastructure into a science-based education, research, training, outreach, testing, and innovation asset that supports school and college practical learning, youth skill development, STEM education, mine-region knowledge transition, technical training, local employment, institutional collaboration, and long-term lease or concession revenue.

This should be treated as a selective repurposing option, not a universal one. A closed coal mine is suitable for a Physics Lab / Science and Innovation Campus only if it can satisfy critical requirements relating to safe and stable land free from subsidence risk, confirmed mine-closure permissibility, building safety and structural stability, adequate road access, power supply, water supply and sanitation, distance from unstable pit edges, highwalls, dumps, shafts and contaminated water bodies, suitability for students, trainees, researchers, teachers, visitors and community users, and environmental safety.

Depending on site potential and project ambition, the proposed project may include one or more of the following components:

  • School-level physics laboratory for Classes 6–12 practical demonstrations
  • College-level undergraduate physics laboratory
  • Advanced instrumentation laboratory
  • Optics, photonics, laser and spectroscopy laboratory
  • Electronics, sensors and embedded systems laboratory
  • Renewable-energy and energy-storage demonstration laboratory
  • Mechanics, heat, sound and materials laboratory
  • Astronomy, space science and remote sensing demonstration zone
  • Geophysics, mine-environment and earth-science demonstration laboratory
  • Computational physics, simulation, data analytics and AI-enabled modelling lab
  • Science museum / discovery centre / visitor gallery
  • Science-on-wheels / mobile physics lab for nearby schools
  • Teacher-training and laboratory-assistant training centre
  • Maker space, fabrication lab, robotics lab, 3D printing and instrumentation workshop
  • Innovation incubation room for student projects and local startups
  • Green open-air science park with physics exhibits
  • Mine-history and energy-transition interpretation centre

The Ministry of Coal mine-closure guidelines support productive, safe, and socially useful post-mining assets — providing a policy basis for education-campus repurposing, subject to site suitability and approvals.

A Physics Lab / Science and Innovation Campus can be a strong repurposing option where a closed mine site offers a combination of safe reclaimed land or stable existing buildings, proximity to schools, colleges, ITIs and polytechnics, existing utilities, workshops and security, and social need for practical science education and technical skilling.

This option differs from ordinary school infrastructure, tourism, or conventional welfare projects because it is education-intensive, technology-enabled, demonstration-led and institutionally managed. It is not merely a classroom asset — it is a long-term knowledge and skill-development asset that can support science education, local employment, equipment servicing, teacher training, public outreach, research collaboration and innovation ecosystems.

Key strategic benefits include:

  • Productive reuse of mine land and existing infrastructure
  • Conversion of a closed mine from an extraction site into a science and knowledge campus
  • Support for practical physics education and hands-on STEM learning
  • Improvement in science access for nearby schools, rural students and mining communities
  • Creation of local employment in laboratory operations, maintenance, training, security, equipment repair, fabrication, visitor management and science outreach
  • Retraining pathways for local youth and selected mine workers in electrical systems, instrumentation, measurement, safety, equipment maintenance and technical support
  • Support for teacher capacity building and practical demonstration-based learning
  • Local development through school visits, workshops, science fairs, competitions, internships and innovation challenges
  • Possibility of hosting district-level or regional science-resource facilities
  • Long-term lease, concession, training-fee, institutional-partnership, CSR, grant, incubation or service-revenue potential
  • Strong alignment with mine repurposing, just transition, STEM education, skill development, scientific temper and community development

The concept should be advanced only where the mine site can support safe public and student use. Building safety, mine-safety buffer, child safety, reliable utilities, trained staffing, community acceptance, institutional funding, and recurring maintenance matter more than mere availability of vacant mine land.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP owner readiness, post-award developer scope, and the indicative implementation pathway for Physics Lab / Science and Innovation Campus projects on mine sites.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

  1. Land, mine-closure and permissibility confirmation — confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, public-access restrictions, building-use permissions and whether the site can be offered for Physics Lab / science education development through owner-led, lease, concession, PPP or hybrid mode.
  2. Site and building safety screening — screen the proposed site for public-use suitability, including distance from open pits, mine voids, unstable highwalls, dumps, shafts, water bodies, subsidence zones and restricted mine infrastructure.
  3. Structural audit and refurbishment requirement — assess existing building condition, floor strength, roof condition, dampness, ventilation, lighting, cracks, electrical wiring, sanitation, accessibility and repair requirement.
  4. User-demand and catchment assessment — identify nearby schools, colleges, ITIs, polytechnics, villages, mine colonies and student population, and assess likely annual use, batch size and outreach requirement.
  5. Functional lab scope decision — decide whether the first phase will be a school physics lab, district physics lab, STEM lab, mine-science demonstration centre, teacher-training lab, mobile science-lab base or full science campus.
  6. Preliminary space planning — prepare room-wise layout covering physics lab, demonstration area, storage, preparation room, electronics corner, digital classroom, staff room, office, washrooms, outdoor science park and parking.
  7. Equipment and technology list — prepare a level-wise equipment list for mechanics, heat, optics, electricity, magnetism, electronics, measurement, sensors, renewable energy, digital learning and mine-science demonstrations.
  8. Power, ICT and utility assessment — confirm electrical load, wiring upgrade requirement, earthing, internet, UPS, projector / smart board, CCTV, emergency lighting, water and sanitation readiness.
  9. Safety and compliance screening — prepare initial fire-safety, electrical-safety, child-safety, laboratory-safety, first-aid, evacuation and accessibility notes.
  10. Environmental and indoor-condition screening — check for coal dust, oil contamination, asbestos risk, dampness, indoor air quality, noise, dust, nearby industrial hazards and any remediation requirement.
  11. Institutional partnership and operating model assessment — identify possible partners such as schools, education department, science centres, universities, ITIs, polytechnics, NGOs, CSR foundations and laboratory-management agencies.
  12. Implementation model and risk-allocation decision — select the preferred model such as Hybrid, EPC + O&M, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, BOOT or EPC based on project size, CAPEX, owner control, recurring funding and expected utilization.
  13. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — prepare bidder scope covering design, building refurbishment, equipment supply, installation, safety compliance, staffing, operations, maintenance, outreach, local employment, reporting, revenue sharing and handback obligations.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

  1. Detailed site and building assessment — undertake detailed building measurement, structural review, electrical audit, ventilation review, accessibility audit, fire-safety assessment, site-risk mapping and utility verification.
  2. Detailed laboratory planning — prepare final room layout, bench arrangement, demonstration zones, storage plan, equipment flow, visitor circulation, emergency exits and outdoor physics park layout.
  3. Detailed equipment specification — finalize experiment-wise equipment lists for mechanics, heat, optics, electricity, magnetism, electronics, digital sensors, renewable-energy demonstrations and mine-science exhibits.
  4. Statutory approvals and permissions — obtain building-use permission, fire-safety clearance, electrical safety approvals, local-body permissions, school / institutional MoUs and other applicable permissions.
  5. Final design and refurbishment — complete civil repairs, flooring, painting, damp-proofing, ceiling, lighting, ventilation, doors, windows, partitions, washrooms, accessibility features and outdoor development.
  6. Electrical, ICT and safety installation — install wiring, distribution boards, earthing, MCB/RCCB, UPS, internet, CCTV, smart classroom systems, emergency lights, extinguishers, signage and public-address systems.
  7. Furniture and storage installation — install lab benches, stools, demonstration table, instrument cupboards, storage racks, preparation counters, lockable cabinets and display boards.
  8. Equipment procurement and installation — procure, test and install physics instruments, electronics kits, optics benches, measuring instruments, renewable-energy trainers, sensors, data loggers, computers and demonstration models.
  9. Curriculum and operating manual preparation — prepare experiment manuals, safety manuals, teacher guides, student worksheets, visitor protocols, maintenance checklists and annual activity calendar.
  10. Staff recruitment and training — appoint lab manager, lab assistants, science communicators, technicians, safety supervisor, front-desk staff, security, housekeeping and outreach coordinators, with preference for trained local youth where feasible.
  11. School and community outreach integration — sign visit schedules with schools, colleges, ITIs and community organizations, and plan science fairs, teacher training, mobile-lab visits and local science clubs.
  12. Testing and trial operation — conduct equipment testing, safety drills, student pilot batches, teacher feedback sessions, emergency drills, demonstration dry runs and final readiness review.
  13. Commercial / public operation and compliance monitoring — begin regular operation after readiness certification, and continue safety inspections, equipment maintenance, attendance tracking, learning-outcome reporting, revenue tracking and annual performance review.
  14. Phased expansion or optimization — expand to advanced electronics, robotics, astronomy, renewable energy, mining-instrumentation, mobile science van, science park or regional science centre only after stable utilization and funding validation.

M
MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward, preferred models, key references, and abbreviations for Physics Lab / Science and Innovation Campus projects on closed mine sites.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid, (2) EPC + O&M / Facility Management, (3) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because a Physics Lab in a closed mine area is primarily a community-development and education-transition asset rather than a high-profit commercial asset. The owner can provide land, building, basic safety works and public-purpose control, while specialist education, science, CSR, university or technical partners can provide equipment, curriculum, operations, outreach and training. EPC + O&M / Facility Management is also highly suitable because the mine owner retains ownership and social control, while a professional operator ensures that the facility does not become an unused building after inauguration. This model is strong where the owner can fund the project but lacks in-house science-lab operating capacity. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is suitable where the site has strong user demand, visitor potential, training revenue or CSR-backed viability and where an experienced education / science partner is willing to invest and operate under defined public-service obligations.

Secondary option: DBFOT or BOOT may be used for larger district-level science campuses, mine-science parks or multi-lab STEM hubs where revenue potential, long concession tenure and professional operation can justify private investment.

Suggestable technical care: Building safety, mine-safety buffer, child safety, electrical safety, fire safety, indoor air quality, equipment durability, trained staffing, operating budget, institutional partnership, accessibility, local-language learning and recurring maintenance must be screened carefully.

Recommended development logic: Apply knockout screening first: safe building / land, mine-hazard separation, structural safety, user demand, power and internet availability, operating partner and recurring funding. Only passing sites should move to detailed space planning, equipment specification and tendering.
