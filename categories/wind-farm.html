Jaisalmer Wind Park, Rajasthan — 1,000+ MW
Wind Farm / Renewable Energy Park development on closed coal mine land transforms suitable overburden dumps, ridgelines, reclaimed plateaus, and haul-road corridors into productive clean-power infrastructure. India's cumulative wind-power capacity reached 56,094.84 MW as on 31 March 2026, with an estimated national potential of 695.50 GW at 120 m and 1,163.9 GW at 150 m above ground level — supporting wind as a compelling repurposing option where mine lands overlap with viable wind corridors.
Wind farm repurposing is most relevant for mines in high-wind-resource states across India. The CCO's A.R.T.H.A. framework identifies closed mines as potential green-energy hubs by leveraging existing land, industrial access, and grid connectivity. Unlike tourism or social-infrastructure reuse, this option is resource-dependent, infrastructure-led, and commercially sensitive — requiring confirmed wind speed, grid evacuation capacity, and geotechnical stability before procurement begins.

PROJECT VISION

Defines the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability criteria, and indicative scale options for wind-farm development on closed coal mine land.

A Wind Farm / Renewable Energy Park on closed coal mine land converts suitable overburden dumps, ridgelines, reclaimed plateaus, and haul-road corridors into clean-power infrastructure. The proposed project may include:

  • Wind turbine generator (WTG) blocks
  • Wind mast / LiDAR / SoDAR wind-resource measurement systems
  • Turbine foundations, crane pads, and erection platforms
  • Internal access roads and heavy-haul logistics corridors
  • Electrical pooling substation and 33 kV / 66 kV collection network
  • Grid evacuation line and grid interconnection bay
  • SCADA, forecasting, and remote monitoring systems
  • O&M building, spare-parts storage, and maintenance workshop
  • Solar-wind hybrid blocks and BESS, where commercially justified
  • Vegetation management, slope protection, and erosion-control systems
  • Community training, local vendor-support, and safety facilities

This is a selective repurposing option — not a universal one. A closed mine is suitable only where wind resource, land availability, geotechnical stability, grid evacuation, access logistics, environmental sensitivity, statutory permissibility, and community acceptance can all be satisfied.

  • Productive reuse of closed-mine land for long-term renewable-energy generation
  • Utilisation of existing roads, substations, utility corridors, and industrial buffers
  • Support for India's renewable-energy and net-zero transition (695.50 GW potential at 120 m)
  • Reduction of idle land and post-closure maintenance burden
  • Creation of construction, civil, electrical, logistics, security, and O&M-support jobs
  • Training and transition pathways for local youth in renewable-energy trades
  • Green-power supply to DISCOMs, industries, or corporate offtakers
  • Long-term lease rent, concession fee, revenue share, or green-power benefit for the mine owner
  • Visible alignment with mine-closure, reclamation, and green-transition objectives

Critical constraint: Land availability alone does not make a wind project bankable. Wind speed, turbulence, grid capacity, access logistics, geotechnical stability, and community acceptance are decisive — advance only where measured wind resource and grid evacuation are strong enough.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Describes what must be ready before the first RFP is issued, and the activities that the selected developer / concessionaire / RESCO may take up post-award.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a wind-farm-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and mine-closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage, reclamation status, leaseability, and permissibility of renewable-energy / wind-power development on the proposed mine land.
  2. Legal, commercial and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, concession / lease rights, power-sale rights, open-access possibility, revenue-sharing permissibility, decommissioning obligations, and insurance requirements.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify unsafe or restricted zones, including unstable overburden dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, old underground workings, mine voids, unsafe water bodies, hazardous structures, and active / restricted mine infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary wind-resource screening — Assess the site using NIWE wind atlas data, mesoscale wind data, nearby wind projects, local topography, wind direction, turbulence risk, and suitability for met mast / LiDAR / SoDAR measurement.
  5. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, access-road review, geotechnical screening, dump-stability screening, slope assessment, drainage review, and preliminary turbine-siting constraints.
  6. Grid-evacuation and connectivity screening — Identify nearest substation, voltage level, bay availability, transmission corridor, evacuation capacity, likely grid-connectivity route, and compliance implications under CEA grid-connectivity standards. CEA connectivity standards prescribe minimum technical requirements for renewable-energy plants to ensure safe, secure, and reliable grid operation.
  7. Environmental and social screening — Assess forest / non-forest status, wildlife sensitivity, bird and bat risks, noise, shadow flicker, visual impact, settlement distance, grazing routes, community access, and possible environmental-clearance pathway.
  8. Preliminary turbine layout and capacity estimate — Prepare an indicative turbine envelope, exclusion-zone map, estimated MW capacity, internal road concept, crane-pad locations, pooling-substation location, and broad phasing plan. MNRE's amended onshore wind guidelines place emphasis on optimized micro-siting using wind-flow modelling and layout optimization rather than only fixed turbine-spacing assumptions.
  9. Market, offtake and revenue assessment — Assess possible commercial structures such as captive use, group captive, third-party open access, DISCOM PPA, corporate PPA, RESCO, concession, or hybrid model, along with tariff, CUF, curtailment, and revenue-share assumptions.
  10. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX, OPEX, grid-cost, access-road, land-development, and decommissioning estimates, and shortlist the preferred model such as Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, RESCO, DBFOT, Hybrid, or EPC + O&M.
  11. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering wind-resource validation, detailed design, turbine procurement, grid connectivity, construction, O&M, SCADA, forecasting, safety, local employment, environmental safeguards, repowering, and decommissioning.
  12. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering wind-development experience, financial capacity, turbine / OEM arrangement, energy-yield methodology, grid strategy, safety record, O&M plan, local livelihood plan, revenue proposal, and compliance with MNRE / CEA requirements.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected developer / concessionaire / RESCO agency / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, overburden-dump stability assessment, subsidence review, foundation investigation, drainage mapping, and access-road engineering.
  2. Bankable wind-resource assessment — Install or validate met mast / LiDAR / SoDAR data, undertake long-term correlation, wind-flow modelling, turbulence assessment, wake-loss analysis, and bankable energy-yield estimation using NIWE / industry-accepted methods.
  3. Detailed environmental and social planning — Prepare E&S management plan, bird / bat study, noise and shadow-flicker assessment, community-safety plan, stakeholder-engagement plan, and site-specific mitigation measures. IFC's wind-energy EHS guidelines cover environmental, occupational health and safety, and community health and safety issues for wind projects.
  4. Final micro siting and master planning — Prepare the final turbine layout, internal road network, crane-pad plan, cable route, pooling-substation location, no-go zones, buffer areas, emergency access, and phased expansion plan.
  5. Detailed Project Report / project execution plan — The selected developer shall prepare the DPR / execution plan covering final surveys, wind-resource assessment, turbine selection, energy yield, civil and electrical design, grid plan, approvals plan, financial model, O&M plan, safety plan, and implementation schedule.
  6. Approvals, grid connectivity and agreements — Obtain project-specific approvals, land / lease documentation, grid connectivity, electrical inspectorate approvals, aviation / defence / telecom clearances if applicable, PPA / open-access agreements, insurance, and statutory registrations.
  7. Detailed engineering and procurement — Finalize turbine model, OEM contract, foundation design, road and crane-pad design, electrical collector system, pooling substation, SCADA, forecasting system, metering, protection, and grid-compliance equipment.
  8. Construction and installation — Develop access roads, turbine foundations, crane pads, cable trenches, pooling substation, evacuation line, control room, O&M facilities, drainage works, safety signage, and then complete turbine erection and electrical installation.
  9. Testing, synchronization and commissioning — Conduct pre-commissioning checks, protection testing, SCADA testing, grid-code compliance checks, trial generation, synchronization, performance testing, and commercial operation declaration as per applicable grid and contract requirements.
  10. Operation, maintenance and compliance monitoring — Carry out routine turbine O&M, BOP maintenance, SCADA monitoring, forecasting, generation reporting, safety inspections, environmental monitoring, community grievance redressal, and compliance reporting.
  11. Local livelihood integration — Implement local hiring, technician training, security deployment, road-maintenance contracts, vegetation management, transport support, local vendor participation, and community-benefit commitments as per RFP / concession terms.
  12. Performance review, hybridization and expansion — Review generation, CUF, curtailment, revenue, safety performance, and community impact before taking up phased expansion, solar hybridization, BESS integration, repowering, or additional renewable-energy uses.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Covers the recommended way forward and preferred models, the 18-step indicative implementation pathway, key references, and abbreviations.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order:

  • Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate
  • RESCO
  • DBFOT
  • Hybrid
  • EPC + O&M

Why preferred: Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is preferred because wind projects are resource-dependent and require specialist measurement, turbine procurement, grid planning, and O&M. The mine owner retains land control while transferring wind-resource and performance risk to the developer.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable only where the owner wants full ownership and has a strong captive or strategic power case.

Recommended development logic: Start with wind mast / LiDAR measurement and grid screening. Develop only where wind resource, evacuation, and offtake are strong; hybridise with solar and BESS where practical.
