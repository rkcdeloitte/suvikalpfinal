Final Recommendations / Landfill + Compost & Biogas
LANDFILL + COMPOST & BIOGAS
Precedents
Indore GOBARdhan Bio-CNG Plant — Asia's Largest, Madhya Pradesh
Landfill + Compost & Biogas development transforms suitable closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land into an integrated solid waste processing and resource-recovery facility. The concept combines scientific / engineered landfill for inert and process rejects, windrow / aerated composting for segregated organic waste, biomethanation / compressed biogas generation from wet biodegradable waste, leachate collection and treatment, landfill gas capture, compost / digestate curing, material recovery for recyclables, green buffer plantations, and local livelihood creation through facility operations and green jobs.
Unlike open dumping, an integrated facility reduces final landfill quantum, generates compost, Bio-CNG and soil conditioner, supports methane reduction, and enables controlled disposal of inert rejects in phased cells. India's Solid Waste Management Rules, 2016 require scientific processing and sanitary landfilling of residual waste, while the SWM Rules, 2026 further emphasize waste processing, monitoring, buffer zones, and restrictions on landfilling recyclable or energy-recoverable waste. Indore's GOBARdhan Bio-CNG plant processes ~550 TPD of segregated organic MSW, producing ~17,000 kg of Bio-CNG and ~100 tonnes of compost per day — demonstrating commercial scale.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability and technical specification matrix, and indicative scale models — from a pilot organic processing zone on 1–5 ha to a regional waste-to-resource and engineered landfill ecosystem at 100 ha+.

This concept proposes the development of Landfill + Compost & Biogas Zones on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert suitable under-utilized mine land into an integrated solid-waste processing and resource-recovery facility.

Components include: scientific / engineered landfill for inert and process rejects; windrow / aerated composting for segregated organic waste; biomethanation / compressed biogas generation from wet biodegradable waste; leachate collection and treatment; landfill gas capture where applicable; compost / digestate curing and quality control; material recovery for recyclables before landfilling; green buffers, odour-control systems, and environmental monitoring; and local livelihood creation through facility operations, compost handling, waste segregation, logistics, and green jobs.

Unlike open dumping, an integrated facility: reduces the quantity of waste finally landfilled; generates compost, biogas / Bio-CNG, and soil conditioner; supports methane reduction and climate objectives; enables controlled disposal of inert rejects; can be developed in phased cells; and is compatible with large, bufferable mine-closure land parcels.

  • Productive reuse of closed mine land for essential public infrastructure
  • Reduction of open dumping and legacy waste accumulation
  • Controlled scientific disposal of inert and process rejects
  • Compost production from segregated biodegradable waste
  • Biogas / Bio-CNG generation from wet organic waste through anaerobic digestion
  • Methane-emission reduction compared with unmanaged dumping
  • Revenue generation through tipping fee, compost sale, Bio-CNG sale, electricity generation, carbon / ESG value, and recyclables recovery
  • Scope for long-term local employment in segregation, composting, gas-plant operation, leachate management, laboratory testing, logistics, and site maintenance
  • Ability to integrate with municipal waste-management systems, mining townships, and nearby ULBs
  • Phased development using landfill cells, compost pads, and modular biogas digesters
  • Opportunity to create green buffer plantations and post-closure ecological improvement
  • Compliance-oriented replacement of unscientific dumping
  • Supports Swachh Bharat Mission, GOBARdhan, circular economy, and mine-closure repurposing objectives

Key Reference: Indore's GOBARdhan Bio-CNG plant processes ~550 TPD of segregated organic MSW, producing ~17,000 kg of Bio-CNG and ~100 tonnes of compost per day — demonstrating commercial viability at scale.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Describes the 13-item checklist for what must be ready before the first RFP — land confirmation, regulatory screening, mine-safety zoning, waste characterization, technology layout, market assessment — and the 15-step post-award implementation sequence.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Landfill + Compost & Biogas based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm ownership, mine-closure status, land-use permissibility, and compatibility for scientific solid-waste processing, composting, biomethanation, and sanitary landfill use.
  2. Legal and regulatory screening — Review SWM Rules 2016/2026, SPCB consent requirements, ULB authorization, tipping-fee rights, waste ownership, compost / Bio-CNG sale rights, and revenue-sharing permissibility.
  3. Mine safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, mine-fire zones, waterlogged pits, old voids, and restricted mine infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Topographic survey, geotechnical screening, hydrogeological note, groundwater depth, drainage review, flood-risk screening, and soil permeability assessment.
  5. Environmental and contamination screening — Soil, groundwater, surface water, air quality, dust, residual contamination, and ecological sensitivity assessment.
  6. Waste catchment and quantity assessment — Identify nearby ULBs, mine townships, markets, institutions, industrial canteens; estimate daily waste in TPD.
  7. Waste characterization and segregation readiness — Assess wet waste, dry recyclables, inert fraction, rejects, C&D waste, and seasonal variation; review whether source-segregated wet waste can be supplied for composting and biomethanation.
  8. Preliminary technology and zoning plan — Initial layout for MRF, composting pad, biomethanation / CBG plant, sanitary landfill cells, leachate treatment, green buffer, roads, weighbridge, laboratory, and no-go areas.
  9. Leachate, landfill gas, and odour-control concept — Basic concepts for leachate collection, stormwater separation, landfill gas venting / flaring, Bio-CNG gas safety, odour control, and daily-cover management.
  10. Market and revenue assessment — Assess tipping fee, compost sale, Bio-CNG / electricity, recyclables, RDF if applicable, carbon / ESG benefits, and landfill user charges.
  11. Cost and implementation model selection — Broad CAPEX, 5-year O&M estimate, tipping-fee assumptions, and preferred model (Hybrid, Concession / LDO, DBFOT, or EPC + O&M).
  12. Draft RFP scope and performance obligations — Bidder obligations: design, construction, operation, SWM compliance, landfill-cell management, compost quality, Bio-CNG performance, local employment, safety, environmental monitoring, and post-closure responsibility.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Criteria: solid-waste experience, landfill design capability, composting / biomethanation track record, SPCB compliance history, financial capacity, O&M plan, local livelihood integration, and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

  1. Detailed site investigation — Topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, hydrogeological study, groundwater monitoring baseline, flood-risk review, and landfill foundation assessment.
  2. Detailed waste and feedstock study — Seasonal waste characterization, wet-waste quality testing, inert / reject assessment, recyclables estimation, and ULB / bulk-waste generator supply commitments.
  3. Detailed environmental and regulatory planning — Environmental management plan, leachate management plan, landfill gas management plan, odour-control plan, stormwater plan, fire-safety plan, and statutory approval roadmap.
  4. Final master planning — Final facility master plan: waste receiving area, MRF, composting zone, biomethanation / CBG plant, sanitary landfill cells, leachate treatment, green buffer, internal roads, emergency access.
  5. DPR preparation — Final surveys, design basis, technology selection, landfill-cell design, composting and biomethanation design, financial model, O&M plan, approvals plan, and implementation schedule.
  6. Approvals and statutory permissions — SPCB Consent to Establish, ULB / local-body authorization, fire NOC, environmental clearance if triggered, PESO / gas-safety approvals, and other project-specific permissions.
  7. Detailed engineering and procurement — Landfill liner, leachate pipes, leachate treatment plant, compost pad, digester, gas-holder, Bio-CNG purification, weighbridge, laboratory, drains, roads, fencing, and utilities.
  8. Construction and installation — Landfill cells, MRF, composting pad, biomethanation unit, leachate treatment system, stormwater drains, access roads, green buffer, weighing system, CCTV, fire-fighting, and worker facilities.
  9. Operating procedures and compliance systems — SOPs for waste acceptance, segregation, composting, digester feeding, gas handling, landfill compaction, daily cover, leachate treatment, odour control, and environmental monitoring.
  10. Recruitment and training — Operators, safety staff, laboratory staff, landfill supervisors, compost workers, equipment operators, SHG members, waste workers, and local youth in PPE, safety, and operating procedures.
  11. Trial operation and performance testing — Trial runs for MRF, composting, biogas generation, leachate treatment, weighbridge, landfill operations, gas safety, odour control, and emergency response.
  12. Commissioning and commercial operation — Full-scale operations after statutory readiness, safety clearance, feedstock confirmation, trial-run stabilization, and approval from the mine owner / authority.
  13. Regular operation and monitoring — Routine O&M, compost quality testing, leachate and groundwater monitoring, landfill gas monitoring, Bio-CNG performance reporting, incident reporting, and SWM compliance.
  14. Local livelihood and market linkage — SHGs, waste workers, transporters, compost distributors, local farmers, nurseries, and service providers for sustained livelihood and product offtake.
  15. Phased expansion — Expand landfill cells, composting capacity, Bio-CNG modules, green buffer, and monitoring based on waste supply, regulatory compliance, revenue performance, and approved master plan.

This pathway is indicative and should be adapted to reflect the specific complexity, regulatory requirements, financing structure, and site conditions applicable to the project.

  1. Concept approval and objective setting
  2. Site suitability, hydrogeology, and mine-safety screening
  3. Waste catchment and waste-characterization study
  4. Environmental, buffer-zone, and landfill-siting assessment
  5. Community engagement and SHG / waste-worker onboarding
  6. Market and value-chain assessment for compost and Bio-CNG
  7. Selection of implementation model
  8. DPR and bid structuring
  9. RFP issuance and partner selection
  10. Landfill cell, compost pad, and biogas infrastructure development
  11. ULB / generator agreements and waste-supply formalization
  12. Trial operations of composting and biomethanation units
  13. Leachate, gas, odour, and environmental monitoring activation
  14. Full-scale processing, reporting, and market linkage
  15. Scaling, additional landfill cells, and diversification into Bio-CNG, compost branding, and green-buffer development

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order:

  1. Hybrid Model — Mine-owner land + ULB waste supply agreement + private waste-processing operator + SHG / waste-worker participation
  2. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate — Strong for community-linked waste infrastructure with livelihood obligations
  3. DBFOT — For large structured PPP projects needing private finance and professional operation

Why Hybrid is preferred: Waste projects require municipal linkage, environmental compliance, technology operation, and community safeguards. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner land, ULB waste supply agreement, private waste-processing operator, and SHG / waste-worker participation.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable for welfare-driven or public-service waste facilities where the owner / local body wants strong control.

Recommended development logic: Start with integrated waste processing, MRF, composting / biomethanation, and engineered reject landfill. Expand into Bio-CNG, compost branding, nursery, and solar only after feedstock quality is reliable.
