PROJECT VISION

Project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability assessment and indicative scales for gravity battery / GES development on closed coal mine infrastructure.

This concept proposes the development of a Gravity Battery / Gravity Energy Storage System (GES) as a mine-closure repurposing project. The objective is to convert suitable closed, abandoned, discontinued, or end-of-life mine infrastructure into a long-duration energy storage asset that can support grid stability, renewable energy integration, mine-area redevelopment, post-closure livelihood creation, and long-term clean-energy transition.

A gravity battery stores electricity by using surplus power to lift a heavy mass — such as modular weights, mine-compatible blocks, or granular material — to a higher elevation. When power is required, the mass is lowered in a controlled manner, driving a motor-generator system to produce electricity. For mine repurposing, the most relevant configurations are:

  • Mine-shaft gravity storage — suspended weights raised and lowered through existing mine shafts using hoisting infrastructure
  • Underground Gravity Energy Storage (UGES) — uses decommissioned mine voids as reservoirs for bulk material such as sand, dropped to release energy
  • Surface-based gravity storage structures — crane-and-block systems on reclaimed mine land where shaft conditions are unsuitable

Unlike electrochemical battery storage, gravity storage does not depend on lithium-ion cells for the core energy-storage medium. The storage value comes from gravitational potential energy, governed by the mass lifted, vertical height available, and system efficiency. Research on abandoned mine shafts has identified their potential for suspended-weight gravity storage, while recent studies on UGES propose using decommissioned mines as storage reservoirs for bulk material.

This note is intended as an early-stage concept and structuring framework — not as a final DPR, tender, feasibility report, or implementation document. It is meant to support internal decision-making, project screening, mine-closure planning, stakeholder discussion, feasibility preparation, approval mapping, and procurement strategy.

Gravity energy storage is emerging as a potential long-duration storage option for locations where vertical height, existing mine shafts, pits, underground voids, hoisting infrastructure, grid connection, or renewable-energy redevelopment potential are available. For mine owners, it may create a productive post-mining use for land and infrastructure that would otherwise require closure, guarding, monitoring, and long-term safety management.

A gravity battery project may be considered for one or more of the following strategic purposes:

  • Mine-closure repurposing and productive reuse of closed mine assets
  • Long-duration storage for solar, floating solar, wind, or hybrid renewable projects
  • Peak shifting from daytime renewable generation to evening demand
  • Grid-support and flexible dispatch where permitted
  • Resilience support for mine-area redevelopment, industrial parks, water systems, townships, or critical infrastructure
  • Local employment generation through construction, fabrication, facility management, operations, safety, and monitoring
  • Reduction of dependency on electrochemical battery replacement cycles where long asset life is preferred
  • Creation of a clean-energy redevelopment narrative for closed mine regions

Key strategic benefits include:

  • Productive use of existing mine shafts, pits, land parcels, workshops, roads, power lines, and substations
  • Potential for long-duration storage with low self-discharge, especially in bulk-material underground gravity systems
  • Decoupling of storage power from storage duration in some gravity configurations
  • Lower exposure to battery-chemistry risks such as thermal runaway, battery degradation, and end-of-life cell disposal
  • Potential for local fabrication of structural components, weights, civil works, access systems, and balance-of-plant
  • Support for just transition and local livelihood in post-mining regions
  • Compatibility with solar, FSPV, wind, and hybrid clean-energy redevelopment

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP owner readiness checklist, post-award activities, and step-by-step implementation pathway for gravity battery development at closed coal mines.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Gravity Battery / Gravity Energy Storage mine-repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Mine closure and land-use confirmation — Confirm mine closure status, ownership / lease rights, approved Mine Closure Plan linkage, and permissibility of using the closed mine area for green energy / energy storage repurposing. The 2025 coal and lignite mine-closure guidelines specifically recognize repurposing for solar, green energy, recreation, waterbody conservation and similar post-mining uses.
  2. Mine asset inventory and no-go zone mapping — Prepare an inventory of shafts, incline entries, pits, underground galleries, dumps, substations, roads, workshops and unsafe zones, including highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, flooded workings and restricted infrastructure.
  3. Preliminary shaft / pit suitability screening — Assess shaft depth, diameter, lining condition, collar stability, water ingress, obstruction, ventilation condition, old hoisting system and accessibility to decide whether the site is suitable for mine-shaft gravity storage, UGES, surface gravity storage or hybrid storage.
  4. Safety, DGMS and closure-risk screening — Carry out preliminary screening for mine-safety risks, abandoned workings, gas, inundation, subsidence, slope instability, emergency access and public safety; the project should not proceed to RFP without a clearly marked safe-development envelope.
  5. Grid connectivity and electrical interface note — Identify nearest substation, voltage level, spare bay availability, evacuation route, metering requirement, protection requirement and whether the project is for captive use, renewable integration, grid support or storage service.
  6. Energy-storage use-case confirmation — Define the primary use case as renewable shifting, peak support, grid balancing, mine-area redevelopment support, backup for critical loads, or hybrid solar / FSPV + gravity storage.
  7. Preliminary technical concept and sizing basis — Prepare an indicative MW / MWh range based on available height, moving mass, shaft condition, operating cycle and technology route; the National Framework for Energy Storage Systems recognizes storage as a key element for renewable integration and grid flexibility.
  8. Environmental and statutory trigger screening — Screen requirements relating to SPCB, forest, wildlife, wetland, waterbody, CRZ / IPZ if applicable, land-use change, mine-closure obligations, contamination, drainage and waste-material reuse.
  9. Electrical safety and approval pathway — Prepare an approval map covering CEIG / Electrical Inspector, CEA safety requirements, grid interconnection, protection, metering, earthing, control room, emergency shutdown and energization permissions. The CEA safety regulations govern safety requirements for electrical supply installations.
  10. Commercial model and funding preference — Select the preferred model from EPC, EPC + O&M / Facility Management, BOOT, BOO, DBFOT, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, Storage-as-a-Service or Hybrid Renewable + Gravity Storage.
  11. Local livelihood and just-transition plan — Identify local employment, reskilling, fabrication, security, facility management, monitoring, transport and maintenance opportunities; mine-closure policy now emphasizes community development, livelihood and just transformation.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid evaluation framework — Prepare bidder obligations covering feasibility validation, shaft certification, technology design, safety compliance, electrical approvals, performance guarantee, O&M plan, local employment, revenue sharing and decommissioning / handback obligations.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / storage developer can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site and shaft investigation — Undertake detailed shaft survey, drone mapping, underground records review, geotechnical investigation, hydrogeological study, subsidence assessment, slope assessment and structural condition assessment.
  2. Detailed feasibility and technology confirmation — Confirm whether the project should use suspended-weight shaft storage, underground gravity energy storage, surface gravity storage, hybrid solar + gravity storage, or another technically suitable configuration.
  3. Detailed Project Report / DPR preparation — Prepare the DPR covering final MW / MWh sizing, shaft-use design, mechanical system, electrical system, grid interface, safety design, revenue model, statutory approval plan, construction schedule and O&M plan.
  4. Mine safety, electrical and statutory approvals — Obtain required approvals from the mine owner / competent mining authority, DGMS where applicable, CEIG / Electrical Inspector, grid utility, SPCB, fire authority and other statutory bodies before construction or energization.
  5. Detailed engineering and design — Prepare final designs for hoist / lifting system, weights or storage medium, shaft guides, brakes, motor-generator, power electronics, transformer, switchgear, EMS / SCADA, foundations, control room and emergency systems.
  6. Grid interconnection and protection studies — Complete load-flow, short-circuit, relay coordination, metering, power-quality, earthing and synchronization studies in line with utility and CEA requirements.
  7. Procurement and fabrication — Procure or fabricate hoist equipment, ropes / cables, weights, motor-generators, drives, transformers, switchgear, control systems, sensors, communication systems, safety barriers and rescue equipment.
  8. Construction and installation — Execute civil works, shaft preparation, mechanical installation, electrical works, control-room setup, access improvement, fencing, lighting, drainage, signage and emergency-access development.
  9. EMS / SCADA and operational control setup — Install dispatch control, monitoring, emergency shutdown, overspeed protection, shaft safety interlocks, grid communication and remote monitoring systems.
  10. Recruitment, training and local livelihood integration — Recruit and train local workers for security, facility management, mechanical support, electrical support, monitoring, housekeeping, vegetation control, emergency response and routine O&M.
  11. Testing, inspection and safety audit — Conduct mechanical load testing, brake testing, electrical testing, protection testing, mock drills, emergency shutdown testing, third-party safety audit and statutory inspection.
  12. Trial operation and performance validation — Run trial charging / discharging cycles, validate MW output, MWh capacity, response time, round-trip efficiency, safety interlocks, dispatch logic and grid synchronization.
  13. Commissioning and commercial operation — Commission the gravity storage project after statutory clearance, grid approval, safety certification, performance testing and owner acceptance.
  14. Regular O&M and compliance monitoring — Carry out periodic shaft inspection, hoist maintenance, electrical testing, safety audits, environmental monitoring, incident reporting, local employment reporting and mine-closure compliance reporting.
  15. Phased expansion / hybrid integration — Expand capacity or integrate solar, FSPV, wind, pumped storage, industrial load support or grid-service participation based on performance, safety record, land availability and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward for gravity battery development at closed coal mines — preferred models, key due-diligence considerations, and recommended next steps.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

  • Availability and condition of mine shafts, pits, galleries, or other vertical-height assets
  • Mine closure status and legal permissibility of repurposing
  • Funding availability and owner's appetite for CAPEX
  • Intended use case and revenue logic
  • Grid connectivity and power evacuation conditions
  • Compatibility with solar, FSPV, wind, pumped storage, or other renewable projects
  • Technology maturity, vendor capability, and performance guarantee bankability
  • Local livelihood, reskilling, and just-transition expectations
  • Shaft safety, mine safety, environmental, and public-safety considerations

Given the current maturity of mine-shaft gravity storage, the recommended first step should be a pre-feasibility and shaft-screening study, followed by a pilot or phased development model. For most mine owners, the most balanced model is likely to be EPC + O&M / Facility Management where funding is available, or DBFOT / Lease-Develop-Operate where private financing and technology-risk transfer are preferred.

Where a floating solar or ground-mounted solar project is also under consideration, a combined Solar / FSPV + Gravity Battery pathway may be examined — storage can shift daytime solar generation to evening or peak periods, reduce intermittency, improve dispatchability, and strengthen the commercial viability of the overall clean-energy redevelopment strategy for the mine site.
