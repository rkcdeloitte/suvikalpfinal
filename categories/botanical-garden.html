Final Recommendations / Botanical Gardens & Native Plant Conservation Landscapes
Botanical Gardens
Global Precedents
Eden Project Biomes, Cornwall, UK
Botanical gardens are increasingly being developed in India for conservation of biodiversity, environmental education, eco-tourism, and recreational purposes. These gardens support preservation of native plant species, improve green cover, and provide educational and research opportunities while attracting visitors and promoting sustainable tourism.
Closed coal mines can be transformed into botanical gardens through the development of reclaimed land, stabilized overburden dumps, and restored mine areas with native, medicinal, ornamental, and rare plant species. Such repurposing supports ecological restoration, biodiversity conservation, carbon sequestration, environmental education, and sustainable post-mining land use while providing recreational facilities and employment opportunities for nearby communities.

P
PROJECT VISION

Botanical Gardens are particularly suited for closed coal mine areas — they transform disturbed land into a long-term ecological, educational and community asset. The Eden Project precedent demonstrates how even the most degraded extractive landscape can become a world-class plant-based destination. This pillar covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability and indicative project scales.

This concept proposes the development of Botanical Gardens / Native Plant Conservation Landscapes on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land into a biodiversity-supporting, education-oriented, eco-tourism-enabled, low-impact public landscape centred around native and climate-resilient plant collections; medicinal, aromatic, rare, threatened and endemic species conservation; ecological restoration of reclaimed mine land; community-based nursery and maintenance livelihoods; environmental education, interpretation and eco-tourism; and long-term carbon, biodiversity and ESG value creation.

Unlike conventional built infrastructure, botanical gardens can be developed progressively on reclaimed or partially restored land, support ecological restoration and soil stabilization, create long-term public and educational value, and are compatible with eco-parks, water bodies, agroforestry, nursery zones and biodiversity parks. This aligns with CCO mine-closure frameworks A.R.T.H.A., L.I.V.E.S. and R.E.C.L.A.I.M.

  • Converts degraded mine land into a productive green public asset with long-term ecological and social value
  • Supports ecological restoration through native plantations, habitat creation and soil stabilization
  • Enables conservation of rare, threatened, endemic, medicinal and economically useful plants
  • Creates local jobs in nursery operations, landscaping, maintenance, guides, interpretation, security and eco-tourism services
  • Supports women SHGs, youth groups and FPOs through nurseries, composting, seed collection, plant sales and visitor services
  • Provides environmental education for schools, colleges, mine workers and surrounding communities
  • Enhances ESG, biodiversity and carbon outcomes — measurable mine-closure compliance co-benefits
  • Diversified revenue streams: entry tickets, guided tours, nursery sales, medicinal plant sales, training, eco-tourism, CSR/grant support
  • Supports climate adaptation through heat-island reduction, shade creation and local biodiversity enhancement
  • Global precedent: Eden Project (Cornwall, UK) developed in an exhausted clay pit — now a world-class destination visited by over 6 million people

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

This pillar covers pre-RFP readiness requirements and activities that can be taken up post-award, enabling a well-structured botanical garden mine-repurposing tender process.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage, reclaimed-land condition and permissibility of botanical garden / eco-park / biodiversity park / public green-space use under the approved Mine Closure Plan.
  2. Post-mining land-use and statutory screening — Review land-use restrictions, revenue / lease permissions, visitor access rights, nursery sale rights, tree-felling restrictions, commercial activity permissions and long-term O&M obligations.
  3. Mine safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and restricted areas, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone zones, mine voids, unsafe water bodies, haul roads, electrical infrastructure and other restricted mine facilities.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, slope-stability screening, dump-stability note, drainage review, erosion-risk assessment and access-road condition note.
  5. Soil, water and contamination screening — Assess soil depth, pH, fertility, compaction, salinity, heavy metals, mine-water influence and residual contamination to decide whether areas are fit for public planting, nursery activity, medicinal plants or only ecological buffer plantation.
  6. Existing vegetation and biodiversity baseline — Prepare a baseline of existing plantations, native species, invasive species, wetlands, grasslands, pollinator areas and bird / butterfly habitat.
  7. Botanical theme and species-suitability assessment — Identify feasible themes such as native arboretum, medicinal plant garden, bamboo garden, butterfly garden, wetland garden, grass garden, orchid / fern house, herbal garden, cactus / xerophyte zone or climate-resilient species zone.
  8. Water and irrigation feasibility — Assess mine-water reuse, treated water availability, ponds, rainwater harvesting, drip irrigation, sprinkler irrigation and water-body integration for garden establishment and nursery operations.
  9. Community and institutional consultation — Consult panchayats, nearby villages, SHGs, FPOs, local youth, schools, forest department, horticulture department, agriculture department, KVKs, universities, BSI / botanical experts and State Biodiversity Board where relevant.
  10. Preliminary zoning and visitor-use plan — Prepare a basic layout showing entry plaza, botanical zones, nursery, interpretation centre, walking trails, water bodies, composting area, visitor amenities, parking, buffer areas, emergency access and no-go zones.
  11. Policy and scheme convergence check — Check convergence with mine-closure funds, CSR, Nagar Van / Nagar Vatika, CAMPA-linked restoration where eligible, biodiversity conservation schemes, horticulture schemes and rural livelihood programmes.
  12. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, DBFOT or EPC + O&M.
  13. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering botanical master planning, plantation, nursery development, irrigation, interpretation, visitor safety, community nursery participation, local employment, O&M standards and biodiversity monitoring.
  14. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering botanical / landscape experience, mine-land restoration experience, technical capability, O&M plan, community integration, financial capacity, visitor-management approach and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, dump / slope assessment, soil testing, water testing and drainage mapping.
  2. Detailed environmental and biodiversity planning — Prepare Environmental Management Plan, biodiversity management plan, invasive-species management plan, soil-improvement plan, water-management plan and plantation survival strategy.
  3. Final botanical garden master plan — Prepare the final layout showing botanical zones, conservation areas, nursery, interpretation centre, visitor circulation, utility areas, emergency routes, parking, buffer zones and restricted areas.
  4. Plant collection and species plan — Prepare the final species list with scientific names, native / exotic status, climate suitability, water demand, soil requirement, medicinal / ecological value and maintenance requirement.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — The selected operator shall prepare the DPR covering surveys, final master plan, species plan, civil works, nursery plan, irrigation design, cost estimate, financial model, O&M plan, approval plan and implementation schedule for mine-owner approval.
  6. Approvals and permissions — Obtain required approvals from the mine owner and relevant authorities. If forest land, protected area, tree cutting, water abstraction, public access, commercial nursery sale or construction is involved, permissions should be taken as applicable under Indian law and state rules.
  7. Nursery and soil-preparation works — Establish nursery, composting area, potting yard, soil-amendment system, shade-net area, mist chamber / propagation unit where required, and plant-hardening area.
  8. Infrastructure and utility development — Develop fencing, internal paths, irrigation network, drainage, lighting, visitor centre, interpretation boards, toilets, drinking water, parking, benches, watch-and-ward points and emergency access.
  9. Plantation and thematic garden development — Develop native arboretum, medicinal plant garden, butterfly garden, wetland garden, bamboo / grass garden, avenue plantation, shade zones and ecological buffer plantation in phases.
  10. Plant documentation and interpretation — Prepare plant accession records, plant labels, QR-code tagging, scientific signage, educational panels, visitor map, biodiversity register support and digital plant database.
  11. Community livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, FPOs, local youth, gardeners, nursery workers, guides, compost producers, plant sellers, kiosk operators and maintenance workers.
  12. Training and capacity building — Train local workers in nursery operations, propagation, pruning, watering, pest management, visitor guiding, interpretation, composting, safety and basic first aid.
  13. Trial operation and safety review — Conduct trial visitor movement, irrigation testing, plant survival review, pathway safety check, signage review, emergency-access check and owner inspection before opening.
  14. Branding and public outreach — Develop garden branding, school / college outreach, eco-tourism linkage, local tourism-circuit linkage, online information, seasonal flower / plant festivals and awareness programmes.
  15. Commissioning and public opening — Open the botanical garden after completion of safety checks, plantation establishment, visitor facilities, staff deployment and O&M systems.
  16. Regular O&M and monitoring — Carry out routine watering, pruning, plant replacement, pest control, composting, nursery production, visitor management, safety checks, biodiversity monitoring and compliance reporting.
  17. Phased expansion — Expand botanical zones, nursery sales, interpretation facilities, medicinal plant collections, water-body landscaping, eco-trails and research / education partnerships based on survival rate, visitor demand, revenue performance and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

This pillar provides the recommended way forward for botanical garden mine repurposing, the indicative implementation pathway, key references for further reading, and a glossary of abbreviations used throughout the PRISM framework.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because botanical gardens need long-term horticultural care, public access, ecological restoration and visitor services. The hybrid mix should include owner-funded core landscape + botanical / horticulture institution + private visitor-service operator + local nursery / SHG participation.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable for welfare-driven gardens where ecological outcomes and public access must remain under owner control.

Suggestable technical care: Soil improvement, species selection, irrigation, nursery, signage, visitor safety, waste management, biodiversity documentation and long-term maintenance must be planned.

Recommended development logic: Begin with native species zones, nursery, walking trails, interpretation boards and visitor amenities. Later add research plots, educational programmes, café, plant sales and eco-tourism linkage.

Final recommendation: Hybrid Model is most suitable because a botanical garden is an ecological, educational and livelihood asset, not only a visitor facility.
