Mushroom Farm
Global Precedents
Underground Mushroom Farm, Namur, Belgium

Mushroom farming offers a sustainable mine repurposing opportunity by utilizing abandoned mine buildings, underground spaces, and reclaimed land for commercial cultivation. The naturally cool and humid conditions in mine areas support year-round production with minimal environmental impact. This approach promotes livelihood generation, women-led self-help groups, agri-based entrepreneurship, and local employment while ensuring productive reuse of post-mining infrastructure.

Successful implementation requires proper hygiene management, adequate ventilation and water supply, technical skill development, strong market linkages, and sustainable long-term operations.

PROJECT VISION

Post-mining site context, suitability criteria, scale matrix, and owner-side readiness before committing to a Mushroom Farming project.

This concept proposes the development of Mushroom Farming Zones on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land, abandoned built spaces, reclaimed platforms, workshops, storage sheds, or low-productivity land parcels into controlled-environment mushroom production, training, processing, and livelihood clusters.

Unlike conventional agriculture, mushroom farming:

  • Requires very limited arable land
  • Can be conducted indoors using sheds, low-cost growing rooms, tunnels, or controlled-environment chambers
  • Uses agricultural residues such as paddy straw, wheat straw, maize stalks, sawdust, and other lignocellulosic biomass
  • Generates a nutrient-rich by-product in the form of spent mushroom substrate, which can be converted into manure or vermicompost
  • Is suitable for women SHGs, land-losers, youth, small entrepreneurs, and local producer groups

Mushroom cultivation is particularly relevant for mine-closure repurposing because it can use compact sites, repurposed buildings, low-disturbance land parcels, and cluster-based production systems. It is an indoor crop, does not require arable land, has a short crop cycle, and provides high yield per unit time. This aligns with India's mine-closure approach, where frameworks such as L.I.V.E.S, R.E.C.L.A.I.M, and A.R.T.H.A promote ecological restoration, community development, green finance, and productive repurposing of closed mine areas.

Mushroom farming is highly suited for closed coal mine areas because it is land-efficient, livelihood-intensive, low-footprint, modular, and scalable. It can be developed in reclaimed land parcels, unused buildings, closed mine infrastructure, training centres, community sheds, or purpose-built low-cost structures.

Key Benefits:

  • No dependence on high soil fertility — suitable for reclaimed, marginal, or non-arable mine land
  • Indoor and vertical-space-based production — high output from small land areas
  • Short crop cycle — faster income generation compared to orchards or forestry
  • High suitability for women SHGs, land-losers, youth, and small rural entrepreneurs
  • Uses agricultural residues and biomass — supports circular economy and waste-to-value conversion
  • Produces spent mushroom substrate — can be used for composting, vermicomposting, nursery media, and mine-land soil improvement
  • Supports diversified revenue streams: fresh mushrooms, dried mushrooms, mushroom powder, pickles and value-added foods, spawn production, compost / substrate production, training and incubation services, spent mushroom substrate manure
  • Compatible with eco-restoration, agroforestry, nursery development, composting, and rural enterprise zones
  • Can be scaled from household units to commercial controlled-environment farms
  • Strong fit with ESG, circular economy, livelihood restoration, and just-transition goals

Mushroom cultivation can convert agricultural, industrial, forestry, and household wastes into nutritious food. India has abundant agricultural residues and labour, which are key enabling factors for mushroom farming.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP readiness, post-award scope, and the 15-step implementation pathway for mine-area Mushroom Farming.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a mushroom-farming-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of mushroom farming, food-processing, training, composting and rural-enterprise use within the post-mining land-use plan.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease / concession rights, produce ownership, commercial rights, revenue-sharing permissibility, food-business responsibility and liability allocation.
  3. Safe and usable zone mapping — Identify usable buildings, sheds, platforms and safe land parcels, while excluding unstable dumps, subsidence-prone areas, abandoned mine openings, contaminated zones and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Built-asset and utility assessment — Prepare an inventory of available sheds, stores, workshops, warehouses, staff buildings, power supply, water source, drainage and access roads that can be repurposed for growing rooms, substrate preparation, packing and training.
  5. Environmental and contamination screening — Assess dust, drainage, residual contamination, wastewater risk, waste-dump proximity and food-safety suitability before allowing any edible mushroom production.
  6. Species and technology suitability note — Identify suitable mushroom types such as oyster, milky, button or paddy-straw mushroom based on local temperature, humidity, water availability, power reliability and level of environmental control required.
  7. Raw-material and substrate assessment — Map availability of paddy straw, wheat straw, sawdust, maize stalk, bagasse, cotton waste, poultry manure, gypsum, packaging material and nearby substrate suppliers.
  8. Food safety and enterprise compliance check — Identify applicable requirements such as FSSAI Registration / Licence for food handling, processing and sale, Udyam Registration for MSME status, GST where applicable, and local trade licence requirements.
  9. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult panchayats, nearby communities, land-losers, SHGs, FPOs, youth groups, women groups, KVKs, horticulture / agriculture departments, district industries centre and rural livelihood missions.
  10. Market and value-chain assessment — Estimate demand and revenue potential from fresh mushrooms, dried mushrooms, mushroom powder, pickles, spawn, compost / spent substrate, institutional supply, retail sale and training services.
  11. Preliminary zoning and process-flow plan — Prepare a basic layout showing growing rooms, substrate preparation area, pasteurization / sterilization area, spawn room, packing room, cold room, training area, composting zone, waste area and vehicle movement.
  12. Cost, funding and model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 3–5 year O&M estimates and select the preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, EPC + O&M, DBFOT or BOOT.
  13. Scheme and support convergence note — Check convergence with official support routes such as NHB / MIDH horticulture support, PMFME for micro food processing, PMEGP for rural enterprises, DAY-NRLM for SHG livelihoods and MSME support.
  14. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, growing-room operation, crop-cycle management, spawn / substrate quality, food safety, local employment, SHG/FPO participation, training, market linkage and waste-to-compost use.
  15. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering mushroom-farming experience, technical capability, food-safety compliance, financial capacity, O&M plan, community integration, market linkage, revenue proposal and risk-management plan.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / mushroom-farming operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site and asset investigation — Undertake detailed measurement of buildings, sheds, platforms, water points, drainage, electrical load, access roads and safe working areas for final project planning.
  2. Detailed technical feasibility — Finalize species mix, production capacity, crop calendar, growing-room design, temperature / humidity control, substrate technology, spawn source and expected yield assumptions.
  3. Detailed environmental and hygiene planning — Prepare the hygiene plan, biosecurity plan, pest and disease-control plan, wastewater plan, spent-substrate management plan and worker-safety plan.
  4. Final master planning — Prepare the final mushroom-zone master plan showing growing rooms, substrate area, pasteurization / sterilization unit, spawn room, packing area, cold storage, training room, composting area, utilities and community enterprise spaces.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — The selected operator shall prepare the DPR with final capacity, layout, engineering, machinery list, crop-cycle plan, financial model, O&M plan, food-safety plan, approval plan and implementation schedule for mine-owner approval.
  6. Approvals, registrations and enterprise formalities — Obtain applicable FSSAI Registration / Licence, Udyam Registration, GST registration where required, trade licence, electricity connection approval, fire and building safety clearances where applicable.
  7. Procurement of equipment and inputs — Procure racks, trays or bags, humidifiers, blowers, foggers, thermometers, hygrometers, pasteurization / sterilization equipment, packing tables, dryers, cold storage, PPE, cleaning equipment, spawn and substrate material.
  8. Construction and installation — Develop or retrofit growing rooms, insulation, ventilation, water lines, drainage, flooring, substrate area, packing room, cold room, training area, composting area and basic worker facilities.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for substrate preparation, pasteurization / sterilization, spawning, incubation, cropping, harvesting, grading, packing, cleaning, disease control, cold storage and waste handling.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train local youth, SHGs, FPO members, supervisors and workers in mushroom cultivation, hygiene, harvesting, packing, record-keeping, food safety and enterprise management.
  11. Trial crop cycles and quality stabilization — Run initial trial batches to stabilize temperature, humidity, contamination control, spawn quality, yield, harvesting schedule, packing quality and market dispatch systems.
  12. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, FPOs, local entrepreneurs and women groups for production, packing, drying, pickle / powder making, composting, retail sale and local distribution.
  13. Processing, branding and market linkage — Develop drying, powdering, packing, labelling, branding, local retail, institutional sale, hotel / canteen supply and online or aggregator-based marketing channels.
  14. Commissioning and commercial operation — Commission the mushroom farming zone after successful trial production, food-safety readiness, trained manpower deployment, market linkage and mine-owner approval.
  15. Regular O&M and monitoring — Monitor production, contamination rate, yield, sales, income generation, local employment, SHG/FPO participation, hygiene compliance, equipment maintenance and spent-substrate utilization.
  16. Phased expansion — Scale up growing rooms, spawn production, cold storage, processing, composting and training activities based on market demand, profitability, community participation and safety performance.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward, preferred models, recommended strategy, references, and abbreviations for mine-area Mushroom Farming.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) EPC + O&M / Facility Management, (4) DBFOT, (5) BOOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because mushroom farming needs small built-up space, technical training, crop-cycle management, SHG participation and market linkage. The hybrid mix should include owner-provided space + SHG / FPO production + technical agency + buyer / processor linkage.

Secondary option: EPC may be used for pilot-scale demonstration infrastructure; BOO should be used only where it is purely commercial and community obligations are secured.

Suggestable technical care: Temperature, humidity, ventilation, substrate supply, hygiene, pest control, drying, packing, cold storage and market access must be ensured.

Recommended development logic: Begin with a 500–2,500 sq ft pilot or 0.05–0.25 ha cluster. Scale after 2–3 successful crop cycles into substrate preparation, drying, packing and cold-chain facilities.
