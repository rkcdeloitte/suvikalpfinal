PROJECT VISION

Project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix and indicative scale options for rewilding and wildlife sanctuary-type development on closed coal mine land.

This concept proposes the development of Rewilding Landscapes / Wildlife Sanctuary-Type Conservation Areas / Biodiversity Recovery Reserves on closed, reclaimed or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy.

The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land, overburden dumps, reclaimed benches, abandoned haul roads, subsidence areas, mine-water bodies and buffer zones into a self-sustaining ecological recovery landscape centered around:

  • Native habitat restoration and assisted natural regeneration
  • Creation of grassland, scrubland, woodland, wetland and riparian habitats
  • Wildlife refuge and movement space for birds, reptiles, amphibians, small mammals and regionally suitable larger fauna
  • Ecological restoration of reclaimed mine land
  • Long-term biodiversity conservation and landscape connectivity
  • Community-based habitat restoration, protection, interpretation and eco-tourism livelihoods
  • Scientific monitoring using GIS, drones, camera traps, acoustic monitoring and biodiversity databases
  • Environmental education, controlled nature tourism and conservation interpretation
  • Long-term carbon, biodiversity and ESG value creation

For legal clarity, a Wildlife Sanctuary in India is a statutory protected area declared by the State Government under the Wild Life Protection Act, 1972. A mine owner can develop a wildlife sanctuary-type rewilding landscape, biodiversity reserve or conservation landscape first, while formal sanctuary / conservation reserve / community reserve notification must be pursued through the competent government authority where legally feasible.

Unlike conventional built infrastructure, rewilding landscapes can be developed progressively over reclaimed and partially restored land, require low built-up intensity and more ecological management, allow nature-led regeneration where land is safe and stable, and support long-term soil stabilization, habitat formation and ecological succession.

Rewilding and wildlife sanctuary-type landscapes are particularly suited for closed coal mine areas because mine lands often contain large contiguous land parcels, water bodies, overburden dumps, low-productivity reclaimed areas, safety buffers, plantations, grasslands and restricted-access zones that can be reorganized into a controlled conservation landscape.

A strong Indian precedent is Asola Bhatti Wildlife Sanctuary in Delhi — covering about 32 square kilometres — which includes former mining areas and abandoned mine pits that have developed into lakes and habitat zones supporting nilgai, leopard, blackbuck, porcupine, civet, jackal, jungle cat and over 193 bird species. Internationally, the Knepp Estate in the United Kingdom (more than 1,400 hectares / 3,500 acres) uses free-roaming herbivores to create dynamic vegetation mosaics, with nature tourism as part of its business model.

Key benefits include:

  • Converts degraded or under-utilized mine land into a long-term ecological conservation asset
  • Supports habitat recovery through native grassland, scrubland, woodland, wetland and riparian restoration
  • Creates refuge for birds, butterflies, reptiles, amphibians, small mammals and regionally appropriate wildlife
  • Enables landscape connectivity between forest patches, water bodies, plantations, agricultural lands and community commons
  • Converts mine-water bodies and abandoned pits into controlled wetland, bird and amphibian habitats where safe
  • Creates long-term local jobs in habitat restoration, patrolling, nursery operations, seed collection, ecological monitoring, eco-guiding, interpretation, visitor management and conservation education
  • Supports women SHGs, youth groups and FPOs through native nurseries, composting, seed collection, grassland management, visitor services and conservation-linked enterprises
  • Provides diversified revenue: guided nature trails, birdwatching, controlled safari / electric-cart trails, nature camps, native nursery plant sales, training programmes, interpretation centre income, CSR and grant support
  • Enhances ESG, biodiversity, carbon and mine-closure compliance outcomes
  • Supports climate adaptation through water retention, shade, vegetation cover, carbon storage and habitat resilience

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Comparative assessment of implementation models, model-wise responsibility logic, and combined revenue-profitability-livelihood analysis for rewilding and wildlife sanctuary projects.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a rewilding / wildlife sanctuary-type mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of ecological restoration, rewilding, conservation, eco-education and controlled nature-tourism use.
  2. Legal and statutory screening — Review forest status, land-use restrictions, WLPA applicability, Conservation Reserve / Community Reserve / Biodiversity Heritage Site / Wildlife Sanctuary feasibility and required government approvals.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence areas, unsafe water bodies, contaminated zones, fire-prone areas and restricted mine infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary site and environmental assessment — Prepare topographic, geotechnical, drainage, soil, mine-water and contamination screening to confirm suitability for habitat restoration and limited public access.
  5. Biodiversity and habitat baseline — Document existing vegetation, natural regeneration, invasive species, birds, reptiles, amphibians, butterflies, mammals, wetlands, grasslands, scrubland and wildlife movement signs.
  6. Preliminary zoning and carrying capacity — Prepare broad zoning for core habitat, restoration zone, wetland zone, buffer zone, visitor interpretation zone, community stewardship zone and no-go areas.
  7. Community and institutional consultation — Consult panchayats, SHGs, FPOs, local communities, Forest Department, Wildlife Wing, State Biodiversity Board, BMCs, NGOs, schools and research institutions.
  8. Livelihood and eco-education potential — Identify roles for local people in native nurseries, seed collection, habitat maintenance, patrolling, eco-guiding, interpretation, visitor services and conservation education.
  9. Market, cost and model assessment — Estimate demand, CAPEX, 3–5 year O&M cost, revenue potential and preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, DBFOT or EPC + O&M.
  10. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — Prepare bidder scope covering restoration works, monitoring, visitor safety, statutory compliance, local livelihood obligations, O&M standards, reporting indicators and revenue-sharing terms.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / conservation partner may take up the following in chronological order:

  1. Detailed surveys and baseline validation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, soil testing, water testing, vegetation survey and seasonal biodiversity assessment.
  2. Statutory and institutional action plan — Prepare approval roadmap covering mine-closure conditions, forest / wildlife permissions, biodiversity requirements, visitor-use permissions and coordination with competent authorities.
  3. Final master plan and DPR — Prepare the rewilding master plan and DPR with conservation zoning, habitat design, native species palette, restoration method, CAPEX, O&M plan, monitoring plan and implementation schedule.
  4. Safety and visitor-management planning — Prepare access-control, emergency-response, fire-response, water-body safety, restricted-zone, wildlife encounter, signage and public-liability protocols.
  5. Nursery, soil and water preparation — Establish native nursery, seed bank, composting unit, soil-improvement measures, water points, wetland edges, rainwater harvesting and mine-water reuse systems.
  6. Habitat restoration works — Implement phased grassland, scrubland, woodland, wetland and riparian restoration through native planting, assisted natural regeneration, invasive control and micro-habitat creation.
  7. Monitoring and protection systems — Deploy GIS database, camera traps, acoustic sensors, monitoring plots, GPS patrolling, biodiversity register and periodic ecological reporting system.
  8. Community livelihood onboarding — Train and engage local youth, SHGs and FPOs as nursery workers, habitat stewards, eco-guides, patrolling support, visitor-service providers and conservation educators.
  9. Low-impact visitor facilities — Develop controlled nature trails, bird hides, watch points, interpretation centre, signage, toilets, drinking water points and regulated eco-education facilities.
  10. Trial operation and audit — Conduct trial walks, mock drills, safety audit, ecological audit, visitor-flow testing and compliance review before public opening.
  11. Commissioning and regulated access — Open the site in phases with guide-led access, visitor limits, no-go zone enforcement, ticketing, safety protocols and biodiversity-sensitive visitor rules.
  12. O&M, adaptive management and statutory upgradation — Carry out habitat maintenance, invasive control, fire management, biodiversity monitoring, visitor regulation and, where feasible, pursue formal recognition as Conservation Reserve, Community Reserve, Biodiversity Heritage Site or Wildlife Sanctuary through the competent authority.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Pre-RFP owner readiness checklist, post-award activities, 26-step implementation pathway, and preferred models for rewilding and wildlife sanctuary development on closed coal mine land.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

  • Hybrid Model — preferred because rewilding requires ecological stewardship, habitat restoration, monitoring, legal compliance and community participation. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner land control + forest / wildlife expertise + NGO / research support + regulated eco-tourism operator.
  • Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate — strong for long-term community-linked rewilding landscapes with SHG/FPO participation, conservation obligations, controlled visitor revenue and revenue sharing.
  • DBFOT — suitable for large structured rewilding parks needing private finance, professional visitor management, monitoring infrastructure and interpretation facilities.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable for owner-controlled rewilding landscapes where public access, habitat quality and monitoring must remain tightly controlled.

Technical priorities: Habitat suitability, native vegetation, water sources, fire control, invasive species, human-wildlife conflict, legal status, fencing, visitor regulation and biodiversity monitoring must be assessed before procurement.

Recommended development logic: Start with habitat restoration, native planting, water-body improvement and controlled access. Introduce eco-tourism only after biodiversity recovery, safety and visitor regulation systems are in place.
