Final Recommendations / Floating Solar PV (FSPV)
Floating Solar
Precedents
NTPC Ramagundam Floating Solar, Telangana

In India, water-filled mine voids and abandoned pits can be repurposed for floating solar power projects, enabling clean energy generation through efficient utilization of unused water surfaces. India has rapidly scaled the technology — NTPC's 100 MW Ramagundam plant and the 278 MW Omkareshwar project on the Narmada reservoir rank among the country's largest — demonstrating that floating solar systems reduce land dependency, limit water evaporation, and support sustainable post-mining land use.

The initiative can create local employment opportunities, strengthen renewable energy infrastructure, and enhance the productive value of reclaimed mining areas. Effective implementation depends on proper water body assessment, secure anchoring and electrical systems, environmental safeguards, regular maintenance, and sustainable long-term operation.

P
PROJECT VISION

Closed mine pit water bodies represent a significant under-utilized post-mining asset class. FSPV converts these water surfaces into productive renewable energy infrastructure while supporting mine closure objectives and environmental rehabilitation.

The concept proposes repurposing closed mine pit water bodies for Floating Solar Photovoltaic (FSPV) systems. These water bodies — formed in post-mining voids — represent under-utilized assets that can be converted into renewable energy infrastructure.

Key objectives include: productive reuse of closed mine pit water, clean energy generation from post-mining assets, reduced pressure on land availability, support for ESG and mine closure visibility, and potential for local employment and community benefit.

Before any FSPV project proceeds, the following legal and regulatory dimensions must be confirmed:

  • Water surface rights — legal right to use the mine pit water surface for FSPV deployment
  • Mine / land approvals — post-mining land-use position; IBM / DGMS / CCO alignment
  • Power approvals — grid connectivity, CEIG electrical approvals, state DISCOM / transmission authority
  • Environmental triggers — screening for forest, wildlife, wetland, CRZ/IPZ, SPCB requirements

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

A structured 8-step implementation pathway guides FSPV projects from concept approval through construction and operation. Pre-RFP readiness must be substantially complete before the first tender is issued.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing the first RFP for an FSPV mine-repurposing project, the Mine Owner should complete the following:

  1. Closure and asset confirmation — Confirm mine closure status and identify the project asset base, including pit water body, shoreline, and access area.
  2. Land / water rights review — Establish legal control over the water body, shore interface, and right of way for evacuation.
  3. CCO alignment — Confirm whether any mining-plan / closure-related revision note is required before repurposing.
  4. Preliminary safety screening — Undertake initial hazard review of highwalls, slopes, shoreline, access, and residual mine risks, with DGMS consultation where needed.
  5. Preliminary technical assessment — Complete basic bathymetry, shoreline screening, access review, and indicative anchoring feasibility.
  6. Preliminary grid feasibility — Identify the likely interconnection point, voltage level, and broad evacuation concept.
  7. Statutory screening — Screen for forest, wildlife, wetland, CRZ / IPZ, SPCB, and other local regulatory triggers.
  8. Project structuring and bid strategy — Define indicative capacity, implementation models, risk allocation, and bidder scope.
  9. Draft RFP framework — Freeze technical scope, owner obligations, bid parameter, and evaluation criteria before issue.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected EPC contractor / developer / O&M agency may take up the following:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed bathymetry, geotechnical investigation, hydrology, shoreline stability, and cable landing assessment.
  2. Detailed engineering and DPR — Prepare final design, plant layout, DPR, equipment selection, and implementation schedule.
  3. Detailed approvals and submissions — Take up CEIG approvals, utility studies, protection coordination, and other project-specific statutory submissions.
  4. Procurement of equipment — Procure modules, floaters, anchoring systems, inverters, transformers, cables, SCADA, and safety systems.
  5. Construction and installation — Execute shoreline works, anchoring, floating array installation, electrical works, and evacuation infrastructure.
  6. Testing and commissioning — Carry out testing, synchronization, statutory inspection, and COD / operational acceptance.
  7. O&M system preparation — Prepare manuals, SOPs, maintenance schedules, spares planning, and monitoring systems.
  8. Recruitment and training — Mobilize O&M staff, safety personnel, and local support staff as per contract.
  9. Regular operation and monitoring — Undertake routine O&M, performance monitoring, compliance reporting, and owner review.
  10. Expansion / optimization — Take up future expansion only if technically viable and approved by the owner.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Model-wise responsibility allocation, local livelihood potential, and the strategic way forward — keeping the project open across multiple models until site conditions, funding, and ownership preferences are fully assessed.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) BOOT / DBFOT, (2) Lease / Concession Model, (3) RESCO Model, (4) EPC + O&M

Why preferred: BOOT / DBFOT is preferred because FSPV requires private capital, specialist anchoring / mooring, long-term O&M and performance responsibility. The mine owner can retain water-body control while transferring design, finance, construction and operational risks.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the mine owner wants ownership from inception and has funds, technical support and long-term O&M capability.

Suggestable technical care: Bathymetry, water-level fluctuation, anchoring, bank stability, grid evacuation, electrical safety, water rights, ecological impact, wind load and O&M access must be assessed.

Recommended development logic: Start with feasibility, bathymetric survey and grid screening. Develop FSPV in phases and consider FSPV + BESS where dispatchability and peak supply value are important.
