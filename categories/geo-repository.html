Final Recommendations / Geo-Repository / Geoscience Data Centre
Geo-repository
Global Precedents
USGS NGMDB Geoscience Repository, Denver, USA
A Geo-Repository / Centralized Geoscience Data Centre on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land converts under-utilised administrative infrastructure into a knowledge, data, training, and decision-support facility. It stores, manages, validates, visualises, and disseminates spatial, geological, exploration, mine-planning, environmental, geotechnical, hydrogeological, drone-survey, remote-sensing, and closure-monitoring data — creating a long-term digital asset from mine-closure legacy records.
A Geo-Repository has a low physical footprint but high institutional value. It is especially suited to mines with existing administrative buildings, control rooms, survey offices, or training facilities. India's reference model is the National Geoscience Data Repository (NGDR) under the National Mineral Exploration Policy, 2016. The CCO frameworks A.R.T.H.A., L.I.V.E.S. and R.E.C.L.A.I.M. explicitly support knowledge-based repurposing as part of structured mine closure and just-transition planning.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability and technical specification matrix, and indicative scale models — from a mine-level pilot Geo-Repository Cell to a national Geoscience and Mine Closure Digital Campus.

This concept proposes the development of a Geo-Repository / Centralized Geoscience Data Centre on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. A Geo-Repository is a centralized platform for storing, managing, validating, visualizing, and disseminating spatial, geological, exploration, mine-planning, environmental, geotechnical, hydrogeological, drone-survey, remote-sensing, and closure-monitoring data.

The objective is to convert under-utilized mine infrastructure or mine-owned land into a knowledge, data, training, and decision-support facility that can serve mine closure planning; geological and exploration data archiving; land-repurposing studies; environmental and biodiversity monitoring; subsidence and slope-risk monitoring; groundwater and surface-water monitoring; drone and satellite data management; GIS-based land-use planning; statutory reporting; training and capacity building; and research collaboration with universities, geological agencies, and technical institutions.

Unlike heavy land-based industrial reuse, a Geo-Repository has a low physical footprint but high long-term institutional value. It is especially suitable where the closed mine has existing administrative buildings, control rooms, survey offices, communication infrastructure, secure compounds, or training facilities that can be repurposed. India already has a national-level reference model in the National Geoscience Data Repository (NGDR) conceptualised by the Ministry of Mines under the National Mineral Exploration Policy, 2016.

  • Creates a long-term digital asset from mine-closure data — preserving legacy mine plans, borehole logs, geological maps, exploration reports, survey records, and environmental data
  • Supports GIS-based mine-closure and post-closure monitoring, enabling safe land repurposing through spatial risk mapping
  • Enables future exploration, mineral assessment, groundwater studies, and land-use planning on and around the closed mine
  • Requires limited land compared with industrial, agricultural, or tourism models — can reuse existing mine offices, survey buildings, training centres, control rooms, or communication facilities
  • Can generate revenue through data services, training, analytics, consulting, GIS services, digital archiving, and institutional partnerships
  • Supports AI / ML-based geological interpretation, risk prediction, resource modelling, and closure-monitoring analytics
  • Supports compliance with geospatial and data-governance frameworks including the National Geospatial Policy 2022 and 2021 Geospatial Data Guidelines
  • Creates skilled local employment in GIS, surveying, data digitization, drone mapping, field validation, database management, and digital operations
  • Provides a centralized system for mine-owner, regulator, researcher, and community-facing data products
  • Aligns with national geoscience and digital infrastructure priorities — NGDR, MERT, OGC standards, and Digital India

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Describes what must be ready before the first RFP — building safety, data inventory, metadata standards, cybersecurity requirements, connectivity assessment — and what can be taken up post-award.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Geo-Repository-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, building and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, building availability, mine closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of using the site as a geoscience data, GIS, archive, training and monitoring facility under the approved Mine Closure Plan / Final Mine Closure Plan framework. Mine closure plans are treated as an integral part of mining plans under the 2025 Coal and Lignite Mining Plan and Mine Closure Plan guidelines.
  2. Legal, data ownership and access screening — Clarify ownership of mine plans, borehole logs, exploration reports, geological maps, survey records, environmental data, drone data and closure-monitoring datasets, including rules for public access, restricted access, commercial use and third-party data sharing.
  3. Geospatial policy compliance screening — Review applicability of the National Geospatial Policy, 2022 and DST Guidelines for acquiring and producing Geospatial Data and Geospatial Data Services including Maps, 2021, especially for data generation, digitization, map production, access control and sensitive attributes.
  4. Data inventory and classification — Prepare an inventory of available physical and digital records, including mine plans, geological reports, borehole data, exploration blocks, CAD files, GIS layers, satellite images, drone survey data, hydrology data, environmental monitoring data and mine-closure records.
  5. NGDR / MERT alignment note — Prepare a note on compatibility with the National Geoscience Data Repository and Mineral Exploration Reporting Template, since NGDR is the Ministry of Mines' national platform for hosting exploration-related geoscientific data and MERT is used for standardized reporting.
  6. Preliminary technical site assessment — Assess building condition, server-room suitability, power reliability, internet connectivity, fire safety, cooling requirement, archive-room suitability, physical security, flood risk, subsidence risk and access safety.
  7. Repository scope and user definition — Define whether the facility will serve only one closed mine, a group of mines, a regional mine-closure database, or a public-facing knowledge centre for mine repurposing, research and training.
  8. Data architecture and standards framework — Prepare a preliminary architecture covering GIS database, document management system, metadata standard, coordinate reference system, backup policy, dashboard requirement, data validation workflow and archival protocol.
  9. Cybersecurity and disaster recovery plan — Define role-based access, user authentication, encryption, audit logs, backup frequency, off-site backup, disaster recovery, server security and physical protection of original records.
  10. Community, institutional and stakeholder consultation — Consult mine planning, geology, survey, environment, safety, IT, land, CSR, local administration, academic institutions, research agencies, GSI / state geology agencies where relevant, and potential training partners.
  11. CAPEX, O&M and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates covering building renovation, scanning, servers, GIS software, cloud / on-premises storage, workstations, archive systems, cybersecurity, staffing and annual maintenance; then select a preferred model such as Hybrid, EPC + O&M, Concession / LDOT or DBFOT.
  12. Draft RFP scope, obligations and bid framework — Prepare bidder scope covering digitization, georeferencing, metadata creation, database development, GIS portal, dashboard, cybersecurity, training, O&M, data confidentiality, system handover, service-level standards and evaluation criteria.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / technology partner can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site and facility investigation — Undertake detailed assessment of the selected building / land, including structural safety, electrical load, server-room feasibility, fire safety, access control, cooling requirement, archive-room suitability and physical security.
  2. Detailed data audit and gap assessment — Review all available maps, mine plans, sections, borehole logs, exploration reports, survey files, environmental data, closure-monitoring data and digital records to identify missing, duplicate, damaged, restricted and high-priority datasets.
  3. Final data governance and access protocol — Prepare the approved data governance framework covering data ownership, access levels, public / restricted / confidential classification, user registration, commercial-use conditions, audit trail and data-sharing approval process.
  4. Detailed Project Report — The selected operator shall prepare the DPR covering final repository architecture, hardware and software design, digitization methodology, metadata framework, NGDR / MERT compatibility, cybersecurity plan, O&M plan, staffing plan, training plan, financial model and implementation schedule.
  5. Facility renovation and infrastructure setup — Renovate the building and establish archive rooms, scanning rooms, GIS workstations, server room, UPS, cooling, fire protection, CCTV, access control, network cabling and secure storage for original records.
  6. Digitization and physical archive management — Scan, catalogue and preserve physical records such as maps, mine plans, registers, reports, sections, drawings, photographs and field books, while maintaining an indexed physical archive for original documents.
  7. Data conversion, georeferencing and standardization — Convert paper, CAD, PDF, raster and tabular records into GIS-compatible formats, georeference spatial data, standardize coordinate systems and prepare clean database-ready datasets.
  8. Metadata and MERT-compatible reporting — Create dataset-level metadata covering title, location, scale, date, source, coordinate system, accuracy, lineage, ownership, restrictions and update status; exploration datasets should be structured to the extent possible in MERT-compatible formats.
  9. Database, GIS portal and dashboard development — Develop the spatial database, document repository, GIS web portal, mine-closure dashboard, land-repurposing layers, environmental monitoring dashboard and controlled user-access system.
  10. Cybersecurity, backup and disaster recovery implementation — Install and test authentication, role-based permissions, encryption, firewall, backup system, off-site storage, disaster recovery process and activity logs before operational use.
  11. Validation, quality check and owner approval — Conduct technical validation of digitized maps, coordinates, attributes, metadata, document links, dashboard outputs and access controls, followed by review and approval by the mine owner.
  12. Training and capacity building — Train mine-owner staff and selected local youth in GIS basics, scanning, metadata entry, document management, dashboard operation, data upload, access control and routine system maintenance.
  13. Trial operation and system audit — Run the repository on pilot datasets, test user access, check retrieval speed, verify backup restoration, conduct cybersecurity checks and resolve errors before full commissioning.
  14. Commissioning and institutional onboarding — Commission the Geo-Repository after successful validation and onboard authorized users such as mine departments, regulators, technical consultants, research institutions and training partners.
  15. Regular operation, updating and phased expansion — Carry out routine data updates, periodic backups, metadata audits, cybersecurity reviews, user support, dashboard improvements, new dataset onboarding and phased expansion to additional mines or regional datasets.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Covers the indicative 15-step implementation pathway, preferred models in order, recommended development position as a Mine Closure Digital Knowledge and Geoscience Data Centre, key references, and domain abbreviations.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) EPC + O&M / Facility Management, (3) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because a geo-repository requires mine data control, institutional credibility, digital systems and public / research access. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner data governance + technical GIS / geoscience partner + academic institution + IT / cybersecurity support.

Secondary option: DBFOT may be used for large regional or multi-mine geoscience data campuses needing private finance and long-term professional operation.

Suggestable technical care: Metadata standards, GIS compatibility, data security, controlled access, digitization, backup, physical archive conditions, dashboard design and sensitive mine-data protocols must be ensured.

Recommended development logic: Start with mine closure GIS database, geological archive, borehole log system, mine-plan record archive and monitoring dashboards. Expand into training, public knowledge gallery and institutional research collaboration.
