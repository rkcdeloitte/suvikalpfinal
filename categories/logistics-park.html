Final Recommendations / Logistics Park / Multi-Modal Logistics
Logistic Park
Precedents
MMLP Bharatmala, Bengaluru, India
This concept proposes the development of Logistics Park Zones / Multi-Modal Logistics Park Zones (MMLPs) on closed, reclaimed, stabilized, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land into a productive logistics, warehousing, transportation, aggregation, distribution, and value-added supply-chain asset — including warehousing and storage blocks, truck terminals, rail-linked cargo handling, cold storage, e-commerce fulfilment centres, packaging and sorting units, and digital control facilities. Government of India has approved 35 Multi-Modal Logistics Park locations to improve logistics efficiency, being developed through PPP on DBFOT basis in a hub-and-spoke model.
Unlike heavy industrial reuse, a logistics park uses large land parcels productively without requiring very high-density manufacturing; leverages existing mine roads, rail connectivity, power infrastructure and industrial buffers; and supports local employment in warehousing, transport, handling, security, packaging and facility management. The concept aligns with PM GatiShakti, the National Logistics Policy 2022, and CCO mine-closure frameworks L.I.V.E.S., R.E.C.L.A.I.M. and A.R.T.H.A. It generates lease rent, concession fees, user charges, warehousing income, and revenue share for the mine owner — creating a long-term revenue-generating post-mining land-use system.

PROJECT VISION

Project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix and indicative scale options for developing a logistics park or MMLP on closed coal mine land.

This concept proposes the development of Logistics Park Zones / Multi-Modal Logistics Park Zones on closed, reclaimed, stabilized, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy.

The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land into a productive logistics, warehousing, transportation, aggregation, distribution, and value-added supply-chain asset centered around:

  • Warehousing and storage blocks
  • Truck terminal and parking zones
  • Rail siding / rail-linked cargo handling, where feasible
  • Container handling and transshipment areas
  • Cold storage and temperature-controlled warehousing
  • E-commerce fulfilment and distribution centres
  • Packaging, sorting, grading and labelling units
  • Repair, maintenance and driver facilities
  • Weighbridges, fuel / EV charging and service yards
  • Customs / bonded storage, where applicable
  • Administrative, security and digital control facilities
  • Green buffer, stormwater, drainage and utility corridors

Unlike heavy industrial reuse, a logistics park uses large land parcels productively without requiring very high-density manufacturing activity; leverages existing mine roads, rail connectivity, power infrastructure and industrial buffers; and supports local employment in warehousing, transport, handling, security, maintenance, packaging, administration and facility management.

This aligns with India's mine-closure shift toward productive and community-oriented repurposing, as reflected in frameworks such as L.I.V.E.S., R.E.C.L.A.I.M. and A.R.T.H.A. The concept is also aligned with India's broader logistics policy direction — Government of India has approved 35 Multi-Modal Logistics Park locations to improve logistics efficiency and reduce logistics costs, being developed through PPP on DBFOT basis in a hub-and-spoke model.

A Logistics Park is particularly suited for closed coal mine areas where large land parcels, heavy vehicle access, existing industrial roads, rail proximity, power corridors and buffer distances are already available or can be upgraded.

  • Converts closed mine land into productive economic infrastructure
  • Supports regional freight aggregation, distribution and supply-chain efficiency
  • Reduces risk of abandoned land misuse, encroachment or unsafe occupation
  • Creates direct and indirect employment in transport, warehousing, loading, unloading, packaging, security, repair, maintenance and facility management
  • Can support local MSMEs, traders, farmers, industries and e-commerce supply chains
  • Can be developed in phases depending on land availability and demand
  • Can integrate road, rail and, where feasible, inland-waterway or port-linked cargo movement
  • Supports PM GatiShakti and National Logistics Policy objectives for seamless movement of goods and services
  • Generates lease rent, concession fee, user charges, warehousing income, parking income and revenue share for the mine owner
  • Can become a regional economic anchor for post-mining transition

PM GatiShakti is intended to address connectivity gaps and enable seamless movement of people, goods and services with cost efficiency. A mine-area logistics park can support this objective if it is located near highways, rail corridors, industrial clusters, ports, consumption centres or mineral / agricultural production belts.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Owner-side readiness checklist before first RFP, post-award workstreams, and indicative implementation pathway for logistics park development on closed coal mine land.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Logistics Park / Multi-Modal Logistics Park based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility and whether logistics / warehousing / transport infrastructure can be legally developed on the site.
  2. Legal and concession screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease rights, concession period, revenue-sharing permissibility, user-charge rights, warehouse leasing rights, sub-licensing rights and asset-transfer conditions.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, unsafe water bodies, abandoned structures, restricted mine infrastructure and required buffer areas.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, soil-bearing assessment, drainage review, flood-risk note and heavy-vehicle circulation feasibility for roads, yards and warehouse platforms.
  5. Connectivity and multimodal screening — Assess highway access, approach-road width, nearest railway siding / station, possibility of rail spur, industrial-corridor linkage, first-mile / last-mile connectivity and PM GatiShakti alignment.
  6. Cargo-demand and market assessment — Estimate potential cargo from nearby industries, mines, agriculture markets, MSMEs, e-commerce users, cold-chain users, rail terminals, ports or consumption centres.
  7. Preliminary logistics zoning and land-use plan — Prepare a basic layout showing entry / exit, truck terminal, warehouses, open storage, cold chain, container yard, repair bay, parking, utility zones, green buffer and future expansion land.
  8. Environmental and statutory screening — Assess dust, traffic emissions, noise, stormwater, waste handling, fire safety, hazardous-material restrictions, pollution-control requirements and local-body / industrial development permissions.
  9. Utility and digital-readiness assessment — Assess power, water, drainage, sewage, telecom, internet, CCTV, weighbridge integration, gate automation and digital logistics-management requirements.
  10. Implementation model and project structuring — Select the preferred model such as Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, Hybrid, BOOT or EPC + O&M, using PPP / DBFOT logic where the project is large and commercially bankable.
  11. Cost, revenue and bankability assessment — Prepare broad CAPEX, 5-year O&M estimate, lease-rent potential, warehouse-rental potential, parking charges, handling charges, user fees, revenue-share logic and viability-gap requirement, if any.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid conditions — Prepare bidder scope covering design, finance, construction, operation, maintenance, safety compliance, local employment, environmental safeguards, minimum investment, phasing and performance standards.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering logistics experience, financial capacity, PPP / concession experience, cargo tie-ups, technical proposal, O&M plan, local livelihood commitments and revenue offer.
  14. Owner-side approvals and enabling-scope note — Identify what the owner will provide before award, such as land access, fencing, safety buffers, basic road link, utility connection, closure-related permissions and support for statutory clearances.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / developer / logistics operator / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, soil testing, pavement investigation, drainage study and final safe-zone verification.
  2. Detailed Project Report and final master plan — Prepare the DPR, final logistics master plan, phasing plan, financial model, risk-allocation plan and implementation schedule for approval by the mine owner.
  3. Detailed engineering and design — Prepare detailed designs for internal roads, truck terminal, warehouse platforms, drainage, utilities, weighbridge, parking, cold chain, container yard, fire systems and security systems.
  4. Statutory approvals and clearances — Obtain required approvals from local authority, fire department, pollution-control authority, electrical inspectorate, factory / warehouse authority, railway authority where applicable and other competent agencies.
  5. SPV / project company and financial closure — Where required under PPP / DBFOT / concession structure, form the project SPV, execute project agreements, achieve financial closure and submit performance security.
  6. Tenanting and cargo-linkage plan — Secure letters of interest or agreements with warehouse users, transporters, 3PL players, e-commerce users, cold-chain users, industrial units and cargo aggregators.
  7. Procurement and contractor mobilization — Procure construction contractors, material-handling systems, warehouse equipment, digital systems, weighbridges, fire-safety systems, CCTV, gate systems and O&M service providers.
  8. Site development and enabling works — Carry out site grading, compaction, boundary works, internal roads, stormwater drainage, utilities, lighting, green buffer, safety barricading and access-control infrastructure.
  9. Construction of logistics assets — Develop warehouses, truck terminal, open storage yard, repair / maintenance bay, admin block, driver facilities, cold storage, container yard and rail-linked facilities where feasible.
  10. Digital and operational systems setup — Install gate automation, RFID / barcode systems, weighbridge software, warehouse-management system, transport-management system, CCTV, fire-alarm system and command / control room.
  11. Local livelihood and vendor integration — Onboard local youth, drivers, handling workers, security staff, housekeeping teams, repair-service providers, food kiosks, transport operators and MSME service vendors as per concession obligations.
  12. Trial operations and safety audit — Conduct trial truck movement, warehouse loading tests, fire-safety checks, emergency drills, traffic-flow testing, digital-system testing and third-party safety / compliance audit.
  13. Commercial commissioning — Commission the logistics park after completion of minimum development obligations, statutory approvals, tenant readiness, operational staffing and safety certification.
  14. Regular operation and performance monitoring — Carry out O&M, cargo handling, warehouse leasing, truck-terminal management, billing, reporting, environmental monitoring, safety audits and compliance reporting to the mine owner.
  15. Phased expansion — Expand warehouses, cold chain, rail terminal, container yard, value-added logistics, EV charging or additional plots based on cargo demand, utilization levels, revenue performance and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward recommendations, key references, and abbreviations for logistics park and MMLP development on mine closure sites.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (2) DBFOT, (3) Hybrid

Why preferred: Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is preferred because logistics parks require private warehousing capability, tenant development, cargo demand and specialist operation. The mine owner retains land control while transferring market and operating risks.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be used only where the owner wants strong control over user charges, local employment and facility standards under a clear public or captive logistics use case.

Suggestable technical care: Road / rail access, cargo demand, land stability, truck movement, drainage, utilities, fire safety, warehousing layout, weighbridge, security and tenant demand must be assessed.

Recommended development logic: Start with land screening, cargo mapping and access planning. Develop in phases through warehousing, truck terminal, cold chain, e-commerce and value-added logistics based on tenant absorption.
