Solar PV Farms
Precedents
Bhadla Solar Power Plant, Rajasthan — Installed Capacity 2245 MW

In India, solar photovoltaic (PV) farms on post-closure coal mines offer a strategic pathway for a just transition by repurposing environmentally degraded lands into renewable energy assets. With over 500 square kilometres of former mine land identified across coal-bearing states, India has a significant potential to install over 27 GW of solar capacity.

This initiative leverages existing infrastructure, such as power transmission lines, and generates new employment opportunities in construction and operations for communities that previously depended on the coal industry. However, successful implementation requires addressing significant challenges, including clarifying land ownership, addressing regulatory hurdles, and ensuring environmental stability through proper reclamation to prevent issues like land subsidence and water contamination.

PROJECT VISION

Converting under-utilized post-mining land into productive renewable energy infrastructure — supporting sustainable mine closure, rehabilitation, and long-term economic value creation.

This concept proposes the repurposing of closed mine land for development of a Solar Photovoltaic (SPV) Farm project. The objective is to convert under-utilized post-mining land assets into productive renewable energy infrastructure that supports sustainable mine closure, environmental rehabilitation, and long-term economic value creation.

The project is intended as an early-stage concept and structuring framework, not as a final tender or implementation document. It is meant to support internal decision-making, stakeholder consultation, and preparation for the next stage of feasibility assessment, approval mapping, and bid structuring.

Closed mine areas often contain large tracts of disturbed or low-alternative-use land that can be productively reused for ground-mounted solar development. Compared to many greenfield projects, mine-affected land may reduce pressure on agricultural or habitation land and can visibly demonstrate post-mining transition.

Key strategic benefits include:

  • Productive reuse of closed mine land
  • Clean energy generation from post-mining assets
  • Reduced pressure on agricultural and other competing land uses
  • Support for ESG, sustainability, and mine closure visibility
  • Potential for local employment, skill development, and community benefit
  • Opportunity to use existing mine infrastructure such as roads, substations, and transmission access where available

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Defining what needs to be in place before tendering, and mapping out the post-award workstreams for construction, commissioning, and operation.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing the first RFP for a Solar PV Farm repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, closure and permissibility confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, access rights, and whether the proposed Solar PV use is compatible with the mine-closure framework and internal repurposing approvals.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease / concession permissibility, right-of-way requirements, commercial rights, revenue-sharing permissibility, and insurance approach for project land and evacuation corridor.
  3. Preliminary site and constructability assessment — Prepare topographic survey, contour screening, drainage review, geotechnical screening, ground-stability note, and broad layout suitability for module mounting, roads, inverter stations, and switchyard.
  4. Resource and layout screening — Prepare preliminary solar resource assessment, usable-area estimate, shading / obstruction screening, row-layout logic, and indicative CUF / PR assumptions for bid structuring. Utility-scale Solar PV bidding and technical documents typically anchor project planning around solar resource basis, generation estimates, and detailed technical specifications.
  5. Grid connectivity and evacuation screening — Identify the interconnection point, voltage level, bay availability, evacuation route, metering implications, and broad protection-system requirements, keeping in view applicable CEA connectivity and metering requirements and state / DISCOM interface conditions.
  6. Technology and standards screening — Freeze the broad technology approach such as fixed-tilt or tracker, string or central inverter philosophy, and preliminary module strategy, including compliance with applicable MNRE requirements such as the Approved Models and Manufacturers framework where relevant.
  7. Safety and statutory screening — Identify likely electrical safety, CEIG / electrical inspectorate requirements, fencing and access-control needs, and site-specific statutory triggers such as forest, wildlife, CRZ / IPZ, and SPCB-related requirements. SECI tender conditions also routinely place electrical safety and grid-compliance responsibility on the project developer in line with the Electricity Act and CEA rules.
  8. Stakeholder and local-use screening — Consult local administration, revenue authorities, DISCOM / STU / CTU interface agencies as applicable, nearby communities, and other land users to identify access issues, land-use conflicts, and local livelihood opportunities.
  9. Preliminary commercial and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and O&M estimates and select the preferred structure such as EPC, EPC + O&M, BOOT, BOO, RESCO, IPP + Long-term PPA, or Hybrid, consistent with current tariff-based competitive bidding practice for grid-connected Solar PV projects.
  10. Draft RFP scope and bid evaluation framework — Prepare bidder scope covering design, supply, construction, grid connectivity, O&M obligations, performance guarantees, local employment provisions, and bid evaluation criteria covering technical capability, financial strength, compliance, and commercial offer. Recent Ministry of Power bidding guidelines specifically provide the framework for tariff-based competitive procurement of grid-connected Solar PV power projects.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected developer / EPC contractor / O&M agency / solar power developer may take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, geotechnical investigation, contour mapping, drainage study, soil testing, and constructability assessment for module foundations, internal roads, inverter stations, pooling area, and switchyard.
  2. Detailed project engineering and layout finalization — Finalize module layout, mounting design, inverter architecture, DC / AC collection system, internal roads, drainage, earthing, SCADA, and plant-side facilities based on final site data and technical specifications.
  3. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — Prepare the DPR covering detailed surveys, final layout, engineering package, generation estimate, financial model, implementation schedule, O&M plan, approvals matrix, and risk-management plan for owner approval before execution.
  4. Approvals and connectivity compliance — Obtain final grid-connectivity approvals, metering approvals, protection approvals, CEIG / electrical approvals, and other statutory clearances from the concerned authorities, utilities, and inspectorates. In Indian solar procurement practice, responsibility for obtaining connectivity and project approvals is often assigned to the project developer, subject to the applicable state and CEA / DISCOM framework.
  5. Procurement of equipment and systems — Procure PV modules, mounting structures, inverters, transformers, switchgear, SCADA, weather station, cables, protection systems, and auxiliary infrastructure in line with bid specifications and applicable MNRE / Make in India requirements where relevant.
  6. Construction and installation — Execute land development, foundations, mounting installation, module erection, inverter and transformer installation, cabling, switchyard works, communications system, fencing, roads, drainage, and project evacuation interface.
  7. Testing, commissioning and performance verification — Carry out pre-commissioning tests, protection testing, meter testing, SCADA integration, grid-synchronization checks, trial generation, and performance demonstration including CUF / PR-related verification as per contract conditions and utility requirements. CEA's metering framework and utility interconnection requirements are central to this stage.
  8. O&M preparation, staffing and local integration — Finalize O&M manuals, spares strategy, preventive-maintenance schedules, safety procedures, staffing plan, and local integration for security, housekeeping, vegetation management, module cleaning, and other support services.
  9. Commercial operation and routine monitoring — Commence regular operation, generation monitoring, grid-compliance reporting, periodic maintenance, safety inspections, environmental compliance, and contractual performance reporting.
  10. Phased optimization or expansion — Optimize layout utilization, add auxiliary systems, or undertake phased expansion subject to land availability, grid capacity, performance history, and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Capturing the way forward, decision criteria for model selection, key references, and abbreviations used across the framework.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) BOOT / DBFOT, (2) RESCO, (3) Lease / Concession Model, (4) EPC + O&M

Why preferred: BOOT / DBFOT is preferred where private investment, long-term O&M and eventual transfer are desired. RESCO is suitable where the owner wants power procurement without upfront CAPEX.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the mine owner has funds, wants ownership and has a clear captive or contracted power use case.

Suggestable technical care: Land stability, shading, module layout, grid evacuation, offtake, land-use permission, drainage, security, vegetation control and O&M access must be assessed.

Recommended development logic: Start with land and grid feasibility. Develop in phases and integrate BESS where peak shifting, reliability or dispatchability improves project value.
