Final Recommendations / Battery Energy Storage System (BESS)
BESS (Battery Energy Storage)
Precedents
SECI 50 MW BESS, Rajnandgaon, India

In India, Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are emerging as a critical component of the clean energy transition, enabling storage and dispatch of renewable energy to meet grid stability requirements and peak demand. Closed coal mines offer a strategically suitable environment for BESS installations due to the availability of large, flat, previously disturbed land parcels, proximity to existing electrical grid infrastructure, and the opportunity to contribute to a just energy transition in mining-affected regions.

The development of BESS facilities in such regions can generate skilled employment and economic activity for local communities, particularly those transitioning from mining-based livelihoods, through roles in installation, operations, maintenance, and grid management. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including securing appropriate land parcels, ensuring grid connectivity and power offtake agreements, managing environmental and safety considerations related to battery storage, and establishing robust regulatory and financial frameworks to ensure the long-term viability and operational efficiency of BESS projects.

PROJECT VISION

Project overview, strategic rationale, site suitability assessment, and indicative BESS scale and configuration matrix for post-mining land.

  • What: A Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) stores electricity during low-demand or surplus periods and discharges during high-demand or grid-stress periods — improving the timing, reliability, and commercial value of electricity on post-mining land
  • Why it fits mine land: Closed mine sites offer large flat land parcels, existing power infrastructure (substations, transformers, cabling), good grid connectivity, and access roads — all directly usable for BESS deployment
  • Grid value: Unlike generation, BESS improves system flexibility — enabling peak shaving, renewable firming, load shifting, frequency regulation, and backup power without adding intermittency
  • National alignment: Supports India's National Energy Storage Mission, SECI's standalone BESS tenders, Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme (RDSS), and Net Zero 2070 commitments
  • Who benefits: Mine owner (asset monetisation, ESG), grid operator (flexibility services), local community (energy security), renewable developers (curtailment reduction and firming)
  • Integration potential: Can be combined with Floating Solar PV (FSPV) or ground-mounted SPV on the same mine site — improving project bankability and dispatch profile
  • Peak demand reduction: Discharges during peak demand to reduce maximum demand charges — significant cost saving for captive users
  • Time shifting / energy arbitrage: Charges during low-price periods, discharges during high-price periods — improves economic efficiency
  • Backup and reliability: Provides short-duration power during grid interruption — enhances reliability for critical operations
  • Renewable firming: Smooths variable solar/wind output — improves predictability and dispatchability of renewable energy
  • Grid support services: Frequency regulation, ramp support, voltage support — strengthens system stability and opens ancillary service revenue
  • Curtailment reduction: Stores otherwise unused renewable generation — increases energy utilisation and project revenue
  • Power quality: Fast response to fluctuations protects sensitive equipment — reduces downtime and maintenance costs
  • Mine-closure alignment: Productive use of otherwise idle land; ESG reporting value; supports just-transition narrative for coal mine closure

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP preparation checklist, post-award activity scope, and indicative implementation pathway for BESS development on post-mining land.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

  1. Land, closure and permissibility confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, site access rights, and whether the proposed BESS use is compatible with the mine-closure framework and internal asset-repurposing approvals.
  2. Project use-case and asset classification note — Clearly define whether the BESS is intended for captive use, peak shaving, renewable integration, reliability support, transmission / distribution support, or ancillary-service-linked use, consistent with the Ministry of Power's BESS utilization framework.
  3. Legal, commercial and contracting screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease / concession permissibility, user charges, revenue-sharing permissibility, insurance approach, and preferred delivery structure such as EPC, EPC + O&M, BOOT, BOO, RESCO, or hybrid.
  4. Preliminary site and geotechnical screening — Prepare preliminary topographic survey, geotechnical screening, drainage assessment, constructability note, and hazard mapping for reclaimed land, subsidence-prone areas, unstable dumps, and access corridors.
  5. Electrical interconnection and evacuation screening — Identify the interconnection point, voltage level, bay availability, evacuation route, metering implications, and broad protection-system requirements for grid-connected or captive integration.
  6. Preliminary load, dispatch and sizing assessment — Prepare a basic assessment of load profile, peak demand pattern, outage profile, charging source, discharge windows, indicative MW / MWh sizing, and expected operating cycles.
  7. Safety and compliance pre-screening — Identify applicable electrical safety, fire safety, hazardous-area, emergency access, and BESS-specific safety requirements, including likely CEIG / electrical inspectorate interface and owner-side safety philosophy.
  8. Environmental and statutory screening — Screen site-specific statutory triggers such as forest, wildlife, CRZ / IPZ, SPCB-related requirements, battery-waste handling implications, and any water / drainage permissions if applicable.
  9. Funding and scheme eligibility assessment — Examine whether the project may be developed under normal commercial mode, PPP mode, VGF-supported mode, or state / central support mechanisms under current BESS policy frameworks.
  10. Preliminary implementation model selection — Select the preferred procurement / delivery model and prepare the broad risk-allocation logic for financing, ownership, dispatch rights, O&M responsibility, performance guarantees, and asset transfer where applicable.
  11. Draft RFP scope and performance framework — Prepare the draft bidder scope covering design, supply, installation, integration, EMS / SCADA, commissioning, safety compliance, availability guarantee, augmentation philosophy, O&M obligations, and end-of-life / battery replacement responsibility.
  12. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering BESS experience, OEM / integrator capability, safety record, technical compliance, financial capacity, lifecycle support, local employment approach, and commercial offer.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, geotechnical investigation, drainage study, soil / foundation assessment, and constructability confirmation for the BESS blocks, control room, transformer area, fire systems, and access roads.
  2. Detailed electrical and interconnection studies — Carry out detailed evacuation design, protection coordination, metering scheme, relay philosophy, short-circuit assessment, auxiliary power design, and utility interface studies.
  3. Detailed BESS sizing and system engineering — Finalize MW / MWh sizing, operating duty cycle, battery chemistry, container / enclosure layout, PCS configuration, EMS, SCADA, augmentation philosophy, and warranty-backed performance assumptions.
  4. Detailed safety, fire and emergency planning — Prepare the detailed safety management plan, fire detection and suppression design, emergency response plan, electrical isolation philosophy, access-control plan, and incident-response protocol in line with applicable safety requirements.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — Prepare the DPR covering final surveys, system design, electrical integration, safety design, project cost, implementation schedule, O&M plan, statutory approvals matrix, and lifecycle management strategy for owner approval before execution.
  6. Approvals, permissions and statutory compliance — Obtain final utility approvals, CEIG / electrical approvals, metering approvals, fire clearances, environmental / SPCB clearances where applicable, and any other permissions required prior to energization.
  7. Procurement of equipment and systems — Procure battery systems, PCS, EMS, SCADA, transformers, switchgear, protection systems, fire systems, security systems, communications equipment, and approved auxiliary systems.
  8. Construction, installation and integration — Execute civil works, foundations, BESS block installation, cabling, control-room works, transformer and switchyard integration, fire systems, communications, and grid / load interconnection.
  9. Testing, commissioning and acceptance procedures — Carry out factory and site acceptance tests, functional tests, protection tests, EMS / SCADA integration checks, trial charging / discharging, performance demonstration, and reliability verification.
  10. Preparation of operating procedures and manuals — Finalize SOPs for charging / discharging, dispatch control, shutdown and isolation, emergency response, inspection, maintenance, augmentation, incident reporting, and battery replacement handling.
  11. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train operators, technicians, safety personnel, control-room staff, maintenance teams, and local support manpower for routine operation and emergency response.
  12. Local livelihood integration — Integrate local employment and service opportunities such as security, housekeeping, minor maintenance support, vegetation control, logistics support, and auxiliary facility services, subject to project structure.
  13. Trial operation and performance stabilization — Undertake initial operating cycles, dispatch trials, reliability runs, safety drills, and performance stabilization before commercial operation date declaration.
  14. Commercial operation and routine O&M — Commence regular operation, scheduled maintenance, safety inspections, warranty management, environmental compliance, and periodic performance reporting.
  15. Lifecycle review and augmentation planning — Plan periodic capacity review, augmentation requirements, major component replacement strategy, warranty-event management, and end-of-life disposal / recycling compliance under applicable battery-waste rules.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward recommendations, key references, and abbreviations for BESS development on post-mining and closed mine sites.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) DBFOT / BOOT, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) Hybrid, (4) EPC + O&M

Why preferred: DBFOT / BOOT is preferred for larger BESS projects because battery storage requires high capital, technology selection, performance guarantees, safety systems and specialist O&M. Hybrid may combine mine-owner land / grid facilitation + renewable developer + storage technology provider + power offtake arrangement.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the mine owner has a captive power use case, funding capacity and wants ownership from inception.

Suggestable technical care: Grid connectivity, fire safety, battery chemistry, thermal management, EMS / SCADA, evacuation capacity, warranties, degradation risk, recycling obligation and integration with solar or wind must be assessed.

Recommended development logic: Start with BESS only where there is a clear use case such as peak shifting, captive supply, renewable smoothing or grid service. Co-located SPV / FSPV + BESS should be prioritized where solar generation is already planned.

Final recommendation: DBFOT / BOOT is most suitable for commercial BESS, while Hybrid is best where the owner wants to enable clean-energy redevelopment without taking full technology risk.
