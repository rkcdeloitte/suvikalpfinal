WELLNESS CENTRE / HEALING / NATURE THERAPY
Precedents
The meadow wellness
Wellness Centre / Healing / Nature Therapy Centre development transforms reclaimed mine land, plantations, water bodies, restored dumps, benches, and natural slopes into a structured restorative facility for healing, yoga, meditation, forest bathing, nature immersion, preventive health, and community wellness. Unlike a general park or recreation facility, its main focus is the structured use of restored mine landscapes for stress reduction, mental well-being, environmental awareness, and community wellness — combining indoor and outdoor functions such as meditation halls, yoga decks, therapy gardens, herbal gardens, sensory trails, and forest bathing paths.
The project is linked with India's wellness-tourism positioning. The Ministry of Tourism has formulated a National Strategy and Roadmap for Medical and Wellness Tourism, and recent Government of India communication describes medical and wellness tourism as complementary pillars within India's healthcare and tourism ecosystem. Systematic reviews have found that forest therapy and nature-based interventions can improve depression, anxiety, stress, and mental well-being — making a mine-area Wellness Centre a credible, evidence-supported, and socially valuable post-mining repurposing option aligned with L.I.V.E.S. and A.R.T.H.A. mine-closure frameworks.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability and technical specification matrix, and indicative scale models — from a 0.5 ha community wellness node to a 25+ ha flagship wellness and ecological healing campus on repurposed mine land.

This concept proposes the development of a Wellness Centre / Healing / Nature Therapy Centre within a closed, closing, reclaimed, or repurposed mine area as part of a broader mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to transform selected mine land, reclaimed landscapes, plantations, water bodies, quiet open spaces, restored dumps, benches, natural slopes, and community-facing areas into a restorative public-use facility.

The Centre is different from a general park or recreation facility because its main focus is the structured use of restored mine landscapes for healing, relaxation, yoga, meditation, forest bathing, therapeutic walking, nature immersion, stress reduction, environmental awareness, and community wellness.

Possible components include: wellness reception and visitor orientation centre; yoga hall, open-air yoga deck, meditation pavilion, and breathing-practice spaces; forest bathing trail, nature-therapy trail, sensory walking path, barefoot path, and quiet walking loops; herbal garden, medicinal plant garden, aroma garden, butterfly garden, and healing landscape zones; naturopathy consultation room, wellness counselling room, therapy rooms, and recovery spaces; water-edge healing deck, reflection pond, mine-lake viewing area, and nature observation points; mindfulness zone, silence zone, digital-detox zone, and community healing space; healthy café, herbal beverage kiosk, local food counter, organic produce shop, and wellness retail; interpretation spaces for ecological restoration, mine-closure transformation, and sustainable living; and community enterprise spaces for local youth, former mine workers, women's groups, self-help groups, and wellness facilitators.

  • Productive reuse of reclaimed mine land, plantations, water bodies, access roads, restored dumps, and open spaces
  • Transformation of a former industrial landscape into a restorative community asset for healing, nature therapy, and ecological awareness
  • Support for community well-being, stress reduction, preventive health, mindfulness, yoga, meditation, and nature immersion
  • Educational value for schools, colleges, community groups, health institutions, and environmental groups
  • Support for wellness tourism, eco-tourism, day retreats, preventive health programs, community healing, and regional destination development
  • Generation of local employment in landscaping, gardening, wellness operations, guiding, ticketing, housekeeping, café, retail, security, transport, and maintenance
  • Livelihood opportunities for former mine workers, local youth, women's groups, self-help groups, yoga instructors, wellness guides, and community-based enterprises
  • Support for ESG, sustainable mine closure, just-transition, ecological restoration, social inclusion, and community-development objectives
  • Adaptive reuse of selected mine buildings, offices, guest houses, training halls, roads, water bodies, and landscaped areas
  • Nature-based healing has growing research support: systematic reviews have found forest therapy and nature-based interventions can improve depression, anxiety, stress, and mental well-being
  • Potential integration with eco-parks, biodiversity parks, medicinal plant gardens, mine-lake restoration, skill centres, cultural centres, and landscaped public spaces

Important note: The mine-area Wellness Centre should be positioned as a safe, guided, restorative, inclusive, and professionally managed wellness and preventive health support space — not as a substitute for clinical treatment.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Describes the 14-item checklist for what must be ready before the first RFP — site safety, ecological quality, wellness concept, demand assessment, accessibility, emergency response — and what can be taken up post-award by the selected bidder.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Wellness / Healing / Nature Therapy Centre mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Internal approval and project authorization — Obtain internal approval for development of the Wellness Centre / Healing / Nature Therapy Centre as the preferred post-mining repurposing use.
  2. Land title and rights confirmation — Confirm site boundary, access corridor, encumbrances, mine-closure status, and permissibility of public wellness use on the identified land.
  3. Legal and land-use screening — Review wellness use, nature-therapy, yoga / meditation, retreat use, food-service, public access, adaptive landscape reuse permissibility, user charges, and revenue-sharing conditions.
  4. Mine-hazard and safety assessment — Assess shafts, underground voids, subsidence risk, gas risk, unstable structures, open water, dump slopes, unsafe benches, and unsafe access areas to define the safe developable zone.
  5. Geotechnical and site-buildability assessment — Prepare geotechnical screening, slope-stability review, settlement assessment, erosion assessment, drainage review, and site-buildability note.
  6. Topographic survey and preliminary grading assessment — Prepare topographic survey and assess grading requirements for buildings, trails, garden areas, parking, and circulation.
  7. Ecological baseline assessment — Assess plantation status, biodiversity, habitat value, invasive species, native species potential, medicinal plant suitability, and ecological carrying capacity.
  8. Preliminary concept planning — Prepare a concept layout showing wellness building, yoga hall, meditation pavilion, therapy rooms, healing gardens, nature-therapy trails, visitor amenities, parking, retreat area, circulation, and landscape areas.
  9. Wellness concept and programme note — Define yoga, meditation, breathing practices, forest bathing, sensory walks, herbal gardens, stress-reduction programs, preventive health sessions, and community wellness camps.
  10. Demand assessment — Assess likely demand from local communities, schools, women's groups, elderly persons, tourists, wellness groups, yoga practitioners, corporate groups, and former mine workers.
  11. Health and emergency response planning — Plan for first-aid room, ambulance access, nearest hospital linkage, emergency contacts, and heat-stress response protocol.
  12. Utility feasibility assessment — Assess electricity, water supply, sanitation, HVAC, lighting, IT systems, CCTV, PA system, telecom, and emergency communication requirements.
  13. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, or EPC + O&M / Facility Management.
  14. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Embed mandatory public-access, safety, environmental-protection, local-hiring, former-worker participation, SHG participation, school-access, community-use, and affordability conditions in the bid structure.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / wellness operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, slope assessment, drainage assessment, foundation investigation, water-body assessment, and detailed safety-zone verification.
  2. Detailed environmental, health and safety planning — Prepare the Environmental Management Plan, site safety plan, emergency-response plan, visitor-flow plan, public-health and hygiene plan, waste-management plan, and final carrying-capacity assessment.
  3. Final wellness master planning — Prepare the final master plan showing wellness building, yoga / meditation areas, healing gardens, nature-therapy trails, silent zones, therapy rooms, café, parking, service areas, emergency routes, buffer zones, and local enterprise spaces.
  4. Detailed Project Report and financial model — Prepare the DPR including detailed surveys, final master plan, engineering drawings, wellness program plan, statutory approval plan, CAPEX, O&M cost, revenue model, environmental safeguards, and implementation schedule for owner approval.
  5. Detailed architectural, landscape and engineering design — Prepare detailed designs for yoga halls, meditation pavilions, therapy rooms, herbal gardens, sensory trails, pathways, decks, water-edge seating, toilets, café, visitor centre, utilities, parking, lighting, signage, and universal-access features.
  6. Approvals, registrations and insurance — Obtain final building permission, fire NOC where applicable, electrical safety approval, occupancy certificate, FSSAI licence, local body permissions, pollution / water permissions where required, insurance cover, and any AYUSH / Clinical Establishment registration if clinical or therapeutic services are offered. NBC Part 4 is the standard reference for fire and life-safety planning in buildings.
  7. Procurement of systems and wellness infrastructure — Procure furniture, therapy equipment, yoga accessories, first-aid systems, CCTV, PA system, digital ticketing / booking system, environmental monitoring devices, signage, lighting, and landscaping material.
  8. Construction and landscape development — Develop buildings, therapy rooms, trails, healing gardens, herbal gardens, meditation decks, seating areas, toilets, drinking-water points, café, parking, drainage works, lighting, barricades, and service infrastructure.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for visitor registration, session booking, yoga / meditation programs, nature-therapy walks, elderly visitor support, hygiene, emergency response, incident reporting, housekeeping, garden maintenance, and daily inspections.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train wellness coordinators, yoga instructors, nature-therapy guides, gardeners, housekeeping staff, security staff, visitor-service staff, first-aid personnel, café staff, and local youth.
  11. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, local food vendors, herbal product sellers, transport operators, gardeners, housekeeping teams, local guides, wellness facilitators, and community entrepreneurs.
  12. Trial operation and compliance audit — Conduct trial wellness sessions, visitor-flow testing, emergency drills, hygiene checks, fire-safety checks, accessibility review, landscape-readiness review, and owner / third-party compliance audit before public opening.
  13. Branding and wellness-tourism linkage — Develop branding, online booking, signage, tourism-circuit linkage, school / college outreach, corporate wellness packages, local health-camp linkages, and seasonal wellness programs.
  14. Commissioning and public opening — Open the Centre after successful trial runs, safety clearance, staff readiness, statutory compliance, visitor-management systems, and owner approval are in place.
  15. Regular operation, monitoring and phased expansion — Carry out routine O&M, visitor management, hygiene monitoring, environmental monitoring, fire-safety maintenance, feedback review, compliance reporting, livelihood tracking, and phased expansion based on demand, revenue, safety, and owner approval.

For a mine-area Wellness Centre / Healing / Nature Therapy Centre intended for public welfare, ecological restoration, preventive health, wellness tourism, community identity, and local livelihood, the most suitable models are likely to be Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, and DBFOT.

Where the owner wants stronger public control, predictable access, direct oversight over pricing, safety, community use, wellness standards, and environmental management, EPC + O&M / Facility Management can also be a strong option.

A BOO model should be used cautiously because long-term private control may reduce owner influence over public access, pricing, ecological protection, wellness standards, local livelihood obligations, and community benefits unless the agreement is carefully structured.

Final model selection should be made only after review of: funding availability; ownership and control preference; landscape potential; tourism and visitor-demand potential; site safety and buildability; accessibility; revenue potential; local livelihood objectives; long-term O&M capability; and environmental-protection requirements.
