Adventure Tourism Landscapes
Global Precedents
Eden Project, Cornwall, UK

Adventure tourism is emerging as a significant driver of sustainable economic growth, supported by diverse landscapes and expanding tourism infrastructure. Activities such as trekking, rafting, rock climbing, camping, and eco-tourism attract both domestic and international visitors. Post-closure coal mines present unique opportunities to be repurposed into adventure tourism destinations by utilizing mine voids, reclaimed land, and existing mining infrastructure for activities such as boating, zip-lining, cycling, camping, and eco-parks, thereby transforming degraded land into vibrant tourism hubs.

This approach promotes sustainable land use, ecological restoration, and regional development while creating employment and income opportunities for local communities, particularly those previously dependent on mining. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring safety and environmental stability of sites, developing adequate tourism infrastructure, providing skill training to local stakeholders, and establishing strong marketing and connectivity to attract visitors and ensure the long-term sustainability of adventure tourism initiatives.

PROJECT VISION

Using existing mine landforms — slopes, benches, ridgelines, quarry walls, voids, haul roads and water bodies — as assets for safe, regulated adventure tourism with community livelihood integration.

This concept proposes the development of Adventure Tourism Zones on closed, reclaimed, stabilised, or repurposed coal mine land. The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land into a safe, regulated, income-generating, tourism-oriented and community-linked adventure landscape centred around zip lines and high-rope courses, trekking and nature trails, mountain biking and cycling tracks, climbing walls and rappelling zones, ATV/off-road trail circuits where environmentally permissible, mine-view points and geological interpretation trails, controlled water-based recreation in safe mine voids if technically approved, camping and eco-lodges, and mine-heritage tourism and guided interpretation circuits.

Unlike heavy industrial reuse, adventure tourism uses existing post-mining landforms, can be integrated with eco-restoration, plantations, lakes, biodiversity zones and mine heritage, creates local employment, requires strong safety systems but comparatively moderate land disturbance, and can create a new public identity for closed mine areas.

Adventure tourism is particularly suited for selected closed coal mine areas because mines often have unusual landforms, elevated viewpoints, terraced slopes, quarry-like walls, haul roads, water-filled voids and industrial heritage features that can be repurposed into controlled adventure and eco-tourism assets after safety screening.

International precedents include Quarry Park Adventures in Rocklin, California (operates within the historic Big Gun Quarry using quarry walls, floor, rim, lake and waterfall) and Zip World Penrhyn Quarry in Wales (zip lines, quarry karts and quarry tours in a historic slate quarry).

  • Converts closed mine land into a tourism and recreation destination
  • Generates local jobs in guiding, operations, safety, hospitality and maintenance
  • Uses existing mine landforms: benches, slopes, roads, ridges and water bodies
  • Supports mine heritage interpretation, geological education and eco-tourism
  • Can be phased from low-risk nature trails to high-value adventure attractions
  • Revenue through tickets, guided tours, adventure activities, parking, food kiosks, events, camping and merchandise
  • Encourages local entrepreneurship through homestays, transport, food stalls, souvenirs and guide services
  • Reduces risk of abandoned land misuse by creating controlled public access
  • Supports ESG, CSR, just-transition and community development objectives
  • Offers strong potential for branding closed mine areas as productive post-mining landscapes

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Defining the procurement groundwork — what must be ready before the first RFP, and what the selected operator can take up post-award.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an adventure-tourism-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of adventure tourism / eco-tourism / recreational use.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, concession / lease rights, visitor liability, insurance requirements, public-access permission, tourism registration, GST applicability, commercial rights, user charges, revenue-sharing permissibility, forest / wildlife restrictions where applicable, water-body use permissions, construction permissions, advertising rights and local-body approvals.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including active mine areas, unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, unsafe water bodies and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, slope-stability screening, geotechnical screening, drainage review and water-body safety note where applicable.
  5. Environmental and contamination screening — Assess soil, water, dust, residual contamination, ecological sensitivity and environmental suitability for public tourism use.
  6. Preliminary safety and rescue framework — Prepare the minimum safety framework for activity selection, guide qualifications, rescue access, ambulance tie-up, weather closure, equipment standards, visitor eligibility, insurance, incident reporting and third-party safety audit requirements.
  7. Activity suitability assessment — Identify feasible activities such as trekking, cycling, viewpoints, rope course, zip line, camping, water recreation or mine-heritage trail based on site safety and terrain.
  8. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult local administration, tourism department, police, fire services, health department, panchayats, nearby communities, youth groups, SHGs and local entrepreneurs.
  9. Preliminary zoning and carrying-capacity plan — Prepare a basic layout showing entry area, visitor centre, activity zones, buffer zones, emergency routes, no-go areas, parking and broad visitor capacity.
  10. Market and revenue assessment — Estimate demand and possible revenue from entry tickets, adventure activities, guided tours, food kiosks, camping, parking, events and local transport.
  11. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, DBFOT or EPC + O&M.
  12. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, operation, safety compliance, equipment standards, local employment, guide training, kiosk allocation and environmental safeguards.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering adventure-tourism experience, safety record, technical capability, financial capacity, community integration, O&M plan and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / adventure-tourism operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, slope assessment, highwall assessment, foundation investigation and water-body assessment where applicable.
  2. Detailed environmental and safety planning — Prepare the environmental management plan, safety management plan, emergency-response plan, rescue plan, visitor-flow plan and final carrying-capacity assessment.
  3. Final master planning — Prepare the final adventure-tourism master plan showing activity zones, visitor circulation, no-go areas, buffer areas, emergency routes, service areas, parking and local enterprise zones.
  4. Detailed engineering and activity design — Prepare detailed designs for trails, zip lines, rope courses, climbing walls, viewing decks, camping areas, visitor centre, parking, utilities, platforms, anchors, towers and safety barriers.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — The selected concessionaire / operator shall prepare the detailed DPR, including detailed surveys, final master plan, detailed engineering, activity-wise safety design, financial model, statutory approvals plan, O&M plan, environmental management plan and implementation schedule, for approval by the mine owner before execution.
  6. Approvals, certification and insurance — Obtain final statutory permissions, operating approvals, safety certifications, equipment certifications, insurance cover and other clearances required before construction or operation.
  7. Procurement of equipment and systems — Procure certified adventure equipment, PPE, rescue equipment, first-aid systems, communication devices, CCTV, public-address system, access-control system and digital ticketing system.
  8. Construction and installation — Develop trails, platforms, towers, rope courses, safety nets, signage, barricades, lighting, visitor facilities, toilets, drinking water points, kiosks, parking and rest areas.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare activity-wise SOPs covering visitor briefing, eligibility checks, medical declaration, equipment use, rescue response, incident reporting, weather closure, inspection and maintenance.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train guides, instructors, safety marshals, rescue personnel, maintenance staff, visitor-service staff and local youth for adventure operations.
  11. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, food vendors, transport operators, souvenir sellers, homestay operators and local entrepreneurs for tourism-linked livelihood activities.
  12. Trial operation and safety audit — Conduct trial runs, mock drills, rescue exercises, equipment testing, third-party safety audits and owner / competent-authority review before opening the facility to the public.
  13. Branding and tourism linkage — Develop branding, marketing, online booking, signage, tourism-circuit linkage, school / college outreach and event promotion.
  14. Commissioning and public opening — Commission the adventure tourism zone after successful trial runs, safety clearance, staff readiness and visitor-management systems are in place.
  15. Regular operation and monitoring — Carry out routine O&M, equipment inspection, visitor management, incident reporting, environmental monitoring, safety audits and compliance reporting.
  16. Phased expansion — Expand activities and facilities in phases based on visitor demand, revenue performance, safety record, environmental compliance and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

A practical implementation sequence, preferred models and scaling logic — from a pilot interpretation trail to a regional mine-tourism and adventure ecosystem.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: The Hybrid Model is preferred because the mine owner can retain control over land, safety, access and mine-closure obligations while bringing in private adventure operators, tourism agencies, CSR support, local enterprises and community benefit obligations. The hybrid structure should combine owner-funded safety infrastructure + concession-based tourism operation + CSR/community livelihood participation.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be used where the project is mainly welfare-driven, low-risk and owner-controlled, especially for walking trails, viewing decks, interpretation areas and basic recreation zones.

Suggestable technical care: Geotechnical stability, slope safety, highwall protection, water-body safety, controlled access, emergency rescue, insurance, visitor carrying capacity, lighting, first-aid and environmental permissions must be verified before high-risk activities.

Recommended development logic: Start with low-risk elements such as boundary control, viewing decks, mine-heritage trail, walking / cycling routes, children’s adventure zone, visitor centre, toilets, parking and first-aid. Add zipline, rappelling, ATV, boating, camping and night tourism only after technical, safety, insurance and rescue-readiness clearance.

Final recommendation: Hybrid Model is the most suitable because adventure tourism requires strong public safety control along with professional tourism operation and local livelihood participation.
