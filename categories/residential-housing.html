RESIDENTIAL HOUSING PROJECT
Global Precedents
Colliery Housing Redevelopment, Ebbw Vale, Wales, UK
Residential Housing Projects on closed mine land propose the transformation of under-utilized mine land into planned residential developments supporting mine-affected communities, former workers, economically weaker households, rental housing demand, and regional settlement growth. Housing requires a higher level of safety assurance than most other post-mining uses — involving long-term occupancy, public infrastructure, utilities, children, elderly persons, and permanent community life.
PMAY-U 2.0 supports affordable housing with carpet area up to 60 sq m in metros and 90 sq m in non-metros, and houses with minimum 30 sq m carpet area with basic civic amenities. RERA 2016 requires registration before advertising, marketing, or selling eligible residential projects in planning areas. Site stability, ground settlement, contamination, legal land-use conversion, and long-term maintenance must be assessed before committing mine land for housing development.
Relevant Schemes & Standards

Policy Levers enabling Residential Housing development on Mine Land

🏠
MoHUA
Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana – Urban 2.0 (PMAY-U 2.0)
PMAY-U 2.0 supports affordable housing with carpet area up to 60 sq m in metros and 90 sq m in non-metros, with house value up to ₹45 lakh. It also supports houses with minimum 30 sq m carpet area with basic civic amenities and social infrastructure. Mine-area housing for EWS / LIG beneficiaries and former workers may qualify for PMAY-linked subsidy and grant support.
📋
MoHUA / GoI
Real Estate (Regulation & Development) Act — RERA 2016
RERA 2016 mandates registration of eligible residential projects before sale, advertisement, or marketing in planning areas. Mine-area housing projects developed for sale or lease must comply with RERA requirements, including registration, disclosure, escrow accounts, and buyer protection obligations. RERA compliance is a key legal prerequisite for residential development.
🏗️
BIS / MoHUA
National Building Code 2016 & Model Building Bye-Laws
The National Building Code 2016 sets structural, fire, accessibility, and building services standards for residential construction in India. Model Building Bye-Laws 2016 provide planning norms including FAR/FSI, setbacks, open-space ratio, height limits, and parking requirements applicable to mine-area housing layouts and apartment blocks.
🏡
MoRD
Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana – Gramin (PMAY-G)
PMAY-G supports rural affordable housing with a minimum size of 25 sq m including a dedicated hygienic cooking area. For mine areas in rural or peri-urban settings, PMAY-G can support housing for mine-displaced families, former workers, and local communities as part of just-transition and resettlement planning linked to mine closure.

Mine Closure Frameworks — R.E.C.L.A.I.M. & Just Transition
CCO's mine-closure frameworks recognize housing as a post-mining land use that supports community stability and just transition. The RECLAIM framework (Community-Centred Mine Closure and Just Transition Planning, 2025) supports worker housing, resettlement, and planned community development as part of structured mine-closure outcomes — especially for mine-affected communities.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix for mine-area housing, and indicative scale matrix — from a 0.5–1 ha pilot rental cluster to a 10 ha+ landmark mine-repurposing township.

This concept proposes the development of Residential Housing Projects within closed, reclaimed, or repurposed mine areas as part of a broader post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to transform under-utilized mine land into planned residential development supporting mine-affected communities, former workers, economically weaker households, rental housing demand, and regional settlement growth.

Residential housing as mine-area repurposing must be treated carefully because not all reclaimed mine land is suitable for habitation. Housing requires a higher level of safety assurance than other post-mining uses — it involves long-term occupancy, public infrastructure, utilities, children, elderly persons, and permanent community life. Site stability, ground settlement, contamination, drainage, mine-void safety, access, utilities, and legal land-use conversion must be assessed before committing the land for housing.

The proposed housing may include: affordable ownership housing; affordable rental housing; worker / transit accommodation; resettlement or rehabilitation housing; plotted housing; low-rise or mid-rise apartment blocks; mixed-income housing with EWS / LIG allocation; senior-friendly housing; community facilities; and internal infrastructure systems.

  • Productive reuse of closed mine land after safety and land-use clearance
  • Creation of planned housing stock for mine-affected communities and affordable housing beneficiaries
  • Support for just transition, rehabilitation, and regional settlement planning
  • Conversion of under-utilized mine land into serviced residential neighbourhoods
  • Local employment during construction, infrastructure, facility management, security, and maintenance
  • Opportunity to integrate green infrastructure, solar energy, rainwater harvesting, and climate-responsive housing
  • Potential to redevelop or upgrade existing mine colonies and townships
  • Support for local economic activity through neighbourhood retail, repair, transport, and community services
  • Monetization potential through sale, lease, rental, concession, or annuity where legally permitted
  • Alignment with ESG, CSR, mine closure, social transition, and inclusive regional development objectives

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Details the pre-RFP checklist including geotechnical safety, land-use conversion, RERA pathway, beneficiary demand assessment, utility feasibility, and what can be taken up post-award by the selected developer or concessionaire.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a residential-housing-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and mine-closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage, handover status, and whether the reclaimed / closed mine land can be considered for residential or township use.
  2. Land-use conversion and planning permissibility — Review whether residential use is permitted under the applicable master plan, development plan, local body regulations, revenue records, mining lease conditions, and post-closure land-use approvals.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, underground workings, subsidence-prone areas, mine voids, contaminated areas, unsafe water bodies, and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, slope-stability review, drainage assessment, contamination screening, and preliminary settlement-risk assessment.
  5. Environmental and public-health screening — Assess soil quality, groundwater quality, dust, mine-spoil conditions, acid mine drainage risk, flood risk, ecological sensitivity, and suitability for long-term residential occupancy.
  6. Housing demand and beneficiary assessment — Identify the target housing need such as affordable housing, rental housing, worker housing, mine-affected family housing, staff housing, rehabilitation housing, or mixed-income housing.
  7. Preliminary layout and density planning — Prepare a broad layout showing residential blocks / plots, internal roads, open spaces, community facilities, parking, green buffers, utility corridors, and emergency access.
  8. Infrastructure and serviceability assessment — Assess availability of approach road, water supply, sewerage, stormwater drainage, electricity, solid-waste management, street lighting, digital connectivity, and public transport linkage.
  9. Statutory approval pathway — Map key approvals such as land-use conversion, layout approval, building plan sanction, environmental clearance if applicable, fire NOC, water / sewerage / electricity NOCs, RERA registration where applicable, completion certificate, and occupancy certificate.
  10. Market, affordability, and revenue assessment — Estimate demand and revenue potential from sale, lease, rental housing, maintenance charges, parking, community retail, service charges, and possible affordable-housing or rental-housing support.
  11. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX, infrastructure cost, 5-year O&M estimate, and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, BOOT, or EPC + O&M / Facility Management.
  12. Draft RFP scope and public obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, approvals, RERA compliance, infrastructure development, estate management, affordability obligations, local employment, safety compliance, and long-term maintenance.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering housing-development experience, financial capacity, technical proposal, construction quality, statutory-compliance plan, affordability proposal, O&M plan, local-livelihood commitments, and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected developer / concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, soil testing, contamination testing, drainage study, flood-risk study, and subsidence / settlement assessment.
  2. Detailed environmental and risk planning — Prepare environmental management plan, construction safety plan, public-health safeguards, dust-control plan, drainage strategy, waste-management plan, and mine-risk mitigation measures.
  3. Final master planning and housing layout — Prepare the final residential master plan showing unit mix, residential blocks / plots, roads, open spaces, parking, utilities, community facilities, green buffers, and no-go areas.
  4. Detailed Project Report — The selected agency shall prepare the DPR including detailed surveys, final layout, engineering design, infrastructure plan, cost estimates, financial model, approval plan, O&M plan, phasing, and implementation schedule for mine-owner approval.
  5. Detailed architectural and engineering design — Prepare detailed building plans, structural design, foundation design, road design, water supply, sewerage, STP, stormwater drainage, electrical, street lighting, fire safety, landscaping, and digital infrastructure design.
  6. Statutory approvals and registrations — Obtain layout approval, building permit, fire NOC, utility NOCs, environmental approvals where applicable, RERA registration where applicable, and other permissions required before sale, construction, or occupancy.
  7. Financial closure and project structuring — Finalize project financing, escrow arrangements, sale / lease / rental structure, concession fee, revenue share, maintenance-charge framework, and affordability or beneficiary-allocation conditions.
  8. Construction and infrastructure development — Develop site grading, boundary, internal roads, drains, water supply, sewerage, STP, power systems, housing blocks, community facilities, parking, open spaces, and safety infrastructure.
  9. Quality control and compliance monitoring — Carry out material testing, structural checks, third-party quality audits, safety inspections, progress reporting, and compliance verification against approved drawings and specifications.
  10. Resident-allocation / sale / lease readiness — Finalize allotment process, sale or lease documentation, rental agreements, beneficiary verification if applicable, maintenance rules, resident handbook, and grievance-redressal system.
  11. Completion and occupancy approvals — Obtain completion certificate, occupancy certificate, fire-compliance confirmation, utility connection approvals, and handover clearances before allowing residential occupation.
  12. Estate management setup — Appoint or mobilize facility-management staff for security, housekeeping, landscaping, STP operation, waste management, common-area maintenance, parking, and resident services.
  13. Community and livelihood integration — Onboard local workers, maintenance teams, women's groups, service providers, small retailers, repair vendors, waste workers, and other local enterprises for housing-linked livelihood opportunities.
  14. Commissioning and handover — Commission the residential project after approvals, utility connections, safety checks, resident-service systems, and estate-management arrangements are in place.
  15. Regular operation and monitoring — Carry out routine O&M, maintenance-charge collection, resident grievance redressal, safety audits, utility monitoring, waste management, environmental monitoring, and compliance reporting.
  16. Phased expansion — Expand later phases only after confirming demand, ground performance, infrastructure capacity, sales / rental absorption, resident feedback, and mine-owner / statutory approval.

This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model; (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate; (3) DBFOT; (4) EPC + O&M / Facility Management.

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because housing projects must balance affordability, public purpose, safety, revenue, and long-term management. The hybrid mix should include owner land + private developer + public allocation policy + facility management / resident welfare system.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the owner wants strong control over beneficiary allocation, affordability, and long-term housing quality.

Technical care required: Land stability, contamination, land-use conversion, building approvals, RERA, fire safety, utilities, access, drainage, public transport, and O&M systems must be addressed before and during development.

Recommended development logic: Start with demand assessment and safe land identification. Develop worker housing, rental housing, or affordable housing in phases with community facilities and livelihood-linked services.
