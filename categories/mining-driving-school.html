Final Recommendations / Mining Driving School
Mining Driving School & HEMM Operator Training
Precedents
NCL CETI HEMM Training, Singrauli, India
Mining Driving Schools at closed coal mines can support skill development, employment generation, and industrial training by utilizing existing mine roads, workshops, and infrastructure for heavy vehicle and mining equipment operation training. These centres can provide hands-on training for dumpers, excavators, loaders, and other mining machinery, creating livelihood opportunities for local youth and strengthening the skilled workforce for mining and infrastructure sectors.
Aligned with CCO's A.R.T.H.A. green-finance framework and the R.E.C.L.A.I.M. post-mining land-use handbook, this repurposing option is especially suitable in coal-belt states where mine closure leaves large, stable land parcels adjacent to active mining and logistics corridors. A 5–15 ha site supports a regional HEMM and HMV training campus; 15–40 ha enables a full-scale mine-safety and skills academy.

PROJECT VISION

This pillar covers the project concept for a Mining Driving School and HEMM Operator Training Centre on closed coal mine land, the strategic rationale for this repurposing option, a site suitability and technical specification matrix, and indicative project scale configurations by land area.

This concept proposes the development of Mining Driving Schools, HEMM Operator Training Centres and Mine-Safety Skill Development Campuses on closed, reclaimed, stabilised or repurposed coal mine land, as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy.

The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land into a productive training, safety, livelihood and equipment-operation asset, centred on:

  • Heavy Earth Moving Machinery (HEMM) operator training — dumpers, shovels, excavators, loaders, dozers, graders, drill machines
  • Simulator-based HEMM, haul-road and emergency-response training
  • Light motor vehicle (LMV) and heavy motor vehicle (HMV) driver training
  • Mine traffic management, haul-road safety and defensive driving
  • Equipment inspection, pre-start checks and basic maintenance training
  • Trainer-of-trainers, assessor training and mine vocational refresher courses
  • Road-safety, vehicle-fitness, first-aid, fire-safety and emergency-response modules
  • Women and youth employment-oriented operator training
  • Digital classroom, virtual reality and augmented-reality training labs
  • Test tracks, haul-road loops, slope sections, reversing areas and visibility-risk zones
  • Workshop, maintenance bay, tyre-safety and battery/EV awareness areas
  • Administrative, hostel, canteen, security and digital learning-management facilities
  • Green buffer, dust control, stormwater and drainage corridors

Unlike heavy industrial reuse, a Mining Driving School uses large but low-intensity land parcels productively without requiring major manufacturing activity. It leverages existing mine roads, haul roads, workshops, yards, power and water supply, and industrial buffers — and can attract public, private and OEM participation through PPP, lease, concession, CSR and skill-development models. It aligns with India's mine-closure shift toward productive and community-oriented repurposing, as reflected in CCO's L.I.V.E.S, R.E.C.L.A.I.M and A.R.T.H.A frameworks.

A Mining Driving School is particularly suited for closed coal mine areas where haul roads, equipment yards, workshop areas, industrial access roads, buffer distances, power supply, water availability and training-relevant terrain may already exist or can be upgraded.

  • Converts closed mine land into productive safety, training and livelihood infrastructure
  • Supports skill development for mining, construction, road transport, logistics and infrastructure sectors
  • Reduces risk of abandoned land misuse, encroachment or unsafe occupation
  • Creates direct and indirect employment in training, equipment operation, maintenance, safety, hostel, canteen, security and facility management
  • Supports local youth, project-affected families, women candidates, ex-mine workers and contractor workforce
  • Can be developed in phases depending on land availability, equipment access and demand
  • Integrates classroom, simulator, workshop, test-track and live-equipment training into one campus
  • Supports safety, productivity, mechanisation and workforce-development objectives in SCMS Qualification Packs
  • Generates training fees, simulator charges, certification income, hostel fees, testing fees, OEM partnerships and service revenue
  • Can become a regional skill anchor for post-mining transition in coal-belt districts
  • MoRTH's IDTR and RDTC models provide a ready benchmark — 10-acre IDTR and 3-acre RDTC — adaptable to mine land of 5–40 ha

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

This pillar covers what should be ready before the first RFP is issued for a Mining Driving School or HEMM Operator Training Centre project, what can be taken up post-award, and the indicative implementation pathway from concept approval to operational commissioning.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Mining Driving School / HEMM Operator Training Centre as a mine-closure repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and mine-closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility for training, skill development, driver training, workshop, hostel and controlled equipment movement.
  2. Legal and statutory screening — Review land-use restrictions, concession rights, liability, insurance, user charges, revenue sharing, hostel operation, commercial training rights and compatibility with mine-closure obligations.
  3. DGMS and mine-training compliance review — Map the proposed centre with applicable DGMS requirements, especially the Mines Vocational Training Rules, 1966, which apply to mines covered under the Mines Act framework and provide the basis for mine vocational training.
  4. Skill and curriculum alignment — Identify relevant NSQF / Qualification Pack / National Occupational Standards for mining equipment roles such as Backhoe Loader Operator, Excavator Operator, Crawler Crane Operator, HEMM Mechanic and related mining-equipment occupations.
  5. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, abandoned workings, unsafe water bodies, restricted infrastructure and live mine-interface areas.
  6. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, slope-stability screening, soil-bearing assessment, drainage review, pavement feasibility and equipment-yard suitability note.
  7. Training-track and simulator feasibility — Assess whether the site can support classroom training, simulator training, HMV track, haul-road loop, gradient section, reversing bay, parking bay, equipment inspection area and workshop demonstration zone.
  8. MoRTH / driver-training interface review — Where LMV / HMV driver training or licence-linked training is proposed, review MoRTH-recognised formats such as IDTR, RDTC, DTC and ADTC, including accreditation and State Transport Authority interface where applicable. MoRTH information records IDTR and RDTC models, while ADTC accreditation may be granted by the State Transport Authority or authorised agency under CMVR-linked provisions.
  9. Environmental and contamination screening — Assess dust, soil contamination, water quality, residual pollution, noise, diesel emissions, vegetation, drainage discharge and suitability for trainee and public use.
  10. Training-demand and catchment assessment — Estimate demand from nearby mines, MDOs, contractors, transporters, construction agencies, ITIs, polytechnics, local youth, women candidates, project-affected families and ex-mine workers.
  11. Preliminary campus zoning and facility plan — Prepare a basic layout covering entry gate, admin block, classrooms, simulator room, training track, haul-road loop, equipment yard, workshop, hostel, canteen, medical room, parking, utilities, green buffer and emergency route.
  12. Cost, revenue and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX, simulator cost, equipment cost, 5-year O&M estimate and revenue logic, then shortlist models such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, BOOT or EPC + O&M.
  13. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, training operations, simulator operation, trainer qualification, DGMS-aligned safety training, NSQF/QP alignment, local trainee quota, women/youth inclusion, equipment standards, hostel operation and reporting.
  14. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering mining-training experience, HEMM / HMV training capability, simulator capability, safety record, trainer strength, financial capacity, local livelihood plan, O&M plan and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / training operator / OEM partner can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, slope assessment, pavement investigation, drainage design inputs and safety-zone validation.
  2. Final regulatory and compliance mapping — Prepare a compliance matrix covering DGMS mine-vocational training requirements, applicable State Transport requirements, CMVR / ADTC interface where relevant, fire safety, hostel permissions, labour compliance and insurance.
  3. Detailed Project Report preparation — The selected operator shall prepare the DPR covering final master plan, engineering design, training model, simulator plan, equipment plan, financial model, statutory approvals plan, O&M plan, safety plan and implementation schedule.
  4. Final campus master planning — Prepare the final layout for classrooms, simulator centre, training tracks, haul-road loop, reversing bay, slope section, equipment yard, workshop, hostel, canteen, parking, emergency access and green buffer.
  5. Curriculum and certification framework — Develop course modules aligned with DGMS vocational training needs, NSQF / QP standards, mine-road safety, HEMM operation, HMV training where applicable, refresher training and contractor-driver induction.
  6. Detailed engineering and safety design — Prepare detailed designs for track pavement, turning radii, gradients, signage, barriers, lighting, workshop floors, simulator rooms, drainage, fire safety, CCTV, access control and emergency assembly points.
  7. Approvals, insurance and operating permissions — Obtain required permissions, fire NOC, training-related approvals, insurance coverage, vehicle / equipment permissions, hostel approvals and State Transport interface approvals where licence-linked driver training is proposed.
  8. Procurement of simulators, equipment and systems — Procure HEMM simulators, driver-training vehicles, PPE, communication systems, CCTV, biometric attendance, learning management system, first-aid equipment, workshop tools and training aids.
  9. Construction and installation — Develop classrooms, simulator lab, training track, haul-road loop, equipment yard, workshop, hostel, canteen, utilities, signage, fencing, drainage, lighting and digital monitoring systems.
  10. Trainer recruitment and capacity building — Recruit qualified trainers, simulator instructors, HEMM supervisors, safety trainers, first-aid trainers, workshop instructors, hostel staff and administrative staff, followed by trainer-of-trainers orientation.
  11. SOPs and safety management system — Prepare SOPs for trainee induction, simulator training, live-equipment movement, pre-start inspection, track operation, fatigue management, emergency response, incident reporting and equipment maintenance.
  12. Local livelihood integration — Implement local trainee quota, women/youth training provisions, project-affected-family preference, local vendor participation, canteen / housekeeping contracts and placement-linkage support.
  13. Trial training and safety audit — Conduct dry runs, simulator calibration, trial batches, mock emergency drills, equipment checks, third-party safety audit and owner review before full-scale operation.
  14. Commissioning and regular operation — Start phased training operations with attendance tracking, assessment records, safety reporting, O&M monitoring, trainee feedback, placement records and periodic compliance reporting.
  15. Performance review and phased expansion — Expand additional modules such as advanced HEMM operation, EV / battery safety, autonomous equipment awareness, mine-rescue orientation, maintenance training and regional skill partnerships based on demand and safety performance.

M
MONITORING GUIDANCE

This pillar covers the recommended way forward and preferred implementation models for a Mining Driving School on closed coal mine land, key references from MoC, DGMS, SCMS and MoRTH, and an abbreviations glossary.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (2) Hybrid, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is preferred because HEMM and mine-driving training require equipment, simulators, trainers, certification and industry linkage. The mine owner can retain land control while the operator manages training, equipment and placement.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the owner wants strong control over trainee eligibility, local youth inclusion, fees and training standards.

Suggestable technical care: Training track safety, simulator facilities, HEMM availability, trainer certification, emergency response, insurance, trainee selection, placement linkage and maintenance must be planned.

Recommended development logic: Start with driving track, simulator lab, safety classroom and basic equipment operation courses. Scale to regional HEMM academy only after trainee demand and industry absorption are proven.
