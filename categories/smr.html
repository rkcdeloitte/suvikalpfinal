Small Modular Reactor (SMR)
Global Precedent
Coal-to-Nuclear Repowering — U.S. Department of Energy Studies, 2024
Small Modular Reactors (SMR) on closed or reclaimed coal mine land represent a highly selective but strategically significant clean-energy repurposing option. Where site conditions satisfy nuclear siting, geotechnical stability, cooling, grid, security and emergency-planning requirements, a closed mine or retired coal-industrial area can be transformed into a firm low-carbon power campus — replacing coal-based generation with advanced nuclear infrastructure that provides round-the-clock electricity, industrial heat, and grid stability.
This option is not a universal reuse strategy — it is nuclear-regulated, capital-intensive, security-sensitive and long-duration. It is most relevant where a retired coal-power or mine-power site offers existing transmission, access, cooling infrastructure, a trained energy workforce, and brownfield land that satisfies atomic-energy, radiation-safety and environmental requirements. Alignment with India's Nuclear Energy Mission, the Bharat Small Modular Reactor (BSMR) programme, and the CCO mine-repurposing framework makes this a visible just-transition flagship for select sites.

PROJECT VISION

This pillar covers the project concept, strategic rationale, critical siting requirements, site suitability assessment matrix, and indicative scale matrix for SMR development on closed mine or retired coal-industrial land. Nuclear safety, geological stability, regulatory approval and authorized institutional capacity are the core foundations.

This concept proposes the development of a Small Modular Reactor (SMR) based clean-energy campus within or around a closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine area as part of a broader post-mining land-use, energy-transition, industrial-repowering and regional economic diversification strategy.

The objective is to transform suitable closed mine land, retired coal-industrial land, associated power-plant areas, workshops, substations, transmission corridors, water infrastructure, rail/road logistics and controlled industrial buffers into a nuclear-energy repurposing asset that supports firm low-carbon power, grid reliability, industrial heat, clean hydrogen, data-centre power, desalination/water treatment where feasible, skilled employment, long-term concession revenue and a visible transition from coal-based extraction to advanced clean-energy infrastructure.

This is a highly selective, regulator-led repurposing option — not a universal one. A closed coal mine is suitable for an SMR project only where it can satisfy critical requirements relating to nuclear siting acceptability; geotechnical stability, subsidence control and seismic suitability; adequate land area, controlled boundary and emergency-planning feasibility; cooling-water availability or technically acceptable dry/hybrid cooling; grid evacuation; access for heavy equipment and nuclear-grade components; compliance with atomic-energy, radiation-safety, nuclear-security and environmental regulations; spent-fuel, radioactive-waste and decommissioning arrangements; environmental clearance, public consultation and social acceptance; and long-term institutional capability through authorized nuclear entities and licensed operators.

SMRs are increasingly relevant because large renewable-energy deployment requires firm, dispatchable and low-carbon power. Unlike solar and wind, SMRs can provide high-capacity-factor electricity and may support industrial decarbonisation, round-the-clock power, process heat and grid stability.

An SMR project can be a strong repurposing option where a closed coal mine/coal-industrial site offers a combination of:

  • Existing energy-sector land with controlled access and industrial buffers
  • Retired coal-power or mine-power infrastructure with potential for reuse
  • High-voltage transmission lines, switchyards or substations
  • Industrial roads, rail connectivity and heavy-equipment logistics
  • Access to cooling water, treated wastewater, mine water or dry-cooling feasibility
  • Local workforce familiar with energy, mechanical, electrical, maintenance and safety systems
  • Regional need for firm low-carbon power to complement variable renewables
  • Potential industrial demand from steel, cement, chemicals, data centres, hydrogen or mining-related clusters
  • Opportunity to replace lost coal-sector employment with advanced energy-sector jobs
  • Potential to reuse selected non-contaminated industrial land while avoiding unstable mine voids
  • Strong alignment with energy transition, just transition and clean firm power planning

Research and policy literature supports the possibility of repowering retired coal-energy sites with advanced nuclear technologies where siting, safety and regulatory conditions are satisfied. Coal-to-nuclear studies highlight that existing transmission, cooling systems, roads, workforce and community identity as an energy-producing region may reduce some development barriers. However, closed mines introduce additional concerns such as subsidence, underground voids, slope instability, mine-water contamination, seismicity, public trust and emergency-planning constraints.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

This pillar identifies the critical owner-side preparatory actions that must be completed before issuing the first RFP, and the principal post-award activities to be undertaken by the selected developer, concessionaire or EPC contractor. Nuclear siting, regulatory pathway, mine-safety mapping and geotechnical screening are absolute pre-requisites before any bid process can begin.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an SMR-based mine-repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, mine-closure and permissibility confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, nuclear-energy land-use permissibility, leaseability and restrictions under applicable law.
  2. Mine-safety and no-go zone mapping — Map pits, highwalls, underground workings, shafts, galleries, subsidence zones, unstable dumps, unsafe water bodies and restricted mine infrastructure.
  3. Preliminary nuclear siting screening — Assess site suitability for nuclear use, including land area, stability, flood safety, seismic context, population interface, access, cooling, grid and emergency planning.
  4. Geotechnical, seismic and subsidence screening — Prepare initial studies on foundation condition, old mine workings, settlement, slope stability, seismic hazard and long-term ground reliability.
  5. Cooling-water and water-quality assessment — Identify river, reservoir, treated wastewater, mine-water treatment, cooling tower, dry-cooling or hybrid-cooling feasibility and environmental implications.
  6. Grid connectivity and evacuation assessment — Confirm nearest substation, transmission capacity, voltage level, grid reliability, system studies and scope for clean firm power injection.
  7. Environmental, contamination and land-risk screening — Assess coal dust, hydrocarbon contamination, heavy metals, acid mine drainage, soil quality, groundwater, biodiversity, air, water and noise baseline.
  8. Population, emergency and social-acceptance screening — Identify nearby settlements, evacuation routes, hospitals, district emergency capacity, public perception, community concerns and communication strategy.
  9. Nuclear regulatory and legal pathway note — Identify applicable atomic-energy, nuclear safety, radiation protection, environmental, security, safeguards, fuel, waste and liability requirements.
  10. Market, offtake and revenue assessment — Assess potential revenue through grid PPA, capacity payments, industrial supply, data-centre power, hydrogen, process heat, regulated tariff or government-backed procurement.
  11. Implementation model and risk-allocation decision — Select preferred model (Hybrid, Concession/LDO, DBFOT, BOOT or EPC+O&M) based on legal feasibility, CAPEX, nuclear eligibility, offtake and risk appetite.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — Prepare bidder scope covering siting studies, regulatory pathway, technology qualification, design, finance, construction, operation, security, emergency planning, community engagement, local employment, decommissioning and handback obligations.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / developer / EPC contractor / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation and nuclear siting studies — Geotechnical drilling, seismic study, hydrology, flood modelling, subsidence modelling, environmental baseline, population assessment and emergency-planning review.
  2. Detailed mine-stability and contamination studies — Subsurface mine-void assessment, ground-improvement plan, contamination remediation plan, mine-water treatment plan and long-term land-stability assurance.
  3. Technology selection and licensing preparation — Finalise approved SMR technology, reactor class, safety case approach, licensing documents, regulator engagement plan and design-basis assumptions.
  4. Statutory approvals and clearances — Obtain nuclear siting approval, construction approval, environmental clearance, water permission, grid connectivity, security approvals, radiation-safety permissions and other state/central approvals.
  5. Final master planning and engineering design — Finalise reactor area, turbine island, cooling system, switchyard, security perimeter, access routes, emergency facilities, administration, training centre and support infrastructure.
  6. Financial closure and offtake arrangement — Finalise equity, debt, public support, regulated tariff, PPA, capacity contract, industrial offtake, grid-service arrangement or government-backed procurement.
  7. Procurement and construction preparation — Procure nuclear-grade components, civil packages, turbines, generators, transformers, cooling systems, control systems, safety systems, security equipment and balance-of-plant systems.
  8. Construction of nuclear, civil, grid and support works — Construct foundations, reactor building, turbine island, cooling systems, switchyard, access roads, water systems, security systems, emergency facilities and support buildings.
  9. Installation of control, electrical and safety systems — Install instrumentation, control systems, protection, communications, cybersecurity systems, emergency systems, monitoring systems and grid-dispatch interface.
  10. Safety systems, operating procedures and staffing — Prepare operating manuals, emergency plans, security plans, radiation-protection procedures, O&M manuals, training programmes and licensed staff deployment.
  11. Local livelihood and vendor integration — Engage eligible local workers, service providers, transport operators, security staff, maintenance support, emergency-response trainees and technical apprentices under strict qualification and safety requirements.
  12. Testing, commissioning and regulatory readiness — Equipment testing, system validation, emergency drills, regulator inspections, grid synchronisation tests and commissioning activities as permitted by the regulator.
  13. Commercial operation and compliance monitoring — Begin operation only after statutory readiness; continue nuclear safety monitoring, radiation monitoring, environmental compliance, security, maintenance, emergency preparedness and annual performance review.
  14. Phased expansion or optimisation — Add future modules, industrial integration, hydrogen production, data-centre linkage or additional grid services only after stable operation, regulator approval, safety validation and owner/authority approval.
M
MONITORING GUIDANCE

This pillar presents the indicative implementation pathway, recommended way forward with preferred models, key references, and glossary of abbreviations for SMR mine-area repurposing projects. The Hybrid model is recommended as the primary approach, followed by Concession/LDO and DBFOT, reflecting the unique nuclear safety, regulatory and institutional requirements of this category.

The following implementation pathway presents a practical high-level sequence for advancing the proposed repurposing concept from initial approval through to project execution and long-term operation. It outlines the broad stages of concept approval, preliminary studies, model selection, bid preparation, procurement, detailed design, statutory approvals, construction, commissioning, and ongoing operation. This pathway is indicative and should be adapted to reflect the specific complexity, regulatory requirements, financing structure, and site conditions applicable to the project.

  1. Concept approval and owner-side structuring
  2. Screening of closed mine / coal-industrial sites for SMR suitability
  3. Land, closure-plan, mine-safety, geotechnical, seismic, flood, cooling, grid and access studies
  4. Preliminary nuclear siting and legal-pathway screening
  5. Environmental, contamination and social-acceptance screening
  6. Market sounding with authorized nuclear entities, utilities, industrial offtakers, technology providers, state agencies and regulators
  7. Concept planning and phasing strategy
  8. Identification of owner-side enabling scope (land, access, grid, water, safety works and community engagement)
  9. Selection of preferred implementation model
  10. Preparation of pre-feasibility report, siting terms, bid documents and concession / partnership structure
  11. Issuance of RFQ / RFP only where legally permissible
  12. Selection of authorized concessionaire / developer / nuclear partner
  13. Detailed siting studies, licensing, clearances, grid connectivity and financial closure
  14. Detailed design, construction and nuclear-grade installation
  15. Testing, commissioning, regulator approvals, grid synchronisation and trial operation
  16. Commercial operation, safety monitoring, security, compliance reporting and phased expansion where feasible

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models (in order of suitability):

  1. Hybrid
  2. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate
  3. DBFOT

Why Hybrid is preferred: An SMR project in a closed mine area requires a combination of mine-owner land control, public oversight, authorized nuclear technical capability, regulatory approval, long-term safety governance, grid integration and community transition planning. The mine owner can provide land, closure-linkage, access, enabling infrastructure and local transition support, while an authorized nuclear entity / utility / qualified developer carries nuclear design, licensing, construction, operation and compliance responsibility.

Concession / LDO is also suitable where the mine owner wants to retain land control but allow an authorized nuclear developer or utility to invest, build, operate and commercialise the SMR under strict concession conditions.

DBFOT is suitable for larger SMR parks where long-term concession structuring, private/public finance, industrial offtake, grid integration and eventual transfer are legally and institutionally feasible.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be used only where the owner itself is an authorized nuclear entity or where a public-sector nuclear owner develops the project with specialist operations and facility-management support.

Recommended knockout screening: Apply knockout criteria first — legal permissibility, authorized nuclear partner, stable land, mine-hazard separation, cooling feasibility, grid evacuation, emergency-planning feasibility, environmental acceptability and community acceptance. Only passing sites should move to nuclear pre-feasibility, regulator engagement, market sounding and implementation structuring.
