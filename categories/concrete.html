Concrete Plants / RMC & Precast Products
Precedents
RDC Concrete RMC Plant, India

Concrete plants are industrial facilities designed for the production of ready-mix concrete, supporting construction activities such as roads, buildings, and infrastructure projects. In the context of post-mining landscapes, reclaimed mine areas provide a practical and strategic location for establishing such plants, as they offer large, previously disturbed land parcels along with access to existing transport networks, thereby enabling efficient material production and supply for regional development.

The establishment of concrete plants in these areas can generate steady employment and livelihood opportunities for local communities, especially those previously dependent on mining activities, by creating jobs in operations, transportation, maintenance, and support services. However, to ensure successful and sustainable operation, it is important to address challenges such as environmental management, dust and emission control, proper waste handling, and the development of safe and efficient infrastructure, while maintaining compliance with regulatory standards.

PROJECT VISION

Project overview, strategic rationale, site suitability assessment, and indicative concrete plant scale and configuration matrix for closed coal mine areas.

  1. What: Concrete plants — ready-mix concrete (RMC), captive batching, and precast products — established on reclaimed or suitable closed coal mine land to serve local infrastructure demand
  2. Why it fits mine land: Closed mine sites often have existing haul roads, weighbridges, power supply, water infrastructure, workshops, and large surface yards directly adaptable for batching plant operation
  3. Dispatch advantage: RMC requires delivery within 15–25 km of plant; mine sites near towns, highways, and infrastructure corridors have a natural locational advantage
  4. Circular economy potential: Fly ash and slag from adjacent thermal plants as supplementary cementitious materials; locally available aggregates and manufactured sand; C&D waste recycling where permitted
  5. National alignment: Supports PMGSY, PMAY, Smart Cities, industrial corridor, and renewable energy infrastructure demand; mine-closure civil works (drains, roads, retaining walls, culverts) can be captive customers
  6. Who benefits: Former mine workers reskilled in batching, quality testing, and dispatch; local contractors and suppliers; mine owner through asset monetisation and ESG compliance
  1. Land parcels: Surface yards, reclaimed land, workshop areas, haul-road access, batching area, aggregate stockyard, cement silo area, testing lab, vehicle circulation, and curing yard — all directly reusable
  2. Haul roads: Internal haul roads and highway access — critical for transit-mixer dispatch and raw material delivery; mine sites often have these at commercial standard
  3. Weighbridge and logistics: Existing weighbridges, vehicle parking, and dispatch offices — directly usable for raw material receipt and commercial dispatch control
  4. Water infrastructure: Mine water, treated water, borewells, surface ponds — concrete plants require water for batching, washing, curing, and dust suppression
  5. Power supply: Existing substations and transformers — required for batching plant, conveyors, mixers, pumps, compressors, and lighting; often available at mine sites
  6. Workshops and buildings: Sheds, offices, garages, control rooms — reusable for laboratory, stores, maintenance, and training without new construction
  7. Labour pool: Former mine workers available for reskilling in batching operations, mechanical maintenance, quality testing, and dispatch — reducing recruitment and training costs
  8. Material advantages: Fly ash and slag as cementitious materials; local aggregates, manufactured sand, or mine overburden — subject to testing; C&D recycling under C&D Waste Management Rules 2016

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP preparation checklist, post-award activity scope, and indicative implementation pathway for concrete plant development on mine closure sites.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

  1. Internal approval and project authorisation
  2. Confirmation of mine closure status, mine-closure plan position, and post-mining land-use permissibility
  3. Clear identification of usable land parcel, access area, truck circulation, stockyard, plant area, and utility interface
  4. Project concept intimated to CCO or relevant controlling/approving authority where applicable
  5. Preliminary geotechnical and subsidence screening for plant foundations, silos, stockyards, weighbridge, and truck movement
  6. Preliminary water availability and quality assessment
  7. Preliminary electrical load and power-supply assessment
  8. Identification of intended use case: captive mine-closure works, commercial RMC, precast products, infrastructure supply, C&D/recycled aggregate, or hybrid model
  9. Preliminary market demand study covering nearby public works, road works, industrial projects, housing, railway, renewable-energy foundations, and local construction demand
  10. Preliminary technology screening: mobile batching, stationary RMC, wet mix, dry mix, precast line, block plant, or integrated circular cluster
  11. Preliminary environmental control philosophy: fugitive dust, cement-silo filters, covered conveyors, aggregate stockpile control, wind barriers, water sprinkling, wheel-wash, wash-water recycling, noise control
  12. Screening of statutory triggers: SPCB consent, factory registration, local authority approval, fire, electrical inspectorate, water extraction/use permission, labour, weights and measures, land-use change
  13. Market sounding with batching plant manufacturers, RMC operators, precast companies, aggregate suppliers, cement suppliers, and infrastructure contractors
  14. Decision on whether RFP should be for feasibility/DPR first, EPC plant supply, O&M operator selection, developer concession, or lease-based development

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

  1. Detailed topographic survey, geotechnical investigation, and foundation design
  2. Detailed plant layout, traffic circulation, stormwater drainage, and environmental control design
  3. Detailed mix design, material testing, aggregate gradation, cementitious material selection, and admixture trials
  4. Testing of mine overburden, local aggregates, manufactured sand, recycled aggregate, fly ash, slag, or C&D material where proposed
  5. Detailed batching plant engineering, silos, conveyors, mixer selection, dust extraction, water systems, and automation
  6. Detailed precast / paver / block / pipe / panel product-line design where applicable
  7. Detailed laboratory design and quality-control plan aligned with IS 4925:2023 and RMC certification requirements
  8. Detailed SPCB consent documentation and environmental management plan
  9. Detailed electrical design, transformer sizing, protection, backup power, and metering
  10. Vendor-specific warranty, performance guarantee, production guarantee, calibration protocol, spare parts, and maintenance plan

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward recommendations, key references, and abbreviations for concrete plant and RMC development on mine closure sites.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) EPC + O&M / Facility Management, (2) DBFOT, (3) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (4) Hybrid

Why preferred: EPC + O&M is preferred where the plant supports mine-closure works and owner-led infrastructure projects. DBFOT or lease models are preferred where private financing, commercial sales and market risk transfer are required.

Secondary option: Hybrid may be used where the owner provides land and anchor demand while a private operator brings batching, precast and market capability.

Suggestable technical care: Material sourcing, aggregate quality, fly ash / slag use, water recycling, dust suppression, wash-water treatment, traffic, noise, certification and product testing must be ensured.

Recommended development logic: Start with a demand and material study for RMC, pavers, blocks, kerbs, drains and mine-closure materials. Scale into precast and recycled aggregate products only where codes, testing and market demand support it.

Final recommendation: EPC + O&M is best for owner-led closure works; DBFOT / Lease-Develop-Operate is better for commercial precast clusters.
