Underground Parks
Global Precedents
Wieliczka Royal Salt Mine Park, Kraków, Poland
Underground Parks proposes the conversion of technically safe closed coal mine galleries, inclines, shafts, drifts, and stable underground chambers into a controlled public-use destination supporting mine heritage tourism, guided underground routes, interpretation centres, light-and-sound shows, underground museums, climate-stable recreation, and local livelihood enterprises. This model can convert closed mine infrastructure into a productive tourism and heritage asset while preserving mining history and community identity.
Unlike conventional surface repurposing, Underground Parks can benefit from existing mine galleries and historic infrastructure, unique visitor experience value, and integration with above-ground eco-parks, amphitheatres, museums, and local cultural circuits. However, closed coal mines require far stricter safety screening than salt or limestone mines — covering methane, spontaneous heating, unstable roof strata, water inflow, and ventilation. Underground Park development should proceed only after comprehensive safety, geotechnical, gas, ventilation, fire, water, access, and emergency-response validation by competent authorities.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix for closed mine underground-space conversion, and indicative scale matrix — from a 1–5 ha pilot heritage route to a 100 ha+ regional mine heritage and underground tourism park.

This concept proposes the development of Underground Parks in suitable closed, abandoned, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine areas as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert technically safe underground mine workings, galleries, inclines, shafts, drifts, chambers, or stable underground voids into a controlled public-use destination supporting underground mine heritage tourism, guided walking routes, interpretation centres, mining museums, light-and-sound shows, climate-stable recreation, art and culture spaces, skill development, and local tourism-based livelihoods.

Unlike conventional surface-only mine repurposing, Underground Parks benefit from existing underground mine galleries and historic mine infrastructure, unique visitor experience value, relatively stable underground temperatures, strong heritage and education branding potential, lower requirement for fresh surface land acquisition, and opportunity to preserve mining history and community identity.

This shall be treated as a site-specific, safety-dependent proposal. Closed coal mines require far stricter technical screening than salt or limestone mines because coal mines may involve methane, spontaneous heating risk, unstable roof strata, water inflow, old workings, subsidence, low oxygen pockets, and ventilation challenges. Underground Park development should be permitted only after comprehensive safety, geotechnical, ventilation, gas, fire, water, access, and emergency-response validation by competent mine-safety authorities.

  • Converts closed mine infrastructure into a productive tourism and heritage asset
  • Preserves mining history, mining culture, and local industrial identity for future generations
  • Creates non-mining employment for local youth, ex-miners, women's groups, guides, security staff, hospitality workers, and local entrepreneurs
  • Supports just transition for coal-dependent communities through tourism and cultural economy
  • Encourages local MSMEs through food stalls, craft shops, transport services, homestays, souvenirs, interpretation services, and visitor support
  • Can use existing mine access roads, administrative buildings, workshops, yards, and power infrastructure where safe
  • Enables phased development from small guided heritage routes to larger underground recreation parks
  • Generates revenue through ticketing, guided tours, parking, events, rentals, concessions, merchandise, food courts, training, and PPP partnerships
  • Can integrate with above-ground eco-parks, solar parks, museums, amphitheatres, and local cultural circuits
  • Supports environmental education, mine-safety awareness, geology education, and climate-resilient recreation
  • Improves public image of closed mine sites by converting unsafe abandoned areas into managed destinations
  • UNESCO recognition of Wieliczka & Bochnia demonstrates long-term heritage tourism value of preserved underground mining landscapes

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Details the pre-RFP checklist including underground mine-data compilation, safety and geotechnical screening, gas and ventilation assessment, fire-risk assessment, mine-water and drainage review, emergency evacuation planning, visitor-route identification, and heritage and tourism demand assessment.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an Underground Park as a mine-closure repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, lease status, closure status, Mine Closure Plan linkage, and permissibility of underground tourism / heritage / recreational use as post-mining land use.
  2. Legal, safety and liability screening — Review land-use restrictions, visitor liability, insurance, concession rights, user charges, revenue sharing, and applicability of DGMS / Coal Mines Regulations requirements for any underground access. DGMS lists the Coal Mines Regulations, 2017 among the applicable mine-safety statutes.
  3. Underground safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe galleries, shafts, inclines, goaf areas, sealed panels, fire-affected zones, waterlogged workings, methane-prone zones, unsupported roof areas, and subsidence-prone zones.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare underground mine-plan review, roof and sidewall stability screening, ventilation feasibility, gas baseline, mine-water condition, access safety, and emergency-exit feasibility.
  5. Environmental and public-use screening — Assess mine water, dust, residual contamination, unstable dumps, ecological sensitivity, drainage, and surface safety before allowing public tourism use.
  6. Activity and route suitability assessment — Identify feasible uses such as guided mine trail, underground museum, interpretation gallery, light-and-sound show, geology gallery, mine-safety education route, or restricted heritage walk.
  7. Stakeholder consultation — Consult district administration, DGMS / mine-safety authorities, tourism department, fire services, police, health department, panchayats, nearby communities, ex-miners, SHGs, and local entrepreneurs.
  8. Preliminary zoning and carrying-capacity plan — Prepare a concept layout showing entry portal, visitor centre, underground route, no-go zones, ventilation points, emergency exits, refuge / assembly areas, parking, amenities, and visitor capacity.
  9. Market and revenue assessment — Estimate demand and revenue from entry tickets, guided tours, museum fees, school visits, food kiosks, souvenir stalls, parking, events, and local transport.
  10. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates for stabilisation, ventilation, lighting, monitoring, emergency systems, visitor facilities, and select Hybrid / Concession / DBFOT / EPC + O&M model.
  11. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering detailed survey, DPR, stabilisation, ventilation, lighting, gas monitoring, emergency response, visitor management, local employment, guide training, and environmental safeguards.
  12. Bid evaluation framework — Define criteria covering underground safety experience, tourism operation capability, technical capacity, financial strength, emergency-management plan, local livelihood integration, O&M plan, and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / Underground Park operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed underground survey, mine-plan validation, drone / laser scanning where feasible, roof stability assessment, gas survey, ventilation assessment, water assessment, and access-condition review.
  2. Detailed safety and environmental planning — Prepare the safety management plan, ventilation plan, gas-monitoring plan, fire and emergency-response plan, evacuation plan, environmental management plan, and final carrying-capacity assessment.
  3. Final master planning — Prepare the final Underground Park master plan showing visitor route, interpretation zones, no-go areas, emergency exits, ventilation points, service areas, control room, surface facilities, and local enterprise zones.
  4. Detailed engineering and DPR — Prepare the DPR with ground-support design, route design, lighting, electrical safety, ventilation, drainage, communication, CCTV, visitor amenities, financial model, approval plan, O&M plan, and implementation schedule.
  5. Approvals, certification and insurance — Obtain applicable mine-safety approvals, local permissions, fire NOC, electrical safety clearance, tourism / local body approvals, public liability insurance, and other statutory clearances before construction or public entry.
  6. Procurement of systems and equipment — Procure certified lighting, ventilation equipment, gas detectors, communication systems, CCTV, emergency alarms, PPE, rescue equipment, first-aid systems, signage, barricades, and digital ticketing systems.
  7. Stabilisation and construction works — Carry out roof / sidewall support, floor levelling, drainage, portal improvement, barricading of unsafe zones, lighting installation, ventilation works, visitor centre, toilets, parking, kiosks, and rest areas.
  8. Preparation of SOPs and training — Prepare SOPs for visitor briefing, route control, gas monitoring, emergency evacuation, weather closure, incident reporting, inspection, maintenance, and train guides, safety marshals, rescue staff, and local youth.
  9. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, ex-miners, guides, food vendors, souvenir sellers, transport operators, photographers, and local entrepreneurs for tourism-linked livelihood activities.
  10. Trial operation and safety audit — Conduct trial walks, mock evacuation drills, gas-monitoring checks, ventilation tests, equipment testing, and third-party safety audit before opening to the public.
  11. Branding, commissioning and public opening — Launch branding, online booking, signage, tourism-circuit linkage, school / college outreach, and open the Underground Park only after safety clearance and staff readiness.
  12. Regular operation, monitoring and phased expansion — Carry out routine O&M, roof / gas / ventilation monitoring, visitor management, incident reporting, environmental monitoring, safety audits, and expand phases only after safety and demand validation.

M
MONITORING GUIDANCE

Covers the 18-step implementation pathway, preferred models (Hybrid, Concession/LDO, DBFOT), mandatory safety infrastructure requirements, key references from UNESCO, NPS, ILO, Ministry of Coal, and domain abbreviations.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order:

  1. Hybrid Model — combines mine-owner controlled underground safety + tourism operator + mine-safety expert + local guide / ex-miner participation; most suitable for balancing tourism development, local livelihood, mine-closure compliance, ESG outcomes, and long-term sustainable heritage land reuse
  2. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate — owner retains land while operator manages Underground Park; strong model for long-term livelihood through guide services, food courts, craft stalls, transport, maintenance, ticketing, events, and tourism-linked enterprises
  3. DBFOT — suitable for large structured PPP projects needing private finance and professional operation with strong safety and performance obligations

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable for smaller owner-controlled underground heritage routes and welfare-driven education projects.

Mandatory safety requirements: Roof stability, ventilation, methane monitoring, fire suppression, flooding prevention, emergency exits, communication, lighting, visitor control, rescue plan, and public liability insurance must be mandatory before public opening.

Recommended development logic: Begin with a pilot underground heritage route only in stable, ventilated, and accessible areas with minimal gas risk. Expand to galleries, light-and-sound, education zones, and controlled events after safety performance is proven over an initial operating period.
