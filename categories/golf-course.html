GOLF COURSE & ECO-RECREATION
Precedents
Kalhaar Blues and Greens in Ahmedabad, Gujarat
Golf Course & Eco-Recreation converts closed mine land into a managed green recreational, sports, and tourism asset — ranging from driving ranges and par-3 facilities to full 18-hole courses, eco-golf parks, and hybrid resort-linked destinations. Mine sites offer large contiguous land parcels, varied topography, internal roads, water bodies, and township infrastructure that can support well-designed golf development.
Suitable for coal mine sites near urban centres, industrial townships, and tourism circuits across coal-bearing states. Aligned with the Ministry of Coal's 2025 mine closure guidelines and CCO frameworks A.R.T.H.A., L.I.V.E.S. and R.E.C.L.A.I.M. — combining landscape rehabilitation, livelihood, and sports tourism.

PROJECT VISION

Establishes the project concept, course format options, strategic rationale, and site suitability criteria for converting closed mine land into a golf course, driving range, eco-golf park, or hybrid recreation destination.

A Golf Course / Golf Training Facility / Eco-Recreation Golf Park on closed mine land converts disturbed post-mining landscapes into managed green recreational assets. Depending on land availability and objectives, a mine-closure golf project may be developed as:

  • Driving range and golf academy (4–10 acres) — training, youth engagement, community recreation, mine-township amenity
  • Par-3 / 6-hole or 9-hole executive course (15–30 acres) — low-maintenance, lower-water, community-oriented short-format golf
  • Standard 9-hole golf course (30–75 acres) — daily play, membership, local tournaments, township and industrial users
  • Executive 18-hole golf course (75–100 acres) — moderate land and water demand, public access, events, memberships
  • Full-size 18-hole par-72 course (120–200 acres) — regional sports, tourism, hospitality, championship events
  • Eco-golf and biodiversity park (50–200+ acres) — limited managed turf, native roughs, walking trails, wetlands, eco-education
  • Hybrid golf + resort + mine heritage destination (100+ acres) — integrated sports, tourism, hospitality, mine heritage interpretation

Golf is suitable for mine closure only where reclaimed land is geotechnically stable, environmentally safe, water-secure, socially acceptable, and legally permissible under the approved mine-closure framework. The Ministry of Coal's 2025 mine-closure guidelines create a policy basis for evaluating post-mining recreational and sports land uses.

  • Productive use of large reclaimed mine land parcels with contiguous, manageable terrain
  • Conversion of mine voids, ponds, and drainage features into landscape lakes, irrigation reservoirs, and stormwater features after safety treatment
  • Creation of visible green post-mining redevelopment and long-term landscape management
  • Long-term employment in turf care, irrigation, landscaping, hospitality, caddie services, events, maintenance, and security
  • Potential to integrate biodiversity, walking trails, native vegetation, wetlands, and environmental education
  • Use of existing mine roads, township facilities, water bodies, club infrastructure, and security systems
  • Support for local youth sports training through golf academy, driving range, school programmes, and beginner clinics
  • Compatibility with mine-area redevelopment, township revitalisation, wellness tourism, sports tourism, and public recreation
  • Improvement of mine-area image and post-closure land value

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Defines what the mine owner must complete before issuing the first RFP — covering land, safety, water, soil, market, and legal readiness — and what detailed work can be assigned to the selected contractor, developer, or operator post-award.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Golf Course / Eco-Recreation Golf Park / Golf Tourism mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage, final land-use plan and permissibility of golf / recreation / eco-tourism use under the approved Mine Closure Plan.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease conditions, commercial-use permission, concession rights, user charges, revenue-sharing permissibility, visitor liability and insurance requirement.
  3. Mine safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, old workings, mine voids, unsafe water bodies, contaminated areas and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, slope-stability screening, geotechnical screening, drainage review, water-body safety note and preliminary suitability of land for tees, greens, fairways, clubhouse and access roads.
  5. Environmental and water screening — Assess soil quality, water quality, residual contamination, ecological sensitivity, irrigation water availability, treated wastewater / mine-water reuse potential and suitability for public recreational use.
  6. Course format and activity suitability assessment — Identify feasible options such as driving range, golf academy, par-3 course, 9-hole course, 18-hole course, eco-golf park, walking trails, lakefront recreation or mine-heritage tourism based on land, water and safety conditions.
  7. Stakeholder and authority consultation — Consult local administration, tourism department, sports department, local body / panchayat, fire services, health department, police, SPCB, nearby communities, SHGs, local entrepreneurs and potential golf / hospitality operators.
  8. Preliminary zoning and carrying-capacity plan — Prepare a basic layout showing entry area, clubhouse, practice range, play zones, eco-buffer zones, water bodies, no-go areas, emergency routes, maintenance yard, parking, public facilities and broad visitor capacity.
  9. Market, revenue and funding assessment — Estimate demand and revenue from green fees, membership, driving range, academy, events, tournaments, clubhouse, F&B, parking, tourism tie-ups and lease / concession income.
  10. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates, including turf establishment, irrigation, clubhouse, maintenance equipment, manpower and water cost, and select a preferred model such as EPC + O&M, DBFOT, BOOT, Concession / LDOT or Hybrid Golf + Eco-Recreation.
  11. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering golf-course design, construction, irrigation, turf establishment, clubhouse, O&M, environmental safeguards, public safety, local employment, caddie / groundskeeper training, insurance and community-access obligations.
  12. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering golf-course development experience, mine-land rehabilitation experience, technical capability, water-management plan, safety record, financial capacity, O&M plan, local livelihood plan and revenue / concession proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / golf operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, slope-stability study, subsidence assessment, highwall assessment, soil testing, water-body assessment and contamination testing.
  2. Detailed environmental and safety planning — Prepare the environmental management plan, water-management plan, irrigation strategy, turf and fertilizer management plan, public-safety plan, emergency-response plan and final carrying-capacity assessment.
  3. Final master planning — Prepare the final golf-course master plan showing course routing, tees, greens, fairways, roughs, water bodies, eco-zones, clubhouse, practice range, visitor circulation, maintenance yard, no-go zones, parking and emergency routes.
  4. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — The selected agency shall prepare the detailed DPR, including final master plan, detailed surveys, course design, water balance, irrigation design, financial model, statutory approvals plan, O&M plan, environmental safeguards and implementation schedule, for approval by the mine owner.
  5. Detailed engineering and agronomy design — Prepare detailed designs for earthworks, grading, drainage, greens, tees, bunkers, cart paths, irrigation network, pump house, clubhouse, maintenance shed, soil amendments, turf selection and landscape restoration.
  6. Approvals, permissions and insurance — Obtain required local body approvals, building permission, fire approval, electrical approval, water-use / treated-water permission, SPCB consent where applicable, tourism / commercial operation permissions, public-liability insurance and worker insurance.
  7. Procurement of equipment and systems — Procure turf equipment, mowers, irrigation systems, pumps, golf carts, maintenance tools, PPE, first-aid systems, CCTV, access-control system, booking / ticketing system and safety signage.
  8. Construction and turf establishment — Develop earthworks, drainage, irrigation, tees, greens, fairways, bunkers, cart paths, clubhouse, maintenance yard, parking, toilets, lighting, signage, fencing, landscape areas and green buffers.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for golf play, visitor entry, cart movement, water-body safety, turf maintenance, pesticide / fertilizer handling, emergency response, weather closure, incident reporting and daily inspection.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train groundskeepers, irrigation operators, caddies, golf-cart attendants, clubhouse staff, security staff, housekeeping staff, gardeners, equipment mechanics and local youth.
  11. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, food kiosks, local transport providers, souvenir sellers, nursery workers, event-support vendors, maintenance agencies and hospitality-linked local enterprises.
  12. Trial operation and safety audit — Conduct grow-in inspection, trial play, irrigation testing, emergency drills, visitor-flow testing, equipment testing and third-party safety / operational audit before public opening.
  13. Commissioning and public opening — Commission the golf course after turf readiness, safety clearance, staff readiness, insurance coverage, statutory compliance and visitor-management systems are in place.
  14. Regular operation and monitoring — Carry out routine O&M, turf maintenance, irrigation monitoring, water-use reporting, fertilizer / pesticide records, visitor management, safety audits, environmental monitoring and compliance reporting.
  15. Phased expansion — Expand to additional holes, academy, events, eco-trails, resort linkage, lakefront recreation or mine-heritage tourism only after demand validation, safety performance, environmental compliance and mine-owner approval.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models (in order):

  1. EPC + O&M / Facility Management — where funding is available and owner control is preferred
  2. Hybrid Golf + Eco-Recreation + Tourism — where recreation-led mine redevelopment and tourism potential are primary objectives
  3. DBFOT / Lease-Develop-Operate — where private financing, tourism linkage, and operational risk transfer are preferred

Recommended Development Position: Given the land, water, and O&M intensity of golf courses, the recommended first step is a pre-feasibility, topographic, geotechnical, water-balance, agronomic, market-demand, and environmental-suitability study. For most mine owners, EPC + O&M / Facility Management is the most balanced model combining ownership, landscape control, public image, profitability, and local livelihood. Where a mine-heritage tourism project, eco-park, or resort is also under consideration, a combined Mine Closure + Golf Course + Eco-Recreation + Tourism pathway should be examined.
