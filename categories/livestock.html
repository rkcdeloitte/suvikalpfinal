Livestock Farming
Global Precedents
Open Grassland Buffalo Grazing, Eastern India — Reclamation Potential under SC 2020 Mandate
In India, livestock rearing is a widely practised livelihood activity that contributes to household income, food security, and rural economic development through the production of milk, meat, wool, and draught power. Reclaimed mine land across coal-bearing states offers a viable opportunity to develop livestock-based enterprises, utilizing restored grasslands, fodder plots, and ancillary land parcels to support cattle, goat, sheep, and poultry rearing for communities transitioning from mining-based livelihoods.
Such initiatives can provide stable and diversified income opportunities for local communities, particularly those previously dependent on mining, through activities such as animal husbandry, milk and meat processing, and allied agro-enterprises. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring availability of water, fodder, and veterinary services, providing technical training and inputs to livestock rearers, managing waste and environmental impacts, and developing reliable market linkages and value chain infrastructure to ensure the long-term sustainability and economic viability of livestock-based enterprises.

P
PROJECT VISION

Concept for Non-Dairy Livestock Zones and Livestock–Fodder Livelihood Clusters on closed coal mine land — covering goat rearing, sheep, poultry, piggery, duckery, fodder cultivation, and egg–meat–manure value chains. Site suitability, species selection, and indicative scale planning are covered.

This concept proposes the development of Non-Dairy Livestock Zones / Livestock–Fodder Livelihood Clusters on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The model excludes dairy farming as a primary component and instead focuses on goat rearing and goat-meat value chain; sheep rearing and wool/meat-based livelihood; piggery units where socially acceptable and market-linked; backyard poultry and commercial poultry units; duckery in water-rich landscapes; fodder cultivation, fodder banks and fodder-tree systems; animal shelters, paddocks and controlled grazing zones; egg, meat, manure, compost and biogas value chains; veterinary, vaccination, breeding and training services; and SHG / FPO / cooperative / producer-group-based livelihood enterprises.

India had 74.26 million sheep, 148.89 million goats, 9.06 million pigs and 851.81 million poultry birds as per the 20th Livestock Census. The National Livestock Mission specifically supports entrepreneurship in poultry, sheep, goat and piggery, along with feed and fodder development.

Non-dairy livestock is suitable for selected closed coal mine areas where dairy may not be ideal due to water demand, milk route limitations, higher daily management needs, or overlap with a separate dairy project.

  • Generates income through meat, eggs, live animals, manure, compost, fodder and breeding-stock sales
  • Requires smaller land parcels compared with large dairy parks, especially for goatery, sheep, backyard poultry and duckery
  • Supports land-losers, women SHGs, youth, traditional livestock keepers and rural producer groups
  • Allows phased livelihood development through small animal units rather than high-capital dairy herds
  • Can utilize reclaimed land through controlled grazing, fodder plots, fodder-tree belts, silvi-pasture and paddock systems
  • Supports circular economy through dung, poultry litter and biodegradable waste conversion into compost, vermicompost, biogas and bio-slurry
  • Enables decentralized livelihood models suitable for SHGs, FPOs, cooperatives and panchayat-level enterprises
  • Creates local employment in animal care, feeding, shed maintenance, vaccination support, feed preparation, egg handling, meat aggregation, composting and logistics
  • Can be integrated with the National Livestock Mission, which supports poultry, sheep, goat, piggery, feed and fodder entrepreneurship
  • Reduces dependence on a single product stream by combining meat, eggs, manure, fodder and livestock-service revenue

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP readiness for non-dairy livestock development requires land safety confirmation, species suitability assessment, market and veterinary linkage planning, and community mobilization. Post-award activities include infrastructure construction, animal procurement, training, market linkage, and phased scale-up led by the selected operator, concessionaire, or SHG / FPO partner.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Livestock Farming, Fodder Landscapes and Meat–Egg–Manure Value-Chain Cluster as a mine-repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and mine-closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of animal husbandry, fodder cultivation, composting, biogas and related livelihood activities.
  2. Legal, regulatory and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease / concession rights, animal ownership, produce ownership, revenue-sharing, insurance, user charges and commercial-use permissions.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, contaminated areas, unsafe water bodies, haul roads and restricted mine infrastructure.
  4. Soil, water and contamination assessment — Test soil, surface water and groundwater for suitability of fodder cultivation, animal drinking, washing and manure use, with special attention to heavy metals, pH, salinity and polluted runoff.
  5. Species and activity suitability assessment — Identify suitable non-dairy animal husbandry activities such as goatery, sheep rearing, piggery, poultry, duckery, rabbitry, fodder bank, composting and biogas based on site conditions, market demand and community acceptance.
  6. Fodder and feed-resource assessment — Assess green fodder, dry fodder, fodder-tree belts, silage, hay, crop residues, feed-supply access and fodder-seed availability, in line with the feed and fodder development focus under the National Livestock Mission.
  7. Veterinary, biosecurity and disease-control linkage — Map nearby veterinary hospitals, mobile veterinary units, vaccination support, disease reporting systems and quarantine arrangements, considering the Prevention and Control of Infectious and Contagious Diseases in Animals Act, 2009.
  8. Animal welfare and housing readiness — Prepare basic norms for species-wise housing, ventilation, shade, floor drainage, drinking water, isolation pens, humane handling and animal transport, consistent with animal-welfare rules listed by the Animal Welfare Board of India.
  9. Market and value-chain assessment — Assess demand and offtake for goats, sheep, pigs, poultry birds, eggs, manure, compost, vermicompost, fodder and biogas, including linkage with traders, processors, FPOs, SHGs and institutional buyers.
  10. Food-safety and product-handling framework — Prepare compliance requirements for egg, meat, processed food, packaging, storage and sale, including FSSAI registration / licensing for relevant Food Business Operators.
  11. Waste, manure and circular-economy plan — Prepare a plan for dung, poultry litter, piggery slurry, bedding waste, dead-animal disposal, composting, vermicompost, bio-slurry, biogas or CBG, including odour, vector and effluent control.
  12. Community, SHG / FPO and livelihood mapping — Identify land-losers, nearby livestock keepers, women SHGs, youth groups, FPOs, local entrepreneurs and panchayat-level institutions for livelihood participation and benefit-sharing.
  13. Implementation model and cost estimate — Prepare broad CAPEX, 5-year O&M estimate and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, EPC + O&M, DBFOT or BOOT.
  14. Draft RFP scope and bid evaluation framework — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, animal procurement, O&M, veterinary services, biosecurity, local employment, SHG / FPO participation, waste management, reporting and revenue-sharing.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / livestock operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation and DPR preparation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, soil-water testing, drainage assessment, species suitability study, fodder feasibility, final master plan, financial model and DPR for mine-owner approval.
  2. Final livestock master planning — Prepare the final layout for goat / sheep sheds, poultry houses, piggery units, duckery areas, fodder plots, quarantine pens, isolation units, manure yard, compost unit, biogas unit, internal roads and buffer areas.
  3. Approvals, registration and compliance planning — Obtain required local permissions, animal-husbandry department linkage, FSSAI registration / licence where applicable, pollution-control consent if required, insurance and other statutory approvals.
  4. Detailed design of sheds and support infrastructure — Prepare species-wise designs for animal housing, ventilation, drainage, drinking-water systems, feed stores, egg / meat handling areas, manure pits, compost platforms, biogas units and effluent systems.
  5. Biosecurity, animal welfare and SOP finalization — Prepare SOPs for quarantine, vaccination, deworming, disease reporting, visitor control, sanitation, humane handling, animal transport, dead-animal disposal and emergency response.
  6. Construction and installation — Develop sheds, paddocks, poultry houses, piggery units, duck shelters, fencing, water systems, feed storage, fodder plots, manure yard, composting units, biogas systems, lighting and basic utilities.
  7. Procurement of animals, birds and equipment — Procure approved breeds / stock, feed equipment, drinkers, feeders, weighing systems, PPE, first-aid kits, vaccination equipment, cleaning tools and record-keeping systems.
  8. Quarantine and animal induction — Induct animals / birds through quarantine, health screening, vaccination, tagging / identification, insurance and initial productivity recording before full-scale operation.
  9. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train local youth, SHG members, handlers, supervisors, feed workers, sanitation workers and veterinary-support workers in animal care, feeding, biosecurity, product handling and record keeping.
  10. Market linkage and livelihood integration — Finalize offtake arrangements with goat / sheep traders, poultry integrators, egg buyers, piggery buyers, FPOs, SHGs, compost buyers and institutional customers.
  11. Trial operation and compliance audit — Run trial operations to test animal survival, feed supply, water supply, waste handling, disease-control systems, product handling, worker training and emergency response.
  12. Commercial operation and reporting — Begin full operation with regular reporting on animal health, mortality, production, revenue, local employment, SHG / FPO participation, waste utilization and environmental compliance.
  13. Phased scale-up and diversification — Expand goatery, sheep units, poultry, piggery, duckery, fodder banks, composting, biogas or value-added activities based on market demand, disease-control performance, community participation and mine-owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

The Hybrid Non-Dairy Livestock–Fodder Livelihood Cluster is the most recommended model for closed coal mine repurposing. This section outlines the preferred models, key references, and abbreviations used throughout the PRISM framework.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) EPC + O&M / Facility Management, (4) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because livestock projects require land, fodder, veterinary support, producer participation and market linkage. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner land + SHG / FPO livestock units + veterinary agency + private buyer / processor linkage.

Secondary option: BOOT may be used where commercial poultry, piggery, feed production or processing with eventual transfer is planned.

Suggestable technical care: Animal housing, fodder, water, disease control, vaccination, biosecurity, waste management, community acceptance, animal welfare and market access must be ensured.

Recommended development logic: Start with low-risk goatery, sheep units, backyard poultry, fodder banks and composting. Take up piggery, commercial poultry and meat-processing models only after biosecurity, market and waste-management readiness are confirmed.
