Final Recommendations / Preservation Centre — Cold Chain & Cold Storage
Preservation Centre
Global Precedents
Banana Ripening Cold Chain Facility, Isfahan, Iran

A Preservation Centre / Cold Chain Facility on closed coal mine land is a temperature-controlled storage, handling, processing, and distribution hub for perishable and temperature-sensitive products — fruits, vegetables, dairy, meat, fish, frozen food, medicines, vaccines, and pharmaceutical items.

This model converts under-utilised mine land or reusable mine infrastructure into a cold-chain and preservation hub that supports food security, post-harvest loss reduction, medical supply resilience, rural livelihoods, agri-business, and regional logistics. It is especially suitable where road connectivity, power, and proximity to farming areas or hospitals exist.

PROJECT VISION

Post-mining site context, suitability criteria, scale matrix, and owner-side readiness needed before committing to a Preservation Centre project.

This concept proposes the development of a Preservation Centre / Cold Chain Preservation Facility on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy.

A Preservation Centre, in this context, refers to a temperature-controlled storage, handling, processing, and distribution facility for preserving perishable and temperature-sensitive products such as:

  • Fruits and vegetables; grains and seeds requiring controlled storage
  • Dairy products; meat and poultry; fish and marine products
  • Frozen foods; ready-to-eat and processed food products
  • Flowers and horticulture products
  • Vaccines and biological products; medicines and pharmaceuticals; diagnostic samples

The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land or suitable mine infrastructure into a cold-chain and preservation hub that supports food security, post-harvest loss reduction, medical supply resilience, rural livelihoods, agri-business, local employment, and regional logistics.

This model is particularly suitable for closed coal mine areas where the site has available land, road connectivity, proximity to farming areas, markets, hospitals, industrial areas, or logistics routes; existing power infrastructure; scope for solar integration; available buildings that can be adapted; and local workforce requiring post-mining livelihood opportunities.

A Preservation Centre is suitable for selected closed coal mine areas because cold-chain infrastructure can generate long-term livelihood, reduce waste, support farmers, improve medical supply reliability, and create a productive post-mining land-use model.

Key Benefits:

  • Converts closed mine land into productive preservation and logistics infrastructure
  • Reduces post-harvest losses by extending shelf life of perishable produce
  • Supports farmers, FPOs, traders, processors, hospitals, pharmacies, and local entrepreneurs
  • Creates skilled and semi-skilled jobs in cold storage operations, refrigeration maintenance, loading, sorting, grading, packing, logistics, quality control, and facility management
  • Enables multi-commodity and multi-temperature storage
  • Supports food processing, value addition, and market linkage
  • Supports vaccine, medicine, and medical-item preservation where regulatory requirements are met
  • Can integrate with pack houses, reefer transport, ripening chambers, blast freezers, IQF units, and food-processing units
  • Can reuse suitable mine buildings, workshops, yards, roads, substations, and administrative infrastructure
  • Supports climate-resilient and energy-efficient infrastructure through solar power, thermal storage, efficient refrigeration, and natural / low-GWP refrigerants
  • Generates revenue from storage charges, handling charges, processing charges, logistics, leasing, user fees, and value-added services

The National Centre for Cold Chain Development has issued engineering guidelines and minimum system standards for cold-chain components. For vaccines, standard refrigerated storage is maintained at +2°C to +8°C, and temperature monitoring is essential per WHO immunisation guidance.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP readiness checklist, post-award scope, and the 15-step implementation pathway for mine-area Preservation Centre development.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Preservation Centre / Cold Chain-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of using the closed mine land for cold storage, food preservation, medical storage, logistics and value-addition activities.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease / concession rights, commercial rights, user charges, revenue-sharing permissibility, produce liability, storage liability and insurance requirements.
  3. Mine safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, subsidence-prone areas, highwalls, unsafe water bodies, abandoned structures, active mine interfaces and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, foundation suitability note, drainage review, flood-risk assessment, road-access assessment and truck-movement feasibility.
  5. Power, water and utility readiness — Assess grid power availability, connected load, transformer capacity, backup power, solar feasibility, potable water, cleaning water, firefighting water and wastewater disposal requirements.
  6. Commodity and demand assessment — Identify likely users and products such as fruits, vegetables, dairy, fish, meat, frozen food, seeds, processed food, vaccines, pharmaceuticals, diagnostics and other temperature-sensitive medical items.
  7. Temperature-zone and capacity planning — Prepare preliminary capacity in MT and define chamber requirements such as chilled storage, frozen storage, controlled ambient storage, blast freezing, ripening chamber, pre-cooling unit and medical cold room.
  8. Regulatory and compliance screening — Identify applicable requirements such as FSSAI licensing / registration, Food Safety and Standards regulations, Legal Metrology requirements, fire NOC, factory / labour compliance, environmental consent and biomedical / pharmaceutical storage norms where applicable.
  9. Cold-chain standards and technology note — Prepare a preliminary technology note in line with NCCD cold-chain guidance covering refrigeration system, insulation, material handling, energy efficiency, digital monitoring, reefer interface and temperature recording. NCCD is the Government of India body for promoting integrated cold-chain infrastructure for agriculture and allied perishables.
  10. Food safety and medical cold-chain protocol — Prepare separate protocols for food products and medical items, including hygiene zoning, pest control, cleaning SOPs, temperature mapping, data loggers, alarm systems, product segregation and recall procedure. WHO guidance treats storage and transport of time- and temperature-sensitive pharmaceutical products as a controlled process requiring documented temperature conditions and monitoring.
  11. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult farmers, FPOs, SHGs, traders, food processors, dairy / fisheries stakeholders, hospitals, pharmacies, district administration, agriculture / horticulture departments, health department and logistics operators.
  12. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates covering civil works, insulation, refrigeration, power backup, solar, WMS, IoT monitoring, pack-house, loading dock, manpower and maintenance; then select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, DBFOT or EPC + O&M.
  13. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, refrigeration installation, operation, food safety compliance, medical cold-chain compliance, energy performance, local employment, FPO / SHG linkage, tariff structure, reporting and handover obligations.
  14. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering cold-chain experience, refrigeration technical capability, food / pharma handling experience, safety record, financial capacity, energy-efficiency proposal, O&M plan, local livelihood integration and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / cold-chain operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, geotechnical investigation, foundation design inputs, drainage assessment, flood-risk study, truck-circulation study, utility survey and building-condition assessment where existing structures are reused.
  2. Detailed demand and product-mix finalization — Validate commodity flow, user demand, seasonality, medical storage requirement, farmer / FPO linkage, institutional demand and chamber-wise utilization assumptions.
  3. Final master planning — Prepare the final preservation-centre layout showing cold rooms, frozen rooms, controlled ambient area, pack-house, ripening chamber, blast freezer, loading docks, reefer parking, utility block, office, quality room, worker amenities and emergency access.
  4. Detailed engineering and refrigeration design — Prepare detailed designs for insulated envelope, floor insulation, refrigeration plant, evaporators, condensers, compressors, electrical systems, backup power, solar integration, fire safety, drainage and wastewater systems.
  5. Detailed Project Report — The selected concessionaire / operator shall prepare the DPR, including detailed surveys, final capacity, chamber-wise design, refrigeration technology, financial model, statutory approvals plan, food safety plan, medical cold-chain plan, O&M plan and implementation schedule, for approval by the mine owner.
  6. Approvals, licences and insurance — Obtain applicable approvals such as FSSAI licence / registration, fire NOC, consent from pollution control authority where required, factory / labour registrations, Legal Metrology compliance, electrical safety approval, insurance cover and medical storage permissions where applicable.
  7. Procurement of equipment and systems — Procure refrigeration plant, insulated panels, insulated doors, dock shelters, forklifts, pallets, racks, data loggers, IoT sensors, WMS, CCTV, fire systems, PPE, cleaning systems and backup-power equipment.
  8. Construction and installation — Develop the cold-storage building, pack-house, loading docks, drainage, roads, reefer parking, refrigeration plant room, utilities, worker facilities, office, quality-control room and safety systems.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for product receipt, grading, storage, temperature monitoring, cleaning, pest control, equipment maintenance, product recall, emergency response, power failure, medical item handling and incident reporting.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train refrigeration technicians, warehouse operators, quality-control staff, WMS operators, loaders, safety staff, security staff and local youth for cold-chain operations.
  11. FPO / SHG and institutional onboarding — Onboard farmers, FPOs, SHGs, traders, processors, hospitals, pharmacies, diagnostic centres and logistics partners through service agreements, tariff terms and operating protocols.
  12. Trial operation and validation — Conduct dry runs, chamber pull-down tests, temperature mapping, alarm testing, backup-power testing, WMS testing, mock product movement and emergency drills before commercial operation.
  13. Commissioning and commercial opening — Commission the Preservation Centre after successful validation, safety clearance, staff readiness, licence compliance and approval by the mine owner.
  14. Regular operation and monitoring — Carry out routine O&M, temperature monitoring, energy monitoring, food safety checks, medical cold-chain records, equipment maintenance, utilization reporting, incident reporting and compliance reporting.
  15. Phased expansion — Expand storage capacity, product categories, reefer fleet, pack-house services, processing, medical storage, solar power and value-added services based on demand, revenue, compliance record and owner approval.

Secondary Option: DBFOT for large regional cold-chain hubs requiring private finance, advanced refrigeration, multi-temperature storage, food processing, medical cold-chain zones, and professional operation.

Suggested Preservation Centre Positioning

For closed coal mine land, the facility should preferably be positioned as a Multi-Product Cold Chain and Preservation Centre with the following modules:

  • Multi-temperature cold storage (chilled, frozen, deep-freezer, controlled atmosphere)
  • Pre-cooling unit; pack house with sorting, grading, and packing
  • Ripening chamber for fruits, where required
  • Blast freezer / IQF unit, where required
  • Reefer vehicle loading and docking area
  • Medical / vaccine cold-room zone, where compliant and demand exists
  • Food safety and quality testing room
  • Inventory and digital monitoring room (IoT / SCADA)
  • Solar rooftop and energy-management system
  • Training room for local youth, SHGs, FPOs, and operators
  • Farmer service centre; administrative and billing office
  • Maintenance workshop; fire safety and emergency control system

The preservation centre should be structured not only as a cold storage warehouse, but as an integrated cold-chain livelihood and logistics platform supporting food preservation, medical supply reliability, local enterprise, farmer income, and responsible post-mining land reuse.
