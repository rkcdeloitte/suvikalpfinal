Final Recommendations / Aquifer Recharge & Rainwater Harvesting
AQUIFER RECHARGE & RAINWATER HARVESTING
Precedents
Tarun Bharat Sangh Johad Network, Alwar, Rajasthan
Aquifer Recharge & Rainwater Harvesting proposes the conversion of under-utilized mine land, mine voids, abandoned pits, overburden dump slopes, haul roads, and drainage channels into a water-conservation, groundwater-recharge, drought-resilience, and community-water-security asset. Existing mine landforms such as pits, benches, haul roads, drains, sumps, and low-lying voids can be progressively repurposed into structured water-harvesting and groundwater-recharge systems.
This aligns with the CGWB's Manual on Artificial Recharge of Ground Water (2007) and the Master Plan for Artificial Recharge to Groundwater in India (2020), which identify check dams, percolation tanks, recharge shafts, recharge trenches, and rooftop rainwater harvesting systems as suitable structures. India's closed open-cast mines have been discussed as potential lakes for rainwater harvesting and aquifer recharge in mine closure planning — subject to site-specific hydrogeological investigation, water-quality safeguards, and statutory approvals.

India's National Water Mission targets conservation of water, minimizing wastage, and equitable distribution. P
PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix for mine-area water harvesting, and indicative scale matrix — from a 1–5 ha pilot recharge demonstration to a 100 ha+ regional aquifer recharge and mine water resilience landscape.

This concept proposes the development of Aquifer Recharge & Rainwater Harvesting Systems on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land, mine voids, abandoned pits, overburden dump slopes, haul roads, roof areas, and drainage channels into a water-conservation, groundwater-recharge, drought-resilience, and community-water-security asset.

This shall be treated as a site-specific, feasibility-dependent proposal. Actual implementation shall be subject to hydrogeological investigation, water-quality testing, geotechnical safety assessment, regulatory approval, and community-access safeguards. Controlled recharge may only proceed after sediment removal, first-flush diversion, water-quality testing, and confirmation that recharge will not contaminate aquifers.

The project can serve four complementary roles: groundwater recharge through wells, shafts, trenches, ponds, and percolation tanks; rainwater storage in abandoned mine pits and low-lying areas; stormwater management through check dams, contour trenches, and vegetated channels; and community water security supporting plantations, nurseries, agriculture, livestock, and firefighting.

  • Converts abandoned pits, mine voids, sumps, drains, and lowlands into productive water-management assets
  • May enhance groundwater recharge where aquifer conditions, recharge capacity, and source-water quality are suitable
  • Conserves monsoon runoff for post-monsoon and dry-season use
  • Reduces erosion, gullying, sediment transport, and uncontrolled stormwater discharge
  • Supports mine-closure plantations, nurseries, eco-parks, wetlands, and agriculture-linked repurposing
  • Supports climate adaptation by increasing water storage and reducing drought vulnerability
  • Reduces pressure on external water supply for mine-closure landscapes
  • Creates long-term local jobs in construction, desilting, monitoring, maintenance, plantation irrigation, and water testing
  • Supports women SHGs, youth groups, and FPOs through maintenance contracts, monitoring support, and local service enterprises
  • Provides environmental education on water conservation, watershed management, and mine-closure sustainability
  • Enhances ESG, water stewardship, climate resilience, and mine-closure compliance outcomes
  • Supports "water-positive" mine-closure positioning and improved community acceptance

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Details the pre-RFP checklist including hydrogeological study, water-quality assessment, AMD and contamination screening, safety zoning, catchment assessment, and what can be taken up post-award by the selected contractor or O&M agency.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

  1. Land, closure, and land-use confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, Mine Closure Plan linkage, and permissibility of water conservation, recharge, storage, or community-water use
  2. Statutory and regulatory screening — Identify required approvals from CCO / Ministry of Coal, CGWA or State Ground Water Authority, State Pollution Control Board, water resources department, local body, and panchayat
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Map unsafe pit edges, highwalls, unstable dumps, subsidence-prone areas, deep-water zones, and weak embankments
  4. Catchment, rainfall-runoff, and water-balance assessment — Assess catchment area, drainage routes, rainfall, runoff, storage potential, evaporation, overflow risk, and dry-season availability
  5. Hydrogeological and recharge feasibility assessment — Assess aquifer type, water-table depth, groundwater-flow direction, infiltration potential, recharge capacity, and contaminant migration risk
  6. Water-quality, AMD, and contamination screening — Test mine water, pit water, stormwater, rooftop runoff, and groundwater for AMD indicators, heavy metals, TDS, TSS, oil/grease, and microbial load
  7. Existing infrastructure and structure suitability assessment — Prepare inventory of pits, voids, sumps, drains, rooftops, tanks, pipelines, and pumps; identify check dam, recharge trench, recharge shaft, percolation tank, and rooftop harvesting options
  8. Preliminary zoning and go/no-go criteria — Classify areas for direct recharge, storage-only use, sedimentation, wetland pre-treatment, rooftop harvesting, plantation-water use, or no-go zone
  9. Community demand and stakeholder consultation — Assess water demand from nearby villages, plantations, nurseries, agriculture, livestock, eco-parks; consult State Ground Water Department, panchayats, SHGs, FPOs, and local communities
  10. Cost, model selection, and RFP framework — Prepare broad CAPEX, O&M, and monitoring cost; select preferred model; prepare bidder obligations and evaluation criteria

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

  1. Detailed site investigation and DPR — survey, drone mapping, geotechnical study, hydrogeological study, water-quality testing, and water-balance modelling
  2. Statutory approvals and technical clearances from CGWA / State Ground Water Authority, SPCB, water resources department, and local authorities
  3. Detailed design of recharge and harvesting systems — check dams, recharge ponds, percolation tanks, recharge trenches, shafts, wells, rooftop harvesting, silt traps, spillways, and overflow drains
  4. Treatment, pre-treatment, and safety design — first-flush systems, desilting chambers, filtration, oil-grease traps, sedimentation ponds, fencing, signage, and embankment protection
  5. Construction and installation of recharge structures, stormwater channels, monitoring wells, protective works, and erosion-control measures
  6. O&M manual and local training for SHGs / FPOs and community groups for desilting, recharge operation, water-quality testing, and safety inspection
  7. Pre-recharge technical clearance — confirm source-water quality, treatment systems, aquifer conditions, recharge design, monitoring wells, overflow routes, and safety arrangements
  8. Controlled trial recharge with monitoring of water level, water quality, recharge rate, mounding behaviour, and nearby well response
  9. Commissioning and regular O&M, desilting, water-level monitoring, water-quality testing, safety inspection, and community-use supervision
  10. Independent audit and phased scaling based on monitored performance and competent approval

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model; (2) EPC + O&M / Facility Management; (3) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate; (4) DBFOT.

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because recharge projects require hydrogeology, public benefit, long-term maintenance, monitoring, and community participation. The hybrid mix should include owner-funded civil works + technical hydrogeology agency + community water users + long-term O&M partner.

Secondary option: EPC may be used for basic rainwater harvesting structures where the owner can separately arrange monitoring and maintenance.

Technical care required: Hydrogeological study, aquifer suitability, water quality, recharge design, desilting, silt traps, flood routing, groundwater monitoring, and contamination safeguards are essential.

Final recommendation: Hybrid Model is most suitable because aquifer recharge is a long-term mine-closure legacy asset rather than a simple civil construction activity. Start with catchment mapping, recharge feasibility, and pilot structures. Scale into integrated water conservation linked with plantations, wetlands, eco-parks, and community water-security planning.
