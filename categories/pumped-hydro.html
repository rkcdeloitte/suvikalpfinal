Pumped Hydro Storage (PHS)
Global Precedents
Kidston Pumped Hydro Project, Queensland, Australia

Pumped Hydro Storage (PHS) — also called a Pumped Storage Project (PSP) — converts electrical energy into potential hydraulic energy by pumping water to an upper reservoir during surplus power, and recovers it through turbines during peak demand. It is the world's most proven form of long-duration grid-scale energy storage.

For closed coal mine areas, existing mine voids, open-cast pits, and mine-water bodies can serve as one of the reservoirs — reducing excavation cost and giving new purpose to post-mining land. This is a selective repurposing option, not a universal one.

P
PROJECT VISION

Post-mining site context, suitability criteria, and owner-side readiness needed before committing to a Pumped Hydro Storage project.

A closed coal mine typically leaves deep open-cast pits, underground workings, overburden dumps, mine-water bodies, access roads, electrical corridors, and perimeter fencing. For pumped hydro storage, the most valuable post-mining assets are: the pit geometry (potential lower reservoir), elevated dumps or adjacent ridges (potential upper reservoir), and existing infrastructure that can reduce enabling costs.

However, the post-mining context also introduces hazards: slope instability, subsidence from underground voids, acid mine drainage, sedimentation, and contaminated mine water. All must be assessed before committing to a PHS project.

India's rapid renewable energy expansion demands large-scale, long-duration storage to balance intermittent solar and wind generation. Pumped Hydro Storage is the only commercially proven, multi-gigawatt-hour storage technology at a cost competitive with new hydropower. Closed coal mines offer three strategic advantages for PHS development:

  • Ready infrastructure: Mine pits, access roads, electrical substations, and grid-evacuation corridors reduce enabling capital expenditure significantly.
  • Repurposing alignment: Transforms a closure liability into a revenue-generating, grid-stabilising clean energy asset with a 50+ year operational lifespan.
  • Community transition: Creates skilled employment in electromechanical, dam safety, water management, and grid-operation roles — directly absorbing trained mine workers.
  • Policy support: Central Electricity Authority and MoP have identified closed-loop PHS as a priority storage technology; mine-area PHS is explicitly aligned with India's energy transition and mine repurposing mandate.

A mine site should pass five critical screening tests before PHS is considered viable:

The following matrix provides a structured checklist for assessing the suitability of the proposed site for the selected repurposing option. It identifies the key physical, technical, environmental, infrastructure, legal, and owner-side readiness parameters that should be reviewed before the project is advanced to the next stage of development. The purpose of this matrix is to help the project owner understand existing site conditions, technical requirements, potential constraints, and preparatory actions required before feasibility studies, approvals, or tendering are initiated.

  1. Hydraulic Head: Sufficient elevation difference between upper and lower reservoir. NREL screening used a minimum of 300 m for closed-loop sites; smaller heads are viable but reduce energy density.
  2. Reservoir Volume: Sufficient live storage for the target generation duration (typically 4–10 hours). A global atlas reported an average combined footprint of ~6 ha per GWh of storage.
  3. Geotechnical & Slope Stability: Pit walls, dumps, and dam foundations must be stable under cyclic water-level fluctuation. Numerical modelling is essential.
  4. Water Balance & Quality: Water must be available for initial fill and annual make-up (evaporation + seepage). Mine water must be treated if acidic or contaminated.
  5. Grid Evacuation: Transmission connectivity at adequate voltage and capacity to absorb pumping load and dispatch generation.

REPURPOSING PATHWAYS

Project configurations, key infrastructure components, and phased development strategy for mine-area Pumped Hydro Storage.

The following table outlines the possible implementation models that may be considered for developing the proposed repurposing project. These models differ in terms of ownership structure, funding responsibility, private sector participation, operational control, and long-term risk allocation. The purpose of this table is to help the project owner compare broad development routes and select an implementation approach that aligns with its financial capacity, institutional preference, risk appetite, and long-term asset management objectives.

Depending on site characteristics, a mine-area PHS project may be structured as:

  • Open-cast pit as lower reservoir: The mine pit provides ready-made storage volume; an upper reservoir is constructed on adjacent elevated land or overburden dump.
  • Closed-loop system: Limited river interaction; water is recycled between upper and lower reservoirs with make-up for evaporation and seepage. Preferred for mine sites due to reduced environmental risk.
  • Semi-closed loop: Uses mine water, treated water, or an existing reservoir for support.
  • Underground pumped storage: Uses mine shafts, galleries, and tunnels as underground reservoirs — viable only where mine workings are structurally safe and watertight.

This matrix is intended to support early-stage decision-making by highlighting the relative strengths and limitations of each model. The final choice of model should be guided by the specific circumstances of the project, including its scale, commercial viability, applicable policy framework, and the owner's institutional preference.

  • Lower reservoir (mine pit / quarry void / mine-water body).
  • Upper reservoir (elevated land, overburden dump, embankment, or natural plateau).
  • Water conductor system: penstocks, pressure shafts, headrace / tailrace tunnels, surge shafts.
  • Reversible pump-turbine units (fixed-speed or variable-speed).
  • Surface or underground powerhouse.

A phased approach is strongly recommended for mine-area PHS projects. A pilot module (5–25 MW) can validate site conditions — head, reservoir performance, slope stability, water quality, and grid response — before committing to full-scale development. Phased expansion reduces upfront risk and allows commercial scale-up aligned with grid and market conditions. DBFOT and Concession models support phasing most naturally through concession terms.

Revenue streams for a mine-area PHS project include:

  • Storage capacity charges under long-term storage-service contracts.
  • Peak-power sale and energy arbitrage (buy cheap, sell dear).
  • Renewable-energy firming contracts with solar / wind developers.
  • Ancillary services: frequency support, spinning reserve, black-start capability.
  • Grid-balancing and resource-adequacy support payments.
  • Lease rentals, concession fees, and revenue-share payments to the mine owner under developer-led models.

Under owner-led models (EPC / EPC+O&M), the mine owner captures full operational revenue but bears full capital and operating risk. Under developer-led models (DBFOT, Concession), the mine owner receives lease income, concession fee, milestone payments, and eventual asset transfer.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-tender readiness, RFP conditions, post-award scope, and the 14-step implementation pathway for mine-area PHS.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Pumped Hydro Storage based mine-repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, mine-closure and permissibility confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, mine-void use permission, and whether the site can be offered for PSP / PHS development through lease, concession, PPP or owner-led development.
  2. Pre-feasibility site screening — Screen the site for the basic PSP requirements, especially available head, lower reservoir potential, upper reservoir location, live storage volume, reservoir area, access, and distance from grid evacuation infrastructure.
  3. Mine-safety and no-go zone mapping — Map unstable highwalls, subsidence-prone areas, underground workings, shafts, overburden dumps, unsafe water bodies, restricted infrastructure and any zones requiring DGMS-related safety review before development.
  4. Topographic, hydrological and reservoir-volume assessment — Prepare drone / LiDAR survey, contour mapping, catchment assessment, rainfall-runoff review, pit-water level history, preliminary reservoir-capacity calculation and likely storage duration in MW / MWh terms.
  5. Geotechnical, slope-stability and subsidence screening — Assess pit-wall stability, overburden dump stability, foundation condition, old underground galleries, settlement risk, seismic sensitivity and behaviour under cyclic water-level variation.
  6. Water availability, water quality and AMD screening — Test mine-pit water and proposed reservoir water for pH, iron, manganese, sulphate, total dissolved solids, silt and other contamination indicators, and prepare a preliminary Acid Mine Drainage / water-treatment note.
  7. Grid connectivity and power evacuation feasibility — Confirm nearest STU / CTU substation, voltage level, transmission corridor, evacuation capacity, grid-study requirement and broad suitability for pumping and generation cycles.
  8. Environmental, forest and social screening — Identify likely requirements for Environmental Clearance, forest clearance, wildlife clearance, public hearing, water-use permission, biodiversity management, downstream drainage protection and community consultation.
  9. Dam safety and reservoir-safety screening — Identify whether the proposed upper / lower reservoirs, embankments, spillways and appurtenant structures may fall under dam-safety review, and prepare an initial reservoir-safety and emergency-risk note.
  10. Market, offtake and revenue assessment — Assess possible revenue routes such as storage-capacity contract, tolling arrangement, peaking-power sale, renewable-energy firming, ancillary services, DISCOM contract, GENCO use, captive use or merchant storage.
  11. Implementation model and risk-allocation decision — Select the preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, BOOT or EPC + O&M based on project size, CAPEX, owner risk appetite, private-sector interest and strategic control requirement.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — Prepare bidder scope covering DPR preparation, CEA concurrence, surveys, design, finance, construction, approvals, operation, dam-safety compliance, local employment, environmental safeguards, revenue sharing and handback obligations.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / developer / EPC contractor / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed investigation and surveys — Undertake detailed topographic survey, bathymetric survey of mine-pit water, geotechnical drilling, hydrogeological study, seismic study, slope-stability modelling, subsidence assessment and tunnel / penstock alignment investigation.
  2. Detailed water, AMD and reservoir studies — Prepare detailed water-balance study, reservoir operation simulation, seepage analysis, evaporation estimate, make-up water plan, sedimentation study, Acid Mine Drainage management plan and water-treatment requirement.
  3. DPR preparation as per CEA requirements — Prepare the Detailed Project Report covering hydrology, geology, reservoir design, energy calculation, installed capacity, pump-turbine selection, civil works, electro-mechanical systems, grid evacuation, cost estimate, financial model and implementation schedule.
  4. Statutory approvals and clearances — Obtain CEA concurrence, Environmental Clearance, forest / wildlife clearance if applicable, water-use permission, SPCB consent, DGMS-related clearance for mine-void use, grid connectivity approval, dam-safety review and other state / central approvals.
  5. Final master planning and engineering design — Finalize the upper reservoir, lower reservoir, powerhouse, penstock / tunnel, surge shaft, switchyard, access road, construction yard, drainage, spillway, monitoring systems and safety buffer layout.
  6. Financial closure and offtake arrangement — Finalize project finance, equity and debt structure, storage service agreement, tolling contract, PPA / PSA, DISCOM or GENCO arrangement, merchant strategy or renewable-firming contract as applicable.
  7. Procurement and construction preparation — Procure pump-turbines, generator-motors, transformers, gates, valves, penstocks, SCADA, protection systems, dam-safety instruments and civil-construction packages, and mobilize contractors and equipment.
  8. Construction of civil, reservoir and grid works — Construct or strengthen reservoirs, embankments, liners, spillways, intake / outlet structures, tunnels, penstocks, powerhouse, switchyard, access roads, drainage systems and transmission interconnection facilities.
  9. Installation of electro-mechanical and control systems — Install reversible pump-turbines, generator-motor units, transformers, switchgear, excitation systems, control systems, SCADA, protection, metering, communication and grid-dispatch interface.
  10. Safety systems, operating procedures and staffing — Prepare reservoir operation rules, Emergency Action Plan, dam-safety inspection schedule, O&M manual, safety management plan, local emergency protocol, staff deployment plan and training programme.
  11. Local livelihood and vendor integration — Engage local workers, service providers, transport operators, security staff, maintenance support, vegetation-management teams and trained youth wherever compatible with safety and technical requirements.
  12. Testing, trial operation and commissioning — Carry out reservoir filling, leakage monitoring, unit testing, pumping trial, generation trial, grid synchronization, protection testing, emergency drill, dam-safety inspection and performance validation.
  13. Commercial operation and compliance monitoring — Begin commercial operation after statutory readiness, and continue O&M, dam-safety monitoring, water-quality monitoring, grid scheduling, environmental compliance, local reporting and annual performance review.
  14. Phased expansion or optimization — Expand capacity, storage duration, renewable-energy linkage, additional units or grid-service participation only after stable operation, safety validation, revenue performance and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Operational safety management, local livelihood generation, and long-term strategic & ESG obligations for mine-area PHS.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

PHS facilities require highly specialized ongoing management across:

  • Reservoir & Dam Safety: Continuous monitoring of embankment instrumentation, water level, seepage, slope indicators, and spillway readiness. Emergency action plan mandatory.
  • Pump-Turbine & Electromechanical: Preventive and corrective maintenance of reversible units, valves, cranes, cooling, ventilation, and dewatering systems.
  • Water Management: Monitoring mine-water quality, managing evaporation and seepage losses, operating water-treatment systems, and controlling sedimentation.
  • Grid Dispatch: Real-time SCADA-based pumping and generation scheduling aligned with grid operator and offtake contracts.
  • Security & Access Control: Reservoir, dam, powerhouse, and switchyard are restricted zones requiring perimeter security and emergency response protocols.

PHS projects generate employment across multiple phases and roles:

  • Construction phase: Civil works, reservoir excavation, embankment construction, slope stabilization, tunnelling, penstock installation, road improvement, transmission works, and electrical / mechanical installation.
  • Operations phase: Reservoir surveillance, dam instrumentation support, mechanical maintenance, electrical maintenance, security, housekeeping, water management, vegetation management, logistics, and local services.
  • Training & transition: Local youth and ex-mine workers can be trained in electrical, mechanical, hydro-safety, reservoir monitoring, and facility-management roles.

    • Hybrid and Concession models are rated Very High for local livelihood generation because they allow explicit employment, training, apprenticeship, and vendor-participation conditions to be written into concession terms.

  • Regular environmental monitoring of water quality, biodiversity, and downstream drainage.
  • Community water-use audit to ensure the PSP does not worsen local water security.
  • Annual ESG reporting covering safety, water, biodiversity, community impact, and employment.
  • Dam-safety review by an independent panel as per statutory requirements.
  • Alignment with mine-closure obligations and post-mining land-use commitments.
  • Transparent reporting under CCO and MoC mine-repurposing monitoring frameworks.
