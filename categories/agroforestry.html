Final Recommendations / Agroforestry
Agroforestry
Precedents
Devachihatti Agroforestry, Karnataka, India

In India, agroforestry is gaining recognition as a productive and sustainable land-use approach that integrates trees, crops, and livestock on the same land parcel, supporting food security, ecological restoration, and rural livelihoods. With extensive tracts of reclaimed mine land across coal-bearing states, there is significant potential to develop agroforestry systems that restore degraded soils, enhance biodiversity, and generate diverse income streams for communities in mining-affected regions.

Such initiatives can provide stable livelihood opportunities for local communities, particularly those previously dependent on mining, through activities such as timber production, fruit cultivation, non-timber forest product harvesting, and allied agro-processing. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring soil remediation and fertility restoration, providing technical training and planting material to farmers, developing market linkages for produce, and establishing long-term institutional support to ensure the sustainability and economic viability of agroforestry-based livelihoods.

PROJECT VISION

Agroforestry on closed coal mine land combines ecological restoration, carbon sequestration, local employment, community welfare, and long-term productive land use.

This concept proposes the development of Agroforestry Zones on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilized mine land into a productive, ecological, livelihood-oriented, and community-serving asset.

Agroforestry may include tree-based farming, fruit orchards, medicinal and aromatic plants, fodder and silvi-pasture, bamboo and timber species, nursery development, community-managed plantations, apiculture, mushroom units, composting, demonstration farms, women SHG-led livelihood clusters, and eco-restoration belts.

The concept aligns with India's 2025 coal/lignite mine plan and closure guidelines, which include restoration and repurposing options such as agriculture, pisciculture, eco-parks, landscaping, waterbody conservation, irrigation, and solar/green-energy uses.

  • Ecological restoration: Agroforestry accelerates soil recovery, builds organic matter, stabilises overburden dumps, and restores biodiversity on degraded mine land.
  • Carbon sequestration: Tree-based systems capture and store carbon on mine land, supporting India's NDC commitments and enabling voluntary carbon credit revenues.
  • Local livelihood generation: Nursery operations, plantation maintenance, harvesting, composting, apiculture, processing, and community marketing create sustained employment for nearby communities and displaced mine workers.
  • Food and nutrition security: Fruit orchards, vegetable intercrops, and fodder plots improve local food access and support smallholder and SHG participation in productive land use.
  • Water conservation: Agroforestry systems reduce runoff, recharge groundwater, and enable productive use of treated mine water or seasonal water harvesting.
  • National policy alignment: Directly supports the National Agroforestry Policy 2014, Sub-Mission on Agroforestry, G.R.O.W. initiative, CAMPA-funded restoration, and mine closure obligations under CCO guidelines.
  • ESG and CSR value: Provides coal companies a credible, documentable, and community-visible post-mining environmental and social responsibility outcome.
  • Long-term land productivity: Converts a closure liability into a revenue-generating, ecologically productive, and community-supporting asset over a 20–40 year horizon.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Before issuing an RFP, the mine owner must prepare land, soil, environmental, community, and legal groundwork. Responsibilities must be clearly defined in bid documents to protect livelihood and ecological obligations.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an agroforestry-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, and agroforestry suitability under applicable mine-closure and land-use provisions.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, plantation rights, harvesting permissions, transit regulations, carbon-credit eligibility, lease conditions, and revenue-sharing permissibility.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe areas including unstable dumps, subsidence-prone zones, contaminated areas, active mine infrastructure, restricted zones, and unsafe water bodies.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Undertake topographic survey, geotechnical screening, slope-stability review, drainage assessment, hydrological review, and irrigation feasibility assessment.
  5. Soil quality and environmental screening — Assess soil fertility, pH, heavy-metal contamination, organic carbon, water quality, erosion risk, ecological sensitivity, and environmental suitability for agroforestry use.
  6. Agroforestry suitability assessment — Identify suitable agroforestry models such as orchards, bamboo plantation, medicinal plants, silvi-pasture, fodder plantation, nursery development, or mixed agroforestry systems.
  7. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult nearby communities, panchayats, SHGs, FPOs, agriculture and horticulture departments, forest authorities, local institutions, and affected persons.
  8. Preliminary zoning and land-development plan — Prepare a basic layout showing plantation zones, nursery areas, irrigation corridors, access roads, buffer zones, community-use areas, storage zones, and phased development areas.
  9. Water-resource and irrigation assessment — Evaluate mine water availability, rainwater harvesting potential, irrigation requirement, groundwater dependency, and water-distribution feasibility.
  10. Market and revenue assessment — Assess demand and revenue potential for fruits, bamboo, fodder, medicinal plants, nursery products, compost, apiculture, eco-tourism linkage, and carbon-credit opportunities.
  11. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare preliminary CAPEX and long-term O&M estimates and identify suitable models such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, or EPC + O&M.
  12. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Define bidder scope covering plantation establishment, irrigation systems, maintenance period, survival-rate obligations, local employment, SHG/FPO participation, environmental safeguards, and monitoring requirements.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering agroforestry experience, technical capability, plantation-maintenance approach, community integration, financial capacity, ESG approach, and livelihood-generation plan.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / agroforestry operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, soil investigation, hydrological assessment, irrigation assessment, drainage study, geotechnical assessment, and contamination verification.
  2. Detailed environmental and land-restoration planning — Prepare environmental management plan, soil-restoration plan, erosion-control strategy, water-management plan, biodiversity strategy, and plantation-survival framework.
  3. Final agroforestry master planning — Prepare the final agroforestry layout showing plantation blocks, species zoning, irrigation systems, nursery areas, storage facilities, community-use areas, access roads, and phased expansion areas.
  4. Detailed plantation and irrigation design — Prepare detailed designs for plantation spacing, species mix, water systems, drip/sprinkler irrigation, soil-conditioning measures, fencing, and erosion-control structures.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — Prepare the DPR including detailed surveys, plantation strategy, soil-improvement plan, water-management plan, species selection, carbon-credit potential, financial model, O&M strategy, implementation schedule, and livelihood integration framework for owner approval.
  6. Approvals and compliance — Obtain required statutory approvals, plantation permissions, groundwater permissions where applicable, environmental clearances, nursery registrations, and related compliances.
  7. Procurement of plantation and irrigation systems — Procure saplings, nursery material, irrigation systems, fencing material, farm equipment, composting systems, monitoring systems, and related infrastructure.
  8. Land development and plantation establishment — Carry out grading, soil improvement, pit preparation, plantation, irrigation installation, fencing, mulching, and initial maintenance.
  9. Preparation of operational procedures — Develop SOPs covering irrigation, plantation maintenance, pruning, pest management, harvesting, fire prevention, soil-health monitoring, and reporting.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train local workers, SHGs, FPO members, nursery workers, irrigation operators, field supervisors, and maintenance personnel.
  11. Community and livelihood integration — Integrate SHGs, FPOs, local farmers, women's groups, nursery operators, apiculture groups, and local entrepreneurs into agroforestry-linked livelihood activities.
  12. Monitoring and survival assessment — Conduct plantation-survival audits, soil-health monitoring, water-use monitoring, growth assessment, and third-party verification where required.
  13. Market linkage and value-chain development — Develop aggregation systems, storage, branding, processing linkage, buyer agreements, nursery sales, and agroforestry-product marketing channels.
  14. Carbon-credit and sustainability integration — Undertake carbon-baseline assessment, sustainability reporting, ESG documentation, green-credit assessment, and voluntary carbon-market integration where feasible.
  15. Regular operation and maintenance — Carry out routine irrigation, pruning, soil management, pest management, harvesting, monitoring, environmental compliance, and O&M reporting.
  16. Phased expansion and diversification — Expand plantation areas, nursery activities, value-addition units, eco-tourism integration, medicinal-plant zones, and community enterprise activities based on performance and owner approval.

The following implementation pathway presents a practical high-level sequence for advancing the proposed repurposing concept from initial approval through to project execution and long-term operation. It outlines the broad stages of concept approval, preliminary studies, model selection, bid preparation, procurement, detailed design, statutory approvals, construction, commissioning, and ongoing operation. This pathway is indicative and should be adapted to reflect the specific complexity, regulatory requirements, financing structure, and site conditions applicable to the project.

  1. Concept approval and owner-side structuring with definition of restoration, livelihood, revenue, ESG, and community objectives
  2. Site screening and agroforestry suitability confirmation — identification of reclaimed dumps, backfilled areas, buffer zones, and safe land parcels
  3. Soil, geotechnical, hydrological, and environmental studies — soil fertility, contamination risk, slope stability, drainage, water availability, and climate suitability
  4. Community and stakeholder consultation — nearby villages, SHGs, FPOs, land-losers, local institutions, and relevant government departments
  5. Market assessment and demand analysis for fruits, bamboo, fodder, medicinal plants, nursery, compost, and allied agroforestry activities
  6. Concept planning and agroforestry zoning — plantation zones, nursery areas, irrigation systems, access roads, community-use areas, and phased development
  7. Identification of owner-side enabling scope — land grading, soil improvement, fencing, irrigation infrastructure, access roads, and initial plantation support
  8. Selection of preferred implementation model among Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, or EPC + O&M based on funding, control, and livelihood priorities
  9. Preparation of DPR, bid documents, and concession or operating structure — species strategy, livelihood obligations, SHG/FPO integration, maintenance period, and revenue-sharing framework
  10. Issuance of RFQ / RFP targeting agroforestry developers, NGOs, FPOs, CSR agencies, and private operators

  15. Market linkage, value addition, and phased expansion — aggregation, processing, branding, eco-tourism integration, and scaling of agroforestry zones

MONITORING GUIDANCE

For agroforestry on closed coal mine land, the Hybrid Model is the practical first preference, followed by Concession / LDO and DBFOT.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because agroforestry needs mine-owner land control, ecological safeguards, community participation and technical agricultural support. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner land and water support + FPO / SHG participation + private nursery / market-linkage partner + CSR or government scheme convergence.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be used where the owner wants strong public control and the project is welfare-oriented rather than commercially driven.

Suggestable technical care: Soil quality, overburden toxicity, water availability, species selection, grazing protection, fire control, irrigation, nursery development, market linkage and long-term maintenance must be assessed.

Recommended development logic: Begin with soil testing, pilot plantation blocks, fodder / fruit / timber / medicinal species trials and community training. Scale only after survival rate, water requirement, community ownership and market linkage are proven.

Final recommendation: Hybrid Model is most suitable because agroforestry is both ecological restoration and livelihood generation, requiring shared technical, social and financial responsibilities.
