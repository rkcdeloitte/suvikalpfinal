BIOENERGY — BIOMASS & BIOSOLIDS
Precedents
GOBARdhan Bio-CNG Plant, Indore, India
Bioenergy Projects using Biomass and Biosolids on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land convert under-utilised sites into productive, livelihood-oriented, circular-economy and renewable-energy assets. They encompass biomass aggregation, storage and processing; anaerobic digestion; Bio-CNG / CBG production; pelletisation; composting; biochar; and community-based feedstock collection — generating energy, organic by-products and long-term employment for mine-affected communities.
Bioenergy is suited for a wide range of mine-closure sites across India — particularly those near agricultural residue belts, urban local bodies, or sanitation infrastructure. MNRE is the nodal ministry for bioenergy through the National Bioenergy Programme and supports Bio-CNG / CBG via the SATAT scheme. This aligns with CCO frameworks A.R.T.H.A., L.I.V.E.S. and R.E.C.L.A.I.M. by linking mine closure with renewable energy, circular sanitation, women-led enterprises, and post-mining economic continuity.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix, and indicative bioenergy scale models for mine-land repurposing using biomass and biosolids.

This concept proposes the development of Bioenergy Projects using Biomass and Biosolids on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land into a productive, livelihood-oriented, circular-economy and renewable-energy asset centred around biomass aggregation, storage and processing; use of agricultural residues, forestry residues, invasive biomass, energy plantations and organic waste; safe use of biosolids such as sewage sludge and treated organic sludge, subject to regulatory and quality safeguards; and production of biogas, Bio-CNG / CBG, electricity, pellets, briquettes, biochar, compost and digestate.

Bioenergy can be developed in modular form — from a small decentralised biogas / composting unit to a large Bio-CNG, biomass gasification, pelletisation or integrated bioenergy park. Unlike conventional land restoration, bioenergy-based mine repurposing creates a structured revenue model from energy, waste treatment, biomass processing and by-product sales.

  • Converts safe reclaimed mine land into productive renewable-energy and circular-economy assets
  • Creates long-term livelihoods in biomass collection, sorting, drying, transport, pelletisation, briquetting, composting, plant operation and maintenance
  • Supports SHGs, FPOs, youth groups, sanitation workers, panchayats, local transporters and micro-enterprises
  • Enables productive use of agricultural residues, forestry residues, invasive biomass, municipal organic waste and treated biosolids
  • Reduces open burning of crop residues and unmanaged biomass where supply chains are properly organised
  • Supports decentralised renewable energy — biogas, Bio-CNG / CBG, electricity, thermal energy, pellets, briquettes and biochar
  • Allows modular scaling from small biogas / composting pilots to large integrated bioenergy parks
  • Provides diversified revenue: Bio-CNG / CBG sales, biogas, electricity, pellets, briquettes, compost, digestate-based manure, biochar, tipping fees, feedstock aggregation fees, and carbon-credit or ESG-linked benefits
  • Supports mine-area just transition through new non-mining skills and income sources
  • Strengthens ESG reporting through renewable energy, waste circularity, land reuse, local livelihoods and community development outcomes

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Describes what must be ready before the first RFP — feedstock mapping, biosolids safety, technology options, environmental screening — and what can be taken up post-award.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Bioenergy (Biomass + Biosolids) mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and mine-closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of using reclaimed mine land for renewable energy, waste processing, biomass storage, biosolids handling or circular-economy use.
  2. Legal and land-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, lease conditions, concession rights, environmental liability, product ownership, user charges, tipping fee, energy sale rights and revenue-sharing permissibility.
  3. Regulatory pathway identification — Identify the likely approvals such as Consent to Establish, Consent to Operate, authorization for waste processing, solid-waste handling permissions, fire safety clearance and factory / labour compliances, depending on final technology and capacity.
  4. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including unstable dumps, mine voids, subsidence-prone land, highwalls, water-filled pits, fire-prone areas and restricted mine infrastructure.
  5. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, geotechnical screening, bearing-capacity note, drainage review, access-road assessment and preliminary layout suitability for digesters, feedstock yards, composting pads, storage sheds and gas systems.
  6. Environmental and contamination screening — Assess soil, water, dust, mine-water influence, acid mine drainage risk, heavy metals, leachate pathways and environmental suitability for biomass and biosolids processing.
  7. Feedstock availability assessment — Map the availability of agricultural residues, cattle dung, poultry litter, market waste, food waste, forestry residues, invasive biomass, sawdust, STP sludge, faecal sludge and other biosolids within a practical transport radius.
  8. Feedstock and biosolids quality testing — Test biomass and biosolids for moisture, calorific value, volatile solids, C:N ratio, grit, plastics, pathogens, heavy metals, odour potential and other contaminants before deciding the technology route.
  9. Technology suitability assessment — Identify suitable options such as anaerobic digestion, CBG / Bio-CNG, biogas power, pelletization, briquetting, composting, co-composting, biochar, gasification or integrated bioenergy park based on feedstock quality and market demand.
  10. Indian policy and compliance alignment — Map alignment with SATAT for CBG offtake, GOBARdhan / waste-to-wealth objectives, Solid Waste Management Rules, CPCB environmental guidelines for CBG / Bio-CNG plants and FCO requirements for sale of compost / organic manure. SATAT was launched to create a CBG ecosystem for automotive market use, while CPCB guidelines provide environmental guidance for CBG / Bio-CNG plants.
  11. Biosolids safety and handling framework — Prepare a biosolids acceptance protocol covering source approval, pathogen control, heavy-metal limits, odour management, vector control, rejection criteria, storage duration and worker PPE. Sewage sludge / biosolids management in India requires dedicated safeguards for treatment, utilization and disposal.
  12. Water, leachate and drainage planning — Assess process-water availability, treated mine-water use, STP water use, rainwater harvesting, contaminated runoff separation, leachate collection and treatment arrangements.
  13. Fire, gas and occupational safety screening — Prepare preliminary safety requirements for biomass fire risk, dust, methane, H₂S, gas holders, compressors, confined spaces, electrical safety, emergency access and firefighting water.
  14. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult local administration, Pollution Control Board, ULBs, panchayats, STPs, sanitation agencies, agriculture department, renewable-energy agency, nearby villages, SHGs, FPOs, farmers and local transporters.
  15. Market and revenue assessment — Estimate demand and revenue from CBG / Bio-CNG, biogas, electricity, steam, pellets, briquettes, compost, digestate, biochar, waste-processing fee, tipping fee and carbon / ESG benefits.
  16. Preliminary zoning and buffer plan — Prepare a basic site layout showing feedstock receipt, biosolids handling, digestion / processing zone, composting area, gas zone, product storage, leachate treatment, green buffer, access roads and no-go areas.
  17. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5–10 year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, DBFOT, BOOT or EPC + O&M.
  18. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, operation, feedstock sourcing, biosolids handling, environmental compliance, local employment, SHG / FPO participation, testing, reporting and product marketing.
  19. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering bioenergy experience, biosolids handling capability, safety record, regulatory compliance, financial capacity, feedstock strategy, O&M plan, community integration and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / bioenergy operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, foundation assessment, hydrology review, drainage survey and access-road design.
  2. Detailed feedstock and biosolids confirmation — Finalize feedstock sources, quantities, transport routes, seasonal availability, quality parameters, source agreements and biosolids acceptance / rejection criteria.
  3. Detailed environmental and safety planning — Prepare Environmental Management Plan, Odour Management Plan, Leachate Management Plan, Fire Safety Plan, Gas Safety Plan, Occupational Health and Safety Plan and Emergency Response Plan.
  4. Final technology selection — Select the final technology package, such as CBG / Bio-CNG, anaerobic digestion, biogas engine, pelletization, briquetting, composting, co-composting, biochar or hybrid technology, based on approved feedstock and commercial model.
  5. Final master planning — Prepare the final bioenergy master plan showing plant area, biomass yard, biosolids receiving area, digesters, gas systems, composting pads, leachate drains, storage, utilities, green buffer, internal roads and emergency access.
  6. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — The selected operator shall prepare the DPR covering detailed surveys, process design, mass balance, energy balance, final layout, financial model, statutory approvals plan, O&M plan, safety plan and implementation schedule for owner approval.
  7. Approvals, consents and registrations — Obtain Consent to Establish, waste-processing permissions, fire safety approvals, factory / labour registrations, electrical approvals and other applicable permissions before construction.
  8. Product and offtake agreements — Finalize offtake arrangements for CBG / Bio-CNG, biogas, electricity, steam, pellets, briquettes, compost, digestate or biochar, including OMC linkage where SATAT-based CBG sale is proposed. SATAT is specifically intended to encourage entrepreneurs to set up CBG production plants and make CBG available through PSU OMCs.
  9. Detailed engineering and procurement — Prepare engineering designs and procure digesters, gas holders, gas cleaning systems, compressors, shredders, pumps, conveyors, pellet / briquette machines, compost turners, safety systems and laboratory equipment.
  10. Construction and installation — Develop plant foundations, feedstock platforms, covered sheds, leachate drains, water systems, digesters, gas systems, product storage, weighbridge, roads, fencing, green buffer and utilities.
  11. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for feedstock receipt, biosolids handling, segregation, digestion, gas safety, odour control, composting, product testing, emergency response, maintenance and incident reporting.
  12. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train plant operators, safety staff, laboratory staff, feedstock handlers, transport coordinators, maintenance workers, SHG / FPO members and local youth.
  13. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, FPOs, farmers, sanitation workers, local transporters and micro-enterprises for biomass collection, feedstock aggregation, compost marketing, logistics and support services.
  14. Trial operation and performance testing — Conduct trial runs, feedstock testing, gas-yield testing, product-quality testing, odour checks, leachate checks, emergency drills and third-party safety review.
  15. Consent to Operate and commercial readiness — Obtain Consent to Operate and complete pre-commissioning checks before commercial operation; compost or organic manure intended for sale should meet applicable FCO quality requirements. FCO regulates notified organic fertilizers such as city compost, vermicompost, phosphate-rich organic manure and organic manure.
  16. Commissioning and commercial operation — Start regular operation after owner approval, statutory compliance, staff readiness, product offtake arrangements, safety systems and monitoring systems are in place.
  17. Regular monitoring and compliance reporting — Carry out routine monitoring of feedstock quantity, biosolids quality, gas generation, emissions, leachate, odour, product quality, worker safety, local employment and revenue.
  18. Community grievance and risk management — Operate a grievance-redressal system for odour, traffic, waste handling, worker safety, local employment and environmental concerns.
  19. Phased expansion — Expand capacity, add new feedstock sources, introduce pellet / briquette / biochar / compost lines or scale CBG output based on performance, compliance record, market demand and owner approval.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Covers the indicative 17-step implementation pathway, preferred models in order, recommended development position, key references, and domain abbreviations.

The following implementation pathway presents a practical high-level sequence for advancing the proposed repurposing concept from initial approval through to project execution and long-term operation. It outlines the broad stages of concept approval, preliminary studies, model selection, bid preparation, procurement, detailed design, statutory approvals, construction, commissioning, and ongoing operation. This pathway is indicative and should be adapted to reflect the specific complexity, regulatory requirements, financing structure, and site conditions applicable to the project.

  1. Concept approval and objective setting
  2. Site suitability, landform and soil assessment
  3. Environmental, contamination and safety screening
  4. Feedstock and biosolids availability assessment
  5. Water availability and water quality assessment
  6. Technology option assessment and preliminary sizing
  7. Community engagement and SHG / FPO onboarding
  8. Market and buyer demand assessment
  9. Bioenergy cluster zoning and technology selection
  10. Selection of implementation model
  11. DPR and bid structuring
  12. RFP issuance and partner selection
  13. Bioenergy, water, power, safety and access infrastructure development
  14. Feedstock supply-chain establishment
  15. Training and livelihood integration
  16. Trial operation, product testing and market linkage testing
  17. Commercial operation, monitoring and scaling

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models (in order):

  1. Hybrid Model
  2. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate
  3. DBFOT
  4. BOOT

Secondary Option: EPC + O&M, for welfare-driven, owner-controlled bioenergy clusters where the mine owner wants strong control over public assets, land use, feedstock sourcing, biosolids safety, community livelihood, product quality and technical standards.

Recommended Development Position: The Hybrid Model is most suitable because bioenergy projects require reliable year-round feedstock supply, safe biosolids handling and testing, suitable bioenergy technology selection, strong environmental compliance, fire and gas safety systems, skilled plant operation, product market linkage, community participation, working capital for feedstock procurement, structured risk sharing, phased capital investment, and convergence with CSR, MNRE / renewable-energy schemes, ULBs, panchayats, SHGs, FPOs, banks, private buyers and technical institutions. A bioenergy cluster is a mine-closure circular-economy and livelihood asset that can combine land repurposing, biomass management, biosolids treatment, renewable energy, local employment, sanitation linkage, women-led enterprise, skill development, waste-to-resource conversion, carbon reduction and ESG value.
