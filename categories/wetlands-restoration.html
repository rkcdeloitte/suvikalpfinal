Indian Precedents
Mine Land Biodiversity Recovery — SCCL, Singareni, Telangana
This concept proposes the development of Wetlands Restoration / Constructed Wetland and Mine Waterbody Ecological Landscapes on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land, mine voids, pit lakes, low-lying reclaimed areas, overburden drainage zones and mine water discharge areas into ecologically functional, water-sensitive, biodiversity-supporting and community-linked wetland landscapes centred around restoration, water-quality improvement, biodiversity habitat, eco-tourism and local livelihood.
Wetlands restoration is particularly suited for closed coal mine areas because many mine-closure landscapes already contain water-filled voids, pit lakes, seasonal drainage channels, low-lying reclaimed areas, sedimentation ponds and mine water discharge points. These can be converted into ecologically functional wetland systems if safety, water quality, slope stability and hydrological continuity are properly addressed. This aligns with the Wetlands (Conservation and Management) Rules, 2017, the National Plan for Conservation of Aquatic Ecosystems, and CCO mine-closure handbooks A.R.T.H.A, L.I.V.E.S and R.E.C.L.A.I.M.

PROJECT VISION

Project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability assessment and indicative scales for wetland restoration on closed coal mine land.

This concept proposes the development of Wetlands Restoration / Constructed Wetland and Mine Waterbody Ecological Landscapes on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy.

The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land, mine voids, pit lakes, low-lying reclaimed areas, overburden drainage zones and mine water discharge areas into ecologically functional wetland landscapes centred around:

  • Restoration of mine waterbodies, pit lakes, low-lying depressions and seasonal wetlands
  • Creation of constructed wetlands for polishing mine water, stormwater and treated wastewater where technically suitable
  • Native aquatic, marsh, riparian and wetland-edge vegetation
  • Bird, butterfly, amphibian, fish and pollinator habitat creation
  • Flood buffering, stormwater retention and groundwater recharge support
  • Nature-based water-quality improvement and erosion control
  • Environmental education, birdwatching, interpretation and low-impact eco-tourism
  • Community-based wetland maintenance, nursery, fishery-support, guide, monitoring and visitor-service livelihoods
  • Long-term carbon, biodiversity, ESG and mine-closure value creation

Unlike conventional built infrastructure, wetlands restoration can use existing mine voids, depressions, drainage lines, pit lakes and reclaimed lowlands; is compatible with eco-parks, biodiversity parks, walking trails, bird hides, interpretation centres and mine-water treatment systems; and allows phased investment depending on landform, water quality, safety and hydrological conditions.

Wetlands restoration is particularly suited for closed coal mine areas because many mine-closure landscapes already contain water-filled voids, pit lakes, seasonal drainage channels, low-lying reclaimed areas, sedimentation ponds and mine water discharge points. Wetlands provide multiple ecosystem services:

  • Converts mine voids, pit lakes, lowlands and drainage zones into productive ecological assets
  • Supports water-quality improvement through sedimentation, filtration, microbial action, plant uptake and wetland biogeochemistry
  • Provides stormwater retention, flood moderation and erosion control
  • Enhances biodiversity through wetland vegetation, bird habitat, amphibian habitat, pollinator zones and fish refuges
  • Creates long-term local jobs in maintenance, nursery raising, vegetation management, monitoring, guided nature trails, security, visitor services and eco-tourism
  • Supports women SHGs, youth groups and FPOs through wetland nurseries, birdwatching guides, maintenance contracts and visitor services
  • Provides environmental education for schools, colleges, mine workers and surrounding communities
  • Enhances ESG, biodiversity, carbon and mine-closure compliance outcomes
  • Offers diversified revenue: entry tickets, birdwatching, wetland nursery sales, training programmes, interpretation centre income, CSR/grant support and eco-tourism facilities

The Nature Conservancy India's Sembakkam Lake restoration (100 acres, Tamil Nadu) and Wular Lake restoration (Jammu & Kashmir) are strong Indian precedents for scientific, economic and participatory wetland restoration approaches.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP readiness requirements and post-award implementation activities for wetlands restoration on closed coal mine land.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

  1. Land ownership and closure-status confirmation
  2. Mine closure and repurposing permissibility note
  3. Safe and unsafe zone mapping
  4. Topographic survey and contour mapping
  5. Bathymetric survey of pit lakes / deep waterbodies where applicable
  6. Slope-stability and subsidence screening
  7. Hydrological assessment including catchment, inflow, outflow, water balance and seasonal water-level variation
  8. Water-quality assessment including pH, EC, DO, BOD, COD, TSS, turbidity, metals, sulphate, oil and grease
  9. AMD risk assessment where mine drainage is acidic or metal-rich
  10. Soil and sediment testing, including fertility, pH, compaction and contamination
  11. Drainage and erosion assessment
  12. Flood-risk and overflow route assessment
  13. Existing vegetation and biodiversity baseline
  14. Invasive species assessment
  15. Fish, bird, amphibian and wetland habitat potential assessment
  16. Community and beneficiary mapping
  17. Consultation with nearby villages, panchayats, SHGs, FPOs, fisheries department, forest department, wetland authority, irrigation department, KVKs, schools and local institutions
  18. Market assessment for wetland eco-tourism, birdwatching, guided trails, nursery plants, training programmes, visitor services and interpretation centre revenue
  19. Preliminary wetland zoning plan
  20. Estimate of CAPEX and 5-year maintenance cost
  21. Proposed implementation model and draft livelihood obligations
  22. Legal review of waterbody ownership, wetland notification implications, fishing rights, visitor access, revenue sharing, vegetation harvesting restrictions, safety responsibilities and land-use restrictions

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Recommended way forward, indicative implementation pathway, key references and glossary for wetlands restoration on closed coal mine land.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models (in order):

  • Hybrid Model
  • Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate
  • EPC + O&M / Facility Management
  • DBFOT

Secondary option: EPC, for basic earthworks, hydraulic structures, fencing, planting and initial restoration where the mine owner wants full control and can separately arrange long-term wetland maintenance.

Recommended Development Position: The Hybrid Model is the most suitable model because wetlands restoration requires long-term hydrological management, water-quality monitoring, scientific wetland design, ecological restoration and biodiversity management, public safety management, community participation, visitor services and interpretation, nursery and maintenance operations, invasive species control, phased capital investment, and institutional partnerships. Wetlands restoration is not only a beautification or revenue project — it is a mine-closure legacy asset that can combine water management, ecological restoration, biodiversity conservation, climate resilience, education, eco-tourism, local livelihood and ESG value.
