R&D Sites
Indian Precedent
IIT Madras Research Park, Chennai — India's first university-based R&D & incubation park

An R&D Site is a knowledge-based mine-closure repurposing option that transforms suitable reclaimed mine land, workshops, and buildings into an applied research, testing, incubation, and technology-development campus. It can take the form of a micro / pilot R&D unit, an applied research and testing laboratory cluster, a renewable-energy and mine-closure demonstration yard, a start-up incubation centre, or a full mine-area research and technology park — depending on site conditions, regional demand, partner ecosystem, and funding model.

Unlike heavy industrial repurposing, a research-led reuse model creates a knowledge-based post-mining economy. It can support applied research, technology testing, start-up incubation, environmental and mine-water monitoring, renewable-energy demonstrations, materials and pilot-scale testing, and reskilling of local youth and mine workers — while productively reusing existing mine buildings, workshops, utilities, and reclaimed land, and enabling industry-academia-government collaboration under real post-mining field conditions.

PROJECT VISION

Post-mining context, R&D-led configurations, site suitability, and indicative scale benchmarks before committing to an R&D site / applied innovation campus in a closed coal mine area.

This concept proposes the development of Research and Development Sites / an Applied Innovation and Technology Development Campus within or around a closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine area, as part of a broader post-mining land-use, industrial transition, skill development, and regional innovation strategy. The objective is to transform suitable mine land, workshops, laboratories, training centres, administrative buildings, reclaimed land, utilities, roads, and electrical infrastructure into an R&D-led asset that supports applied research, technology testing, start-up incubation, industrial problem-solving, environmental monitoring, mine-closure innovation, renewable-energy demonstrations, material testing, skilling, prototyping, pilot-scale trials, and industry-academia collaboration.

This should be treated as a selective repurposing option, not a universal one. A closed coal mine is suitable for an R&D site only where it can satisfy critical requirements relating to safe and legally usable land or buildings; distance from unstable mine voids, pits, dumps, shafts, and subsidence-prone areas; reliable power, internet, water, and sanitation; structural safety of buildings proposed for reuse; environmental safety and contamination control; compatibility with mine-closure conditions; availability of technical partners and users; fire, electrical, laboratory, and occupational safety; safe handling of hazardous, electronic, chemical, or biological materials where applicable; and long-term operation, maintenance, governance, and funding capability.

Depending on site potential and project ambition, the project may include one or more of the following components:

  • Applied research & testing laboratories: environmental and mine-water testing; soil, overburden, and material testing; electronics, instrumentation, and prototyping.
  • Renewable-energy testing & demonstration yard: solar, wind, hydrogen, battery, energy-efficiency, and micro-grid demonstrations.
  • Mine-closure & land-reclamation research centre: reclamation, slope monitoring, erosion control, and mine-water treatment trials.
  • Drone, GIS, remote-sensing & digital mine-monitoring centre; robotics, automation, and sensor development laboratory.
  • Fabrication, welding, machining & repair workshop; clean-technology and circular-economy innovation centre.
  • Start-up incubation & co-working / technology business incubation centre; IP, technology-transfer, and industry-liaison cell.
  • Pilot-scale testing sheds for mining, energy, water, and materials technologies; digital command and data-analytics room.
  • Training rooms, seminar halls, conference facility, guest-researcher rooms, administrative offices, stores, and outdoor testing plots.

This is an early-stage concept and structuring framework — not a final DPR, architectural design, laboratory accreditation plan, environmental-impact assessment, safety certification, research-institution approval, or concession package.

An R&D site can be a strong repurposing option where a closed coal mine offers existing industrial land with controlled access; reusable workshops, stores, offices, or laboratories; existing roads, electrical systems, water-handling infrastructure, and a security perimeter; proximity to mining, power, manufacturing, engineering, environmental, or academic institutions; mine-related problems that can become research and testing opportunities; and scope for industry-academia-government collaboration.

This option differs from ordinary training centres, warehouses, tourism, parks, or recreation facilities because it is knowledge-led, technology-intensive, institution-driven, and capable of creating long-term innovation value. It is not only a community asset; it is a research, technology, incubation, and industrial-transition asset with social, educational, environmental, and commercial co-benefits.

Key strategic benefits:

  • Productive reuse of mine buildings, workshops, and reclaimed land — converting a closed mine into a technology and innovation site.
  • Support for research on mine reclamation, water treatment, slope monitoring, and environmental recovery.
  • Creation of a local innovation ecosystem linked with universities, start-ups, and industries.
  • Support for clean-energy testing — battery systems, solar, wind, hydrogen, energy efficiency, and micro-grid demonstrations.
  • Development of local capacity in drones, GIS, sensors, instrumentation, automation, and data analytics.
  • Testing of technologies under real post-mining field conditions not possible in dense urban areas.
  • Local employment and skill-transition opportunities for mine workers, ITI trainees, diploma holders, and engineering graduates.
  • Potential revenue from leases, lab services, testing fees, training, incubation charges, industry projects, and sponsored research.

The concept should be advanced only where the site can support safe and compliant R&D activity. Building safety, environmental suitability, laboratory safety, partner ecosystem, equipment maintenance, data connectivity, operating revenue, governance, staffing, and regulatory compliance matter more than the mere availability of land.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP owner readiness, post-award developer scope, and the indicative implementation pathway for R&D site projects on closed mine sites.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an R&D-site-based mine-repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, mine-closure and permissibility confirmation — confirm land ownership, closure-plan status, reclaimed land status, end-use permissibility, public / institutional / industrial R&D use, leaseability and restrictions.
  2. Mine-safety and no-go zone mapping — map pits, highwalls, shafts, subsidence zones, dumps, unstable areas, unsafe water bodies, old mine infrastructure and restricted areas.
  3. Building and infrastructure condition assessment — assess existing buildings, workshops, stores, electrical systems, roads, water supply, drainage, internet, fire safety and repair needs.
  4. R&D theme and demand assessment — identify suitable themes such as mine closure, environment, water, renewable energy, materials, robotics, sensors, GIS, safety, circular economy or industrial testing.
  5. Institutional and industry ecosystem mapping — identify potential universities, research institutions, engineering colleges, ITIs, industries, MSMEs, start-ups, CSR partners and government agencies.
  6. Preliminary space and phasing plan — prepare concept layout for labs, workshops, testing yards, incubation space, offices, stores, training rooms, utilities, parking and future expansion.
  7. Utilities and digital readiness assessment — confirm electrical load, transformer, backup power, water, wastewater, HVAC requirement, internet, data systems and cybersecurity requirement.
  8. Environmental and contamination screening — check coal dust, hydrocarbons, heavy metals, acidic water, asbestos, contaminated soil, noise, vibration and indoor air quality.
  9. Laboratory safety and approval screening — identify fire, electrical, pollution control, hazardous material, biosafety, chemical storage, waste-management and occupational-safety requirements.
  10. Revenue and operating model assessment — assess potential income from lab rentals, testing services, incubation, training, sponsored research, pilot trials, industry projects, consultancy and grants.
  11. Implementation model and risk-allocation decision — select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, EPC + O&M, BOOT or EPC based on scale, CAPEX, risk appetite and private-sector interest.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — prepare bidder scope covering design, refurbishment, equipment, labs, pilot facilities, operations, safety, environmental compliance, local employment, reporting, revenue sharing and handback obligations.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / developer / EPC contractor / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site, building and utility assessment — building measurement, structural audit, utility audit, environmental sampling, electrical load study, ICT assessment, fire-safety review and access planning.
  2. Detailed R&D master planning — finalize lab blocks, workshop zones, pilot sheds, testing yards, incubation space, training rooms, stores, administrative areas, safety zones and expansion areas.
  3. Detailed laboratory and equipment planning — laboratory-wise equipment lists, technical specifications, utility requirements, furniture plans, instrument-room requirements and maintenance standards.
  4. Statutory approvals and clearances — building-use permissions, fire safety, electrical inspection, pollution control permissions, hazardous storage approvals, biosafety / chemical permissions where applicable, and institutional approvals.
  5. Final engineering design — architectural, structural, electrical, HVAC, plumbing, fire, ICT, laboratory furniture, gas-line, waste-management and safety designs.
  6. Financial closure and partnership agreements — project finance, partner MoUs, anchor-tenant agreements, sponsored research arrangements, industry collaborations and incubation model.
  7. Procurement and construction preparation — procure equipment, furniture, HVAC, ICT, safety systems, workshop tools, lab instruments, field systems and pilot-scale testing infrastructure.
  8. Construction, refurbishment and installation — building repair, lab interiors, utilities, workshop setup, testing yards, pilot sheds, access roads, security systems and waste-management infrastructure.
  9. Installation of instruments and digital systems — analytical instruments, sensors, data loggers, computers, servers, GIS systems, drones, calibration tools, monitoring systems and cybersecurity controls.
  10. Safety systems, SOPs and staffing — laboratory SOPs, emergency plans, waste protocols, IP policies, visitor rules, O&M manuals, training plans and staff deployment.
  11. Local livelihood and vendor integration — engage local technicians, trainees, field monitors, sample handlers, security staff, housekeeping, transport providers, maintenance vendors and trained youth.
  12. Testing, trial operation and validation — equipment testing, calibration, safety drills, sample-workflow trials, pilot batches, user onboarding and operational-readiness review.
  13. Commercial operation and compliance monitoring — begin formal operation and continue lab safety audits, environmental compliance, equipment maintenance, revenue tracking, research-output reporting and annual performance review.
  14. Phased expansion or optimization — expand into additional labs, pilot facilities, start-up blocks, clean-tech facilities, research partnerships, certification pathways or advanced testing only after stable utilization and safety validation.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward, preferred models, key references, and abbreviations for R&D site projects on closed mine sites.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

An R&D site in a closed mine area is likely to have both public-transition value and commercial potential. Based on the PRISM assessment, the preferred implementation models, in order, are:

  1. Hybrid
  2. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate
  3. DBFOT

Hybrid is preferred because the owner can provide land, buildings, utilities, safety works and mine-closure linkage, while universities, research institutions, start-ups, industries, CSR partners and specialist operators provide equipment, research programmes, staffing, incubation and commercialization.

Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is also highly suitable because R&D sites require professional operation, long-term maintenance, anchor users, market linkage and flexible expansion. The owner can retain land control while granting development and operating rights to a qualified partner.

DBFOT is suitable for larger R&D parks where private finance, structured concession obligations, anchor tenants, research partnerships and eventual transfer are desirable. As a secondary option, EPC + O&M may be used where a PSU, government agency, university, research institution or strategic mine owner wants to retain ownership and control but requires specialist laboratory and facility-management support.

Suggestable technical care: land permissibility, mine-safety buffer, building stability, environmental contamination, electrical load, digital connectivity, laboratory safety, hazardous-material control, HVAC, wastewater management, equipment maintenance, institutional demand, staff capability and recurring revenue must be screened carefully.

Recommended development logic: apply knockout screening first — safe land / building, mine-hazard separation, environmental suitability, power and internet availability, R&D demand, institutional partner and a recurring operating model. Only passing sites should move to pre-feasibility, market sounding and bid structuring.
