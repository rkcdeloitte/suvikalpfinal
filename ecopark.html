Eco Park
Precedents
NLC Eco-Park, Neyveli, Tamil Nadu, India

Eco parks are being increasingly developed in India to promote environmental conservation, eco-tourism, and recreational activities. These parks provide green spaces for nature-based tourism, biodiversity conservation, environmental awareness, and community engagement while supporting sustainable development and local economic growth.

The closed coal mine can be repurposed into eco parks by utilizing reclaimed land, mine voids, water bodies, and restored green areas for development of landscaped gardens, walking trails, boating facilities, biodiversity zones, and recreational spaces. This approach supports ecological restoration, sustainable post-mining land use, tourism promotion, environmental education, and livelihood generation for local communities.

Project Vision

Overview, strategic rationale, site suitability and indicative scale configurations for eco-park and mine tourism development on closed coal mine land.

An Eco-Park on closed mine land transforms a post-mining liability into a long-term public green asset — combining ecological restoration, community recreation, mine tourism, and local livelihood generation. Closed mine areas offer unique built-in advantages:

  • Haul roads and access routes: Converted into walking trails, cycling tracks and visitor vehicle access
  • Levelled surface areas: Ideal for lawns, play zones, seating, outdoor gyms and event spaces
  • Mine water bodies and pit lakes: Lakefront development, boating, viewpoints and waterfront recreation where geotechnically safe
  • Overburden dumps: Reclaimed and stabilised into terraced viewpoints, nature trails and green landscapes
  • Existing mine buildings: Workshops and offices repurposed as interpretation centres, visitor facilities, ticketing booths and cafeterias
  • Power infrastructure: Existing substations support solar lighting, CCTV, PA systems and visitor amenities
  • Labour pool: Former mine workers reskilled as guides, gardeners, security staff, boating operators and visitor-service teams

Eco-park development on closed mine land addresses multiple strategic imperatives simultaneously:

  • Just Transition: Provides a post-mining livelihood pathway for displaced mine workers, SHGs and surrounding communities
  • Mine Closure Compliance: Supports mine closure plan obligations related to land reclamation, greening and community development
  • ESG and Corporate Responsibility: Enhances coal company ESG ratings, demonstrates environmental stewardship and social accountability
  • Revenue Generation: Entry fees, boating, kiosk rentals, events, eco-tourism and CSR receipts provide long-term revenue streams
  • Tourism Integration: Links closed mine areas to national and state tourism circuits — mine tourism is an emerging segment in India
  • Biodiversity and Carbon: Native plantation and habitat creation contribute to carbon sequestration, biodiversity corridors and ecological restoration targets
  • Public Good: Converts idle hazardous land into productive, safe, publicly accessible green infrastructure — a lasting mine-closure legacy asset

Repurposing Pathways

Implementation options, comparative model assessment and responsibility logic for eco-park development on closed mine land.

The following table outlines the possible implementation models that may be considered for developing the proposed repurposing project. These models differ in terms of ownership structure, funding responsibility, private sector participation, operational control, and long-term risk allocation. The purpose of this table is to help the project owner compare broad development routes and select an implementation approach that aligns with its financial capacity, institutional preference, risk appetite, and long-term asset management objectives.

  • EPC (Engineering, Procurement & Construction): Mine owner funds and directs the full build of eco-park infrastructure — owner retains complete control and all revenue; suitable for welfare-led, publicly accessible parks
  • EPC + O&M: Build by contractor, operations managed by a professional facility-management agency — owner retains revenue while outsourcing day-to-day operations and technical management
  • Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate (LDO): Private operator or SHG/FPO takes lease, develops and operates — revenue-sharing model with strong community livelihood integration
  • DBFOT (Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Transfer): Full PPP for large eco-parks with visitor facilities, eco-tourism and boating — private developer finances, builds and operates; transfers after concession period
  • BOOT (Build-Own-Operate-Transfer): Developer builds and owns until transfer — suitable where private investment in visitor infrastructure is needed
  • Hybrid Model: Combination of mine owner capital, CSR funding, government schemes, private operator, SHG/FPO and NGO support — most balanced and widely recommended for eco-parks

Funding logic, revenue generation, livelihood impact and combined financial comparison across implementation models.

The following table presents the broad funding logic for each implementation model. It explains who is expected to finance the project, the level of owner-side funding requirement, and the commercial rationale behind each structure. This section is intended to help the project owner assess whether the project is more suitable for owner-funded development, private investment, public-private partnership, lease or concession structure, or a hybrid funding arrangement.

  • Mine Closure Fund / Owner Budget: Primary source for EPC and EPC+O&M models — covers infrastructure, fencing, pathways, lighting, landscaping and interpretation
  • CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility): Available from coal companies and neighbouring industries — particularly suitable for livelihood, community and ecological components
  • CAMPA (Compensatory Afforestation Fund): National CAMPA Authority funds for plantation, ecological restoration and green buffer development on mine land
  • Private Developer Capital: Used in DBFOT and BOOT models — developer finances visitor infrastructure and recovers through revenue over concession period
  • Viability Gap Funding (VGF): Government support to bridge financial viability gap for large eco-parks where commercial returns are insufficient without subsidy
  • Government Schemes: MGNREGS (labour for land development), SHG funds, state tourism grants, district-level development funds — complement primary sources
  • Revenue Reinvestment: Profits from entry, boating, kiosk rentals and events reinvested in park maintenance, expansion and community programs

Strategic Structuring

Pre-RFP readiness, post-award activities and indicative implementation pathway for eco-park development on closed mine land.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an eco-park-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land and closure confirmation — Confirm land ownership, closure status, mine-closure linkage and permissibility of eco-park / mine-tourism / recreational use under the approved Mining Plan and Mine Closure Plan.
  2. Post-mining land-use and statutory screening — Review lease conditions, land-use restrictions, local body permissions, commercial rights, user charges, revenue sharing and applicable forest, environment or water-body restrictions.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Map unsafe areas such as unstable dumps, highwalls, subsidence-prone zones, mine voids, unsafe water bodies, active mine interfaces and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical site assessment — Prepare topographic survey, drainage review, slope-stability screening, geotechnical screening and water-body safety note where applicable.
  5. Environmental and reclamation status review — Assess reclaimed land condition, plantation status, soil quality, dust exposure, water quality, contamination risk and ecological sensitivity.
  6. Eco-park activity suitability assessment — Identify feasible components such as walking trails, cycling tracks, play areas, viewpoints, picnic lawns, interpretation zones, boating and eco-tourism facilities based on safety and terrain.
  7. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult district administration, tourism department, police, fire services, health department, panchayats, nearby communities, SHGs, youth groups and local entrepreneurs.
  8. Preliminary zoning and visitor management plan — Prepare a basic layout showing entry plaza, visitor centre, eco-park zones, water-body buffers, activity areas, emergency routes, parking, no-go zones and preliminary carrying capacity.
  9. Market and revenue assessment — Estimate visitor demand and revenue potential from entry tickets, parking, guided tours, food kiosks, boating, events, exhibitions, local products and eco-tourism services.
  10. CAPEX, O&M and model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 5-year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT or EPC + O&M.
  11. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Define bidder scope for design, construction, operation, maintenance, visitor safety, landscaping, local employment, SHG participation, kiosk allocation, waste management and environmental safeguards.
  12. Bid evaluation framework — Set evaluation criteria covering eco-park experience, safety record, landscape and tourism capability, financial capacity, community integration, O&M plan, revenue proposal and ESG commitments.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / eco-park operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, drone mapping, geotechnical investigation, slope assessment, water-body assessment, drainage study and utility mapping.
  2. Detailed environmental and safety planning — Prepare the Environmental Management Plan, Safety Management Plan, Emergency Response Plan, visitor-flow plan, fire-safety plan and final carrying-capacity assessment.
  3. Final eco-park master planning — Prepare the final master plan showing visitor circulation, landscape zones, recreation areas, interpretation areas, water-body buffers, emergency routes, parking, service areas and local enterprise zones.
  4. Detailed engineering and landscape design — Prepare detailed designs for pathways, cycling tracks, viewpoints, decks, lawns, play areas, lighting, toilets, kiosks, drainage, irrigation, seating, signage, fencing and public amenities.
  5. Detailed Project Report — Prepare the DPR covering final surveys, master plan, engineering drawings, landscape design, cost estimates, statutory approval plan, O&M plan, financial model and implementation schedule.
  6. Approvals, permissions and insurance — Obtain local body permissions, tourism-related approvals, fire and safety clearances, water-body permissions where applicable and public liability insurance.
  7. Procurement of materials and systems — Procure landscape materials, certified play equipment, lighting, CCTV, public-address system, waste bins, benches, signage, digital ticketing system, safety equipment and maintenance tools.
  8. Construction and installation — Develop the entry plaza, internal paths, visitor centre, parking, toilets, kiosks, lawns, plantations, viewpoints, lighting, fencing, water-body protection, signage and emergency access.
  9. Operating procedures and maintenance plan — Prepare SOPs for ticketing, visitor movement, crowd control, cleaning, waste management, water-body safety, emergency response, landscape maintenance and incident reporting.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train gardeners, security staff, guides, ticketing staff, housekeeping workers, maintenance teams, kiosk operators and local youth for visitor-service roles.
  11. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, food vendors, parking operators, souvenir sellers, local guides, nursery workers, transport operators and community enterprises through defined contract conditions.
  12. Trial operation and safety audit — Conduct trial runs, mock drills, emergency-response checks, lighting checks, visitor-flow testing and third-party safety inspection before public opening.
  13. Branding and tourism linkage — Develop eco-park branding, online information, school outreach, signage, tourism-circuit linkage, event calendar and district tourism coordination.
  14. Commissioning and public opening — Open the eco-park after successful trial operation, safety clearance, staff readiness, visitor-management systems and maintenance arrangements.
  15. Regular operation and monitoring — Carry out daily O&M, visitor management, safety inspection, waste management, plantation maintenance, revenue reporting, environmental monitoring and compliance reporting.
  16. Phased expansion — Expand facilities in phases based on visitor demand, safety record, revenue performance, environmental compliance, community feedback and mine-owner approval.

Monitoring Guidance

Way forward, references and abbreviations for eco-park and mine tourism implementation on closed coal mine land.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because eco-parks require ecological restoration, public safety, visitor services and community livelihood. The hybrid mix should include owner-funded landscape restoration + private visitor-service operator + NGO / community participation + local vendor obligations.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the owner wants full control over public access, ecological outcomes and park maintenance.

Suggestable technical care: Slope safety, water-body safety, planting, trails, lighting, waste management, visitor carrying capacity, security and emergency response must be planned.

Recommended development logic: Start with fencing, trails, plantation, seating, toilets, kiosks and interpretation. Add boating, cycling, events and eco-tourism only after safety and demand are proven.
