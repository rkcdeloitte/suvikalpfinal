UNDERGROUND VAULTS / WAREHOUSE
Precedents
Mangalore LPG Cavern approx. capacity 6 Lacs barrels
Underground Vaults / Warehouse development transforms technically suitable underground mine voids, tunnels, galleries, caverns, decline accesses, and associated surface infrastructure into secure, climate-stable, commercially usable storage and warehousing facilities. The concept covers secure document and records vaults, archive storage, high-security vaults, general warehouse space, temperature-stable storage, cold-chain storage, digital-media and disaster-recovery vaults, e-commerce inventory, and logistics staging — converting a post-mining asset into a long-term revenue-generating commercial facility.
International examples demonstrate the viability of underground mine reuse for commercial warehousing. SubTropolis in Kansas City was developed from a former limestone mine into a major underground business and warehousing complex. Iron Mountain has used former mine environments for secure records, media, and data-related storage. Unlike tourism- or community-focused repurposing, underground vaults are primarily an economic reuse and asset-monetization project aligned with A.R.T.H.A., L.I.V.E.S. and R.E.C.L.A.I.M. mine-closure frameworks and India's National Logistics Policy 2022.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability and technical specification matrix, and indicative scale models — from a small pilot vault facility to a flagship underground warehouse and business park.

This concept proposes the development of underground vaults / warehouse facilities within a closed or repurposed mine area as part of a post-mining land-use strategy. The objective is to transform technically suitable underground mine voids, tunnels, galleries, caverns, decline accesses, or surface infrastructure into secure, climate-stable, commercially usable storage and warehousing facilities.

Components may include: secure document and records storage vaults; archive storage for government, institutional, corporate, legal, medical, and financial records; high-security vaults for sensitive files, art objects, rare books, films, and media; general underground warehouse space; temperature-stable storage; cold-chain or refrigerated storage where feasible; digital media and disaster-recovery storage; e-commerce, spare-parts, and industrial-supplies storage; logistics staging and cross-dock support; and surface support facilities including weighbridge, parking, admin office, ventilation plant, and emergency-response facilities.

International precedents: SubTropolis in Kansas City was developed from a former limestone mine into a major underground business and warehousing complex. Iron Mountain has used former mine environments for secure records, media, document, and data-related storage — demonstrating that underground mine voids can become long-term commercial assets.

  • Productive reuse of underground mine voids, access tunnels, caverns, service galleries, and selected surface assets — converting a closed mine into a revenue-generating commercial facility
  • Potential use of naturally stable underground temperatures for storage and reduced cooling / heating loads, subject to site-specific conditions
  • Secure storage due to limited access points and controlled entry / exit — suitable for archives, vaults, disaster-recovery, and records management
  • Reduced surface footprint compared with equivalent above-ground warehousing
  • Possible adaptive reuse of mine roads, portals, service buildings, ventilation shafts, workshops, electrical infrastructure, and internal haulage routes
  • Generation of local employment in conversion works, operations, security, loading, inventory handling, maintenance, ventilation, utilities, transport, and facility management
  • Potential support for regional industrial users, government record rooms, banks, hospitals, educational institutions, e-commerce companies, logistics operators, and manufacturers
  • Support for ESG, sustainable mine closure, circular land use, and post-mining economic diversification
  • Potential integration with surface logistics parks, data centres, cold-chain facilities, and regional warehouse networks

Caution: Coal mines, gassy mines, highly fractured areas, flooded workings, unstable roofs, high-temperature mines, and mines with serious subsidence or fire risk may require extensive treatment or may be unsuitable for public or commercial storage use.

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Describes what must be ready before the first RFP — underground survey, stability assessment, mine-hazard screening, demand assessment, fire-safety concept, connectivity — and what can be taken up post-award.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an underground vaults / warehouse-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, closure and use permissibility — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, underground access rights, and permissibility of non-mining storage / warehousing use.
  2. Legal and commercial screening — Review land-use restrictions, concession rights, leasing rights, user charges, revenue sharing, insurance, liability, and warehouse registration requirements.
  3. Mine safety and no-go mapping — Identify unsafe roofs, weak pillars, shafts, goaf areas, flooded zones, gas-prone areas, fire-risk areas, subsidence zones, and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary technical assessment — Prepare underground layout check, geotechnical screening, roof / pillar stability review, portal assessment, drainage review, and ventilation-path review.
  5. Environmental and storage suitability screening — Assess water ingress, humidity, air quality, residual contamination, flood risk, and suitability for records storage, vaults, pallet storage, cold storage, or secure warehousing.
  6. Fire, emergency and security concept — Prepare basic fire compartment, emergency exit, smoke-control, refuge, fire-water, CCTV, access-control, and portal-security concept.
  7. Preliminary zoning and capacity plan — Prepare a basic layout showing storage zones, vault areas, loading / unloading area, internal circulation, no-go zones, emergency routes, utilities, parking, and surface support facilities.
  8. Market, cost and model assessment — Estimate demand, storage revenue, broad CAPEX, 5-year O&M, and select a preferred model such as Concession / LDOT, DBFOT, Hybrid, BOOT, or EPC + O&M.
  9. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — Define bidder scope, safety obligations, insurance requirements, O&M standards, local employment conditions, revenue proposal format, and technical / financial evaluation criteria.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / warehouse operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed investigation — Undertake detailed underground survey, 3D mapping, geotechnical investigation, roof / pillar assessment, gas testing, ventilation survey, drainage study, and portal assessment.
  2. Detailed planning and DPR — Prepare final master plan, safety plan, environmental plan, fire plan, emergency plan, ventilation plan, drainage plan, warehouse layout, financial model, O&M plan, and DPR for mine-owner approval.
  3. Approvals and insurance — Obtain mine-safety permissions, fire NOC, electrical approvals, local-body permissions, warehouse registration where applicable, certifications, and insurance cover.
  4. Detailed engineering and procurement — Design and procure rock support, floor treatment, ventilation, pumps, electrical systems, lighting, fire systems, CCTV, access control, racking, forklifts, WMS, RFID / barcode systems, and emergency communication systems.
  5. Construction and installation — Execute stabilization, roof support, floor paving, drainage, ventilation, electrical works, fire systems, security systems, loading facilities, office facilities, signage, and warehouse fit-out.
  6. SOPs, staffing and training — Prepare SOPs for goods intake, storage, retrieval, vehicle movement, restricted goods, fire / flood response, security, inspection, maintenance, and train local staff including former mine workers where suitable.
  7. Trial operation and safety audit — Conduct trial storage, mock drills, evacuation test, fire-system test, ventilation test, pump test, security test, material-handling trial, and third-party safety audit.
  8. Commissioning and operations — Finalize customer contracts, tariffs, service-level agreements, access protocols, inventory systems, and start operations after safety clearance and mine-owner approval.
  9. Monitoring and phased expansion — Carry out routine O&M, structural monitoring, ventilation checks, fire-system inspection, pump maintenance, humidity monitoring, security audit, compliance reporting, and phased expansion based on demand and safety performance.

Secondary Option: EPC + O&M is suitable where the owner wants direct control over tenants, pricing, security, and underground asset use.

Recommended Development Logic: Start with feasibility for records storage, archive storage, secure vaults, or general warehousing. Avoid high-risk goods unless fire, ventilation, insurance, and safety compliance are fully established. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate is preferred because underground warehousing needs commercial demand, security, ventilation, fire systems, and professional warehouse management — the owner can retain underground asset control while transferring commercial and operating risk.

Critical technical care: Roof stability, flooding, ventilation, fire suppression, emergency exits, access control, CCTV, humidity, drainage, insurance, goods liability, and warehouse management systems must be ensured before any public or commercial use of underground mine spaces.
