Final Recommendations / Apiculture Zones (Beekeeping & Pollination Landscapes)
Apiculture (Beekeeping)
Global Precedents
Aladağlar National Park Apiary, Turkey
In India, apiculture (beekeeping) offers a sustainable pathway for livelihood generation and ecological restoration in post-mining landscapes by utilizing available floral resources and promoting biodiversity. Reclaimed mine lands provide strong potential to integrate beekeeping with afforestation, enhancing honey production and supporting rural livelihoods.
This initiative can generate income and employment opportunities for local communities, especially those previously dependent on mining activities, by providing low-investment and quick-return livelihood options. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including ensuring availability of diverse floral resources, providing training and technical support to beekeepers, managing environmental factors, and establishing market linkages to ensure fair pricing and sustainability of apiculture-based enterprises.

P
PROJECT VISION

Apiculture is uniquely suited for closed coal mine areas — it requires no high-fertility soil, supports ecological restoration through pollination, and generates livelihoods with minimal land disturbance. This pillar covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability, and indicative project scales.

This concept proposes the development of Apiculture Zones / Beekeeping and Pollination Landscapes on closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine land as part of a sustainable mine-closure strategy. The objective is to convert under-utilised mine land into a livelihood-generating, ecology-restoring, circular-economy-based apiculture ecosystem centred around honey production, bee colony management, floral landscape development, pollination services, value-added bee products, and local employment through SHGs, FPOs and beekeeping cooperatives.

India is the world's largest honey producer with over 1.33 lakh MT annual output. Closed mine sites with reclaimed land, water infrastructure, road access and nearby communities offer a strong physical and social basis for apiculture-based repurposing. This aligns with CCO mine-closure handbooks A.R.T.H.A, L.I.V.E.S and R.E.C.L.A.I.M and the 2025 mine-closure policy shift toward inclusive, just-transition development.

  • No dependence on high soil fertility — suitable for reclaimed or marginal mine land with minimal soil preparation
  • Enhances biodiversity and ecological restoration through pollination of mine-area plantations and native vegetation
  • High livelihood intensity per hectare — especially for women SHGs, small producers and land-loser families
  • Low CAPEX compared to plantation or construction models — bee boxes, basic infrastructure and training are the primary investments
  • Supports integration with agroforestry, orchards, and eco-restoration zones already planned under mine closure
  • Generates diversified revenue streams: honey, beeswax, pollen, propolis, royal jelly, eco-tourism, pollination services
  • Strong market demand — domestic and export market for quality certified honey and bee products is growing
  • Alignment with climate, ESG and just-transition goals — measurable biodiversity, livelihood and ecological co-benefits

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

This pillar covers pre-RFP readiness requirements, activities that can be taken up post-award, and an indicative implementation pathway from concept approval to operational apiculture enterprise.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP, the mine owner should preferably complete the following activities in a phased and priority-based manner:

  1. Land ownership and mine-closure status confirmation, including confirmation that apiculture and allied livelihood activities are permissible under the approved Mine Closure Plan and applicable Ministry of Coal / CCO guidelines.
  2. Safe and unsafe zone identification, including topographic survey, slope-stability assessment, subsidence screening, and identification of suitable reclaimed land parcels for apiary development.
  3. Environmental and site suitability assessment, covering contamination screening, dust and pollution risk, water availability, drainage, climate suitability, and floral-resource assessment for bee forage.
  4. Food-safety and honey-quality risk assessment, including testing plan for honey quality, heavy metals, pesticide residues, dust contamination, water quality, processing hygiene and FSSAI compliance.
  5. Community and stakeholder consultation, including nearby villages, panchayats, SHGs, FPOs, KVKs, KVIC, National Bee Board, and relevant agriculture / horticulture departments.
  6. Market and value-chain assessment for honey, beeswax, pollen, propolis, pollination services, processing, branding, and local enterprise opportunities.
  7. Preliminary apiary zoning and infrastructure planning, including hive clusters, floral augmentation zones, access roads, processing area, training facilities, storage, and safety buffers.
  8. Assessment of funding and scheme convergence opportunities, including NBHM, Honey Mission, SFURTI, CSR, DMF, DAY-NRLM, PMEGP, and other applicable Government support mechanisms.
  9. Preliminary CAPEX and O&M estimation, including bee boxes, processing equipment, training, floral development, operations, and maintenance requirements.
  10. Selection of preferred implementation model, such as Hybrid, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, or EPC + O&M, based on livelihood, operational, and commercial objectives.
  11. Preparation of draft livelihood obligations, monitoring indicators, food-safety framework and legal structure, including FSSAI registration / licence applicability, honey testing protocol, produce ownership, branding rights, benefit-sharing, revenue sharing, community participation, liability allocation, insurance and recall / quality-control responsibility.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award of the project, the selected agency / concessionaire / operator may undertake the following activities in a phased implementation manner:

  1. Detailed apiary planning and implementation strategy, including hive layout, phased development, and operational scheduling.
  2. Bee species selection and hive deployment planning, including suitable bee varieties, colony management, and migration strategy where required.
  3. Floral augmentation and site development, including bee-friendly plantation, water points, shade arrangements, fencing, and access improvements.
  4. Development of honey extraction, processing, packaging, and storage infrastructure, along with food safety and quality-control systems compliant with FSSAI requirements.
  5. Establishment of SHG / FPO participation framework, including livelihood integration, beneficiary onboarding, and community engagement mechanisms.
  6. Training and capacity-building programmes for local communities, beekeepers, women SHGs, and youth beneficiaries.
  7. Bee health, disease management, and operational maintenance systems, including colony monitoring, seasonal feeding, migration planning, pest and predator control, pesticide-exposure prevention, veterinary / expert support where required, and emergency response for colony loss.
  8. Market linkage, branding, and value-chain development — Commercial sale should begin only after FSSAI registration / licensing applicability is confirmed, honey-quality testing is completed, packaging and labelling requirements are met, and batch-level traceability is established.
  9. Digital MIS, monitoring, and reporting systems, including tracking of hive survival, honey production, beneficiary participation, and revenue indicators.
  10. Development of sustainability, eco-tourism, and expansion initiatives, including awareness programmes, ESG-linked activities, biodiversity assessment, and long-term O&M framework.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

This pillar provides the recommended way forward for apiculture mine repurposing, key references for further reading, and a glossary of abbreviations used throughout the PRISM framework.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because apiculture requires ecological restoration, flowering vegetation, training, community participation and market linkage. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner plantation support + SHG / FPO beekeeper participation + technical beekeeping agency + honey processing / branding partner.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be used for demonstration or welfare-driven apiculture projects where the owner funds basic infrastructure and training.

Suggestable technical care: Floral calendar, pesticide exposure, water source, hive protection, training, bee species selection, processing hygiene, storage, branding and market linkage must be addressed.

Recommended development logic: Begin with pilot bee boxes linked to plantation areas and SHG training. Scale into honey processing, wax products, branding and eco-tourism sales only after colony survival and market demand are proven.

Final recommendation: Hybrid Model is most suitable because apiculture is low-land, livelihood-oriented and works best when linked with eco-restoration and community enterprise.
