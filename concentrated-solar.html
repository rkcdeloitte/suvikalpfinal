Final Recommendations / Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)
CONCENTRATED SOLAR POWER (CSP)
Global Precedents
Noor Ouarzazate CSP Plant, Morocco
Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) projects convert closed mine land into utility-scale dispatchable renewable energy infrastructure. Unlike solar PV, CSP concentrates solar radiation using mirrors or reflective surfaces to generate heat, which drives a conventional power block. A key advantage is the ability to incorporate thermal energy storage (TES) — allowing generation beyond daylight hours and improving dispatchability for grid stability. Closed mine land can offer large contiguous tracts suitable for utility-scale CSP, particularly where terrain is manageable and solar resource (DNI) is strong.
India's Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) supports concentrated solar thermal development as part of its solar energy roadmap. CSP technologies include parabolic trough, solar tower, linear Fresnel, and dish systems — each with different land requirements, water use, and storage potential. The World Bank and IRENA both recognize CSP with thermal storage as a critical technology for flexible renewable generation in emerging markets. Mine-land repurposing for CSP directly supports ESG, mine closure, and India's Net-Zero 2070 trajectory.

PROJECT VISION

Covers the project concept, strategic rationale, site suitability matrix for CSP, and indicative technology-capacity scale matrix — from a 5–10 MW pilot to a 100–200 MW+ utility-scale project with thermal energy storage.

This concept proposes the repurposing of closed mine land for Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) development as part of a sustainable mine-closure and post-mining land-use strategy. Unlike solar PV, CSP generates electricity by concentrating solar radiation using mirrors to produce heat that drives a conventional power block (steam turbine / generator). A key differentiator is the ability to integrate Thermal Energy Storage (TES), allowing generation beyond daylight hours and improving grid dispatchability.

CSP technologies include: Parabolic Trough (most deployed globally); Solar Tower / Central Receiver (suitable for large scale, high TES integration); Linear Fresnel (lower CAPEX, less efficient); Dish / Stirling (small scale, remote applications). Each has different land requirements, water demand, and storage integration potential.

Global Reference: Noor Ouarzazate (Morocco) — 510 MW CSP complex with thermal storage, demonstrating viability of large-scale CSP on semi-arid landscapes. India's MNRE supports solar thermal as part of its renewable energy technology portfolio.

Important: CSP is technically complex, capital-intensive, and sensitive to DNI quality. Mine-area CSP projects should proceed only after DNI validation and water / cooling feasibility are confirmed. If CSP is not viable, Solar PV or FSPV with BESS may be more practical alternatives.

  • Productive reuse of large contiguous closed mine land for utility-scale clean energy generation
  • Dispatchable renewable generation through thermal storage — generation beyond daylight hours
  • Reduced pressure on agricultural and other competing land uses
  • Support for ESG, mine-closure visibility, and India's Net-Zero 2070 commitment
  • Potential to reuse existing mine roads, substations, and transmission access infrastructure
  • Local employment during construction (civil, mechanical, electrical, piping) and long-term O&M
  • Alignment with MNRE solar thermal programme, RPO obligations, and REC mechanisms
  • Industrial decarbonisation support — dispatchable solar power for nearby industries
  • Revenue through electricity sale, captive use, PPA, REC, and thermal storage premium

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Details the 13-item pre-RFP checklist including DNI validation, land stability, water/cooling strategy, grid connectivity, technology selection, and what can be taken up post-award by the selected developer or concessionaire.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land title and closure-position confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, post-mining land-use permissibility, access corridor, and whether the proposed CSP use is consistent with the approved closure framework and internal land-allocation position.
  2. Legal and statutory screening — Screen land-use restrictions, lease / concession conditions, forest and wildlife triggers, environmental-clearance applicability, utility-interface requirements, and whether water-use approvals, SPCB consents, or local building permissions are likely to be triggered.
  3. Safe and developable area demarcation — Identify buildable and non-buildable zones, including unstable reclaimed land, subsidence-prone areas, restricted infrastructure corridors, drainage channels, safety buffers, and setback requirements.
  4. Preliminary topographic and geotechnical package — Complete topographic survey, contour mapping, slope assessment, geotechnical screening, drainage review, and preliminary grading requirement assessment because CSP is landform-sensitive and foundation-intensive.
  5. Preliminary DNI and climate screening — Prepare a Direct Normal Irradiation (DNI) screening note, seasonal cloud profile, ambient-temperature profile, wind and dust assessment, and broad climate-suitability note for CSP technology selection.
  6. Water and cooling feasibility screening — Assess raw-water source, recycled / industrial-water option, groundwater dependence, cooling approach feasibility, and whether a CGWA NOC or equivalent water abstraction permission pathway is likely to apply.
  7. Grid connectivity and evacuation screening — Identify the nearest pooling point / substation, voltage level, tentative bay availability, evacuation route, metering and protection implications, and the applicable utility / STU / CTU interface for connectivity processing under prevailing grid standards.
  8. Preliminary technology and capacity optioning — Prepare a concept note comparing parabolic trough, solar tower, linear Fresnel, likely storage hours, tentative capacity range, and broad land-water-grid fit.
  9. Environmental and pollution-control screening — Carry out preliminary screening for dust, runoff, wastewater, mirror-washing discharge, hazardous-material handling if any, and the likely pathway for Consent to Establish (CTE) / other pollution-control permissions if applicable.
  10. Commercial and implementation-model assessment — Prepare broad CAPEX, preliminary O&M estimate, storage-related cost implications, and identify the preferred structuring model such as DBFOT, BOOT, Concession / BOO, or EPC + O&M.
  11. Stakeholder and utility consultation — Consult internal departments, state utility / STU, DISCOM if relevant, local administration, water-resource authority, SPCB, and safety / mine-closure stakeholders before finalizing bid conditions.
  12. Draft RFP scope and risk-allocation framework — Prepare bidder scope covering surveys, detailed design, CSP technology selection, thermal storage, cooling system, power block, grid interface, statutory approvals, safety compliance, local employment, water management, and performance obligations.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation parameters covering CSP experience, thermal-storage capability, grid-integration capability, land-development approach, water strategy, safety record, financial capacity, and lifecycle O&M capability.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected developer / EPC contractor / concessionaire / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site investigation — Undertake detailed topographic survey, geotechnical investigation, settlement-risk assessment, drainage study, constructability assessment, and detailed land-development planning.
  2. Bankable solar-resource assessment — Carry out detailed DNI measurement / validation, climate-risk assessment, soiling study, and long-term yield modelling for the selected CSP technology.
  3. Final technology selection and plant optimization — Finalize CSP configuration, solar-field layout, thermal-storage design, power-block rating, cooling system, auxiliary loads, and net-generation model.
  4. Final master planning and engineering — Prepare the detailed plant layout covering mirror field, HTF / molten-salt system, storage area, turbine-generator block, water system, switchyard, internal roads, drainage, control room, workshop, and safety systems.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — Prepare the DPR with detailed surveys, energy model, engineering package, financial model, statutory matrix, construction methodology, O&M philosophy, and implementation schedule for owner approval.
  6. Statutory approvals and utility processing — Obtain or support processing of project-specific approvals, including utility connectivity / evacuation approvals, SPCB consent pathway, groundwater / water-use permissions, and any building / fire / local approvals as applicable.
  7. Electrical-system compliance and inspection planning — Finalize metering, protection, communication, switchyard, and evacuation design in line with applicable CEA technical standards and the concerned utility / electrical-inspectorate process.
  8. Procurement and package finalization — Procure long-lead CSP packages such as mirrors / heliostats, receivers, storage system components, piping, turbine-generator equipment, transformers, switchyard equipment, SCADA, and safety systems.
  9. Construction and installation — Execute site grading, foundations, solar-field installation, storage-system erection, power-block construction, water / cooling system installation, switchyard works, and internal infrastructure development.
  10. Commissioning procedures and performance testing — Carry out pre-commissioning, synchronization, heat-balance checks, storage-system commissioning, protection testing, trial runs, and contractual performance tests.
  11. Operations readiness and training — Prepare SOPs, maintenance systems, spare-parts planning, emergency-response protocols, SCADA workflows, and training for operators, technicians, and local workforce.
  12. Commercial operation and compliance monitoring — Begin regular operation after successful commissioning, utility clearance, safety readiness, and compliance closure, followed by routine O&M, water-use monitoring, environmental monitoring, and performance reporting.
  13. Phased optimization / expansion — Take up storage optimization, capacity augmentation, or performance-improvement measures in phases subject to owner approval, land availability, and evacuation headroom.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order:

  1. DBFOT / BOOT — most suitable because CSP is capital-intensive and requires specialist design, solar-resource assessment, thermal storage, and long-term performance risk management
  2. Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate — land control retained; professional CSP operator develops and manages
  3. Hybrid — mine-owner land + approvals support + private CSP developer + power offtake partner
  4. EPC + O&M — suitable only where the owner has strong funding, captive power demand, and technical capacity

Key technical safeguards required: DNI assessment, land stability, solar-field layout, cooling water requirement, thermal storage, grid connectivity, dust control, mirror cleaning water, and offtake structure must be assessed before commitment.

Final recommendation: Proceed only after DNI and water suitability are proven. If CSP feasibility is weak, Solar PV or FSPV with BESS is likely more practical.
