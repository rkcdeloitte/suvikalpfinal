Final Recommendations / Pollinator / Wildlife Corridors
Pollinator Corridors
Precedent
Kanha–Pench Wildlife Corridor, NH-44, Madhya Pradesh — India's first dedicated wildlife underpass

Pollinator / Wildlife Corridors convert closed coal mine land — including overburden dumps, reclaimed slopes, safety buffers, abandoned haul roads, drainage lines, and unused industrial strips — into a connected ecological landscape that supports pollinators, birds, small mammals, reptiles, amphibians, butterflies, bees, beneficial insects, and native vegetation. Where landscape context permits, they also support movement of larger wildlife between existing habitat patches.

Mining often fragments habitat, alters drainage, removes vegetation, and separates ecological patches. A well-planned corridor can convert a closed mine landscape from an ecological break into a functional landscape link — supporting biodiversity recovery, pollination services, soil stabilization, carbon sequestration, climate adaptation, environmental education, and community-based livelihood through nurseries, seed collection, beekeeping, eco-guiding, and biodiversity monitoring.

PROJECT VISION

Post-mining context, corridor configurations, site suitability, and indicative scale benchmarks for Pollinator / Wildlife Corridor repurposing on closed coal mine land.

This concept proposes the development of Pollinator / Wildlife Corridors within or around a closed, reclaimed, or repurposed coal mine area as part of a broader post-mining land-use, ecological-restoration, biodiversity, climate-resilience and community-development strategy.

The objective is to transform suitable mine land, overburden dumps, reclaimed slopes, safety buffers, plantation belts, abandoned haul roads, mine-water edges, drainage lines, grassland patches, scrubland, forest-edge areas, riparian strips and unused industrial buffers into a connected ecological landscape that supports pollinators, birds, small mammals, reptiles, amphibians, butterflies, bees, beneficial insects, native vegetation, and where appropriate, larger wildlife movement between existing habitat patches.

This should be treated as a selective repurposing option, not a universal one. A closed coal mine is suitable only if it can satisfy critical requirements relating to availability of contiguous or connectable land parcels, ecological connection with nearby forests, wetlands, grasslands, agricultural fields, river corridors or biodiversity patches, mine-safety separation from unstable pits, highwalls, shafts and subsidence zones, soil stability, water availability, human-wildlife conflict risk, compatibility with mine-closure plan, and long-term maintenance capability.

Depending on site potential and project ambition, the proposed project may include one or more of the following components:

  • Native grassland restoration zones
  • Pollinator meadow strips with nectar and host plants
  • Butterfly gardens and bee-forage zones
  • Native tree and shrub corridors
  • Riparian buffer restoration along drains, streams or mine-water outflows
  • Bird habitat patches with fruiting and nesting vegetation
  • Wetland edges, amphibian ponds and seasonal water points where safe
  • Stepping-stone habitat patches between larger ecological areas
  • Overburden dump slope greening and erosion-control planting
  • Wildlife movement strips away from human disturbance
  • Hedgerows, shelterbelts and living fences
  • Wildlife underpass / overpass linkage planning where roads interrupt movement
  • Invasive species removal and native species enrichment
  • Biodiversity monitoring plots, camera traps, acoustic sensors and pollinator transects
  • Community nursery, seed bank and native-plant propagation unit
  • Interpretation trails, watch points and controlled eco-education zones
  • Local livelihood activities: nursery raising, habitat maintenance, beekeeping, nature guides and monitoring assistants

A Pollinator / Wildlife Corridor can be a strong repurposing option where a closed coal mine site offers reclaimed land that can be converted into native vegetation, long mine-safety buffers or unused linear strips that can function as ecological corridors, overburden dumps suitable for slope stabilization and native planting, and proximity to forest patches, river corridors, agricultural landscapes or biodiversity areas.

This option differs from ordinary plantation, landscaping or beautification because it is connectivity-led, species-sensitive, habitat-based and long-term. It is not merely a green-cover activity; it is an ecological infrastructure project designed to support movement, nesting, feeding, breeding, pollination, genetic exchange and landscape-level biodiversity recovery.

Key strategic benefits include:

  • Productive ecological reuse of mined and reclaimed land
  • Restoration of native vegetation and habitat structure
  • Creation of pollinator resources for bees, butterflies and beneficial insects
  • Support for birds, reptiles, amphibians and small mammals
  • Stabilization of slopes, dumps and erosion-prone areas
  • Improvement of soil organic matter and microclimate
  • Support for nearby agriculture through pollination and beneficial insect habitat
  • Reduction of dust and heat-island effects in former mine areas
  • Integration of mine closure with biodiversity and climate-resilience goals
  • Community-based livelihood through native nurseries, seed collection, habitat maintenance, ecological monitoring, beekeeping and guided nature education
  • Low-carbon and nature-based repurposing option compared with built development
  • Long-term visibility of transition from extraction to ecological restoration

The concept should be advanced only where the site can support safe, durable and ecologically meaningful restoration. Ecological connectivity, native species selection, soil recovery, water availability, invasive control, fire management, human-wildlife conflict, legal status and long-term maintenance matter more than simply planting trees.

S
STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Pre-RFP owner readiness, post-award developer scope, and the indicative implementation pathway for Pollinator / Wildlife Corridor projects on closed mine sites.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for a Pollinator / Wildlife Corridor based mine-repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, mine-closure and permissibility confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, reclaimed land status, ecological end-use permissibility, leaseability and restrictions on public access, forest land, water bodies or safety zones.
  2. Mine-safety and no-go zone mapping — Map pits, highwalls, shafts, dumps, subsidence areas, unsafe benches, deep water bodies, unstable slopes and restricted mine infrastructure.
  3. Ecological baseline survey — Undertake preliminary flora, fauna, pollinator, bird, reptile, amphibian, invasive-species, soil and habitat-condition survey.
  4. Landscape connectivity assessment — Identify nearby forests, wetlands, rivers, grasslands, agricultural edges, village commons and existing biodiversity patches that can be connected.
  5. Target species and habitat objective selection — Decide whether the first phase will focus on pollinators, butterflies, birds, small wildlife, riparian habitat, grassland restoration, wildlife movement or mixed biodiversity.
  6. Soil, slope and water assessment — Test soil, assess spoil quality, pH, compaction, erosion, drainage, water availability, mine-water risks and early-stage irrigation requirement.
  7. Human-wildlife conflict and community-use screening — Identify grazing routes, fuelwood use, agriculture interface, village concerns, livestock movement, snake risk, crop damage risk and public access issues.
  8. Native species and planting strategy — Prepare local native plant list covering grasses, herbs, shrubs, trees, nectar plants, host plants, fruiting species, wetland plants and soil-binding species.
  9. Protection and maintenance strategy — Prepare approach for fencing, live barriers, grazing control, fire lines, invasive control, watering, gap filling, nursery support and survival monitoring.
  10. Legal, forest, wildlife and water approval screening — Identify forest clearance, wildlife clearance, eco-sensitive zone issues, wetland rules, water-body safety, biodiversity committee involvement and local permissions.
  11. Implementation model and risk-allocation decision — Select preferred model such as Hybrid, EPC + O&M, Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, DBFOT, BOOT or EPC based on scale, ecological risk, funding and local participation.
  12. Draft RFP scope and bid framework — Prepare bidder scope covering ecological design, baseline survey, site preparation, native nursery, planting, fencing, maintenance, monitoring, community engagement, livelihood integration, reporting and handback obligations.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / developer / EPC contractor / O&M agency can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed ecological and site surveys — Undertake detailed flora, fauna, pollinator, bird, soil, slope, hydrology, invasive-species, disturbance and connectivity surveys.
  2. Detailed corridor planning — Finalize corridor alignment, habitat patches, stepping stones, buffer zones, no-entry areas, public access trails, water points, nursery area and monitoring plots.
  3. Detailed soil and restoration design — Prepare soil amendment plan, topsoil handling, composting, mulch, biochar use, erosion-control measures, slope stabilization and drainage treatment.
  4. Statutory approvals and permissions — Obtain forest / wildlife approvals where applicable, water-body permissions, local body approvals, biodiversity committee support, CSR approvals and community agreements.
  5. Native nursery and seed collection — Establish nursery, collect local seeds, propagate native grasses, herbs, shrubs and trees, and prepare species-wise planting calendar.
  6. Site preparation and protection works — Undertake decompaction, grading, erosion control, fencing, fire lines, access paths, water-storage systems, signage and hazard barricading.
  7. Habitat creation and planting — Implement native planting, pollinator meadows, hedgerows, grasslands, shrub patches, tree belts, riparian buffers, wetland-edge planting and water points.
  8. Invasive species control and habitat enrichment — Remove or control invasive species, conduct gap planting, add nesting substrate, logs, rock piles, bee banks, bird perches and microhabitat features.
  9. Community stewardship and livelihood integration — Engage local workers, self-help groups, nursery teams, beekeepers, eco-guides, fire watchers, monitoring assistants and school eco-clubs.
  10. Monitoring systems and baseline validation — Install camera traps, transect routes, pollinator counts, bird monitoring, vegetation plots, GIS database and annual biodiversity reporting format.
  11. Safety systems and operating procedures — Prepare visitor rules, no-entry zones, fire response, snake encounter protocol, water-body safety, emergency contacts and maintenance SOPs.
  12. Trial opening and adaptive management — Open selected education trails or demonstration zones only after safety validation, and adjust planting and protection based on survival and species use.
  13. Long-term operation and compliance monitoring — Continue watering, weeding, invasive control, fire protection, biodiversity monitoring, community engagement, reporting and annual ecological review.
  14. Phased expansion or optimization — Expand corridor length, add stepping-stone habitats, connect to adjoining forest / wetland patches, introduce eco-education facilities or strengthen livelihood activities only after stable establishment.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Way forward, preferred models, key references, and abbreviations for Pollinator / Wildlife Corridor projects on closed mine sites.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred models, in order of recommendation:

  • Hybrid — preferred because Pollinator / Wildlife Corridors usually produce high ecological and social value but limited direct commercial revenue. The owner can provide land, mine-safety works, basic fencing, water support and closure-plan linkage, while ecological agencies, CSR partners, forest institutions, NGOs, universities, panchayats and community groups can support native planting, monitoring, maintenance and livelihood integration.
  • EPC + O&M / Facility Management — highly suitable because corridor success depends not only on initial planting but on multi-year maintenance, invasive control, fire management, monitoring and adaptive ecological management. The owner retains control while a specialist ecological agency ensures technical quality.
  • Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate — suitable where an experienced conservation NGO, ecological restoration company, CSR foundation, biodiversity-credit developer or nature-based enterprise is willing to invest, manage and monitor the corridor under defined ecological performance conditions.

Secondary option: DBFOT or BOOT may be used for larger biodiversity corridors, eco-restoration parks, pollinator landscapes or nature-based livelihood zones where long-term concession support, CSR sponsorship, conservation finance, eco-education or service payments can sustain operations.

Technical care required: Ecological connectivity, target species, native plant selection, soil recovery, slope stability, water availability, invasive species, fire risk, grazing pressure, human-wildlife conflict, community acceptance, monitoring and legal approvals must be screened carefully.

Recommended development logic: Apply knockout screening first — land availability, mine-hazard separation, ecological connectivity, soil restoration feasibility, water support, community acceptance and legal permissibility. Only passing sites should move to baseline survey, ecological design, market / partner sounding and implementation structuring.
