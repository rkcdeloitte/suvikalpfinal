Aquaculture
Precedents
Circular Cage Fish Farming on Mine Pit Lake

In India, aquaculture in mine pit lakes presents a promising pathway for sustainable livelihood generation and productive reuse of post-mining landscapes by utilizing water-filled voids with suitable quality parameters. With numerous abandoned mines, there is considerable potential to convert these water bodies into aquaculture hubs, supporting fish production and enhancing food security while contributing to rural economic development.

This initiative can create employment and income opportunities for local communities, particularly those previously dependent on mining, by transforming idle water resources into viable fisheries enterprises. However, successful implementation requires addressing key challenges, including maintaining appropriate water quality, providing technical training and inputs to fish farmers, ensuring ecological balance, and developing efficient market linkages and cold-chain infrastructure to support sustainable aquaculture practices.

PROJECT VISION

Converting mine voids, pit lakes, and water-filled depressions into productive blue livelihood systems — enabling food security, rural income, and long-term value from under-utilised water assets.

This concept proposes the development of Aquaculture Zones on closed, reclaimed, abandoned, or water-filled coal mine areas. The objective is to convert under-utilised mine voids, pit lakes, water bodies, subsidence depressions, and reclaimed land into productive, income-generating, community-oriented blue livelihood systems centred around fish culture, cage culture, fingerling rearing, ornamental fishery, integrated fishery-linked livelihoods, fish processing and value addition, and eco-tourism and recreational fisheries.

Unlike conventional agriculture, aquaculture requires limited fertile soil, can use mine pit lakes and water-filled voids, can support nutrition security and local livelihoods, and can be integrated with wetlands, eco-parks, plantation zones, and tourism landscapes.

  • No dependence on high soil fertility — suitable for reclaimed or marginal mine land
  • Productive use of mine pit lakes and water-filled voids
  • Supports protein security and rural livelihood generation
  • High livelihood intensity through fish farming, netting, feeding, harvesting, sorting, packaging, transport, and marketing
  • Suitable for SHGs, fisher groups, FPOs, cooperatives, displaced families, and local youth
  • Integration with eco-parks, wetlands, plantations, and tourism zones
  • Diversified revenue streams: table fish, fingerlings, cage-cultured fish, processed fish, ornamental fish, aquatic plants, recreational angling, and eco-tourism
  • Strong domestic demand for fish and fish products
  • Alignment with blue economy, ESG, nutrition security, and just-transition goals

STRATEGIC STRUCTURING

Defining the procurement groundwork — what must be ready before the first RFP, what the selected operator takes up post-award, and the indicative implementation pathway from concept to operation.

The following section identifies the key owner-side preparatory actions that should ideally be completed before issuing the first Request for Proposal. These actions are aimed at reducing uncertainty for prospective bidders, improving project bankability, clarifying site availability, identifying approval pathways and risks, and defining the technical and commercial scope of the project. Completing these preparatory steps before tendering can improve bid quality, attract credible responses, and reduce the risk of delays during project implementation.

Before issuing an RFP for an aquaculture-zone-based mine repurposing project, the mine owner should prepare the following in priority order:

  1. Land, closure and water-body confirmation — Confirm land ownership, mine-closure status, mine-closure linkage, water-body ownership and permissibility of fisheries / aquaculture / cage culture use.
  2. Legal and water-use screening — Review land-use restrictions, fishing rights, lease rights, water-use rights, revenue-sharing permissibility, insurance requirement and commercial harvesting rights.
  3. Safety and no-go zone mapping — Identify safe and unsafe zones, including deep pit lakes, unstable highwalls, dump slopes, subsidence-prone areas, active mine areas and restricted infrastructure.
  4. Preliminary water-body assessment — Prepare bathymetry, water-spread area, depth profile, seasonal water-level variation, inflow-outflow status and bank-stability note.
  5. Water quality and contamination screening — Test pH, dissolved oxygen, BOD, COD, turbidity, alkalinity, heavy metals, acid mine drainage risk and sediment contamination before permitting edible fish culture.
  6. Aquaculture suitability assessment — Identify feasible options such as pond culture, cage culture, pen culture, fingerling rearing, ornamental fishery, recreational fishery or integrated aquaculture based on site conditions.
  7. Carrying-capacity and stocking note — Prepare preliminary carrying-capacity, cage-density, species selection and stocking-density assumptions to avoid overloading, eutrophication and fish mortality.
  8. Community and stakeholder consultation — Consult Fisheries Department, local administration, panchayats, fisher groups, SHGs, FPOs / FFPOs, KVKs, nearby communities and potential market partners.
  9. Preliminary zoning plan — Prepare a basic layout showing cage zones, pond zones, nursery area, landing point, feed store, processing area, access routes, buffer zones and no-go areas.
  10. Market and revenue assessment — Estimate demand and revenue from table fish, fingerlings, cage culture, ornamental fish, processed fish, angling, tourism linkage and lease / revenue share.
  11. Cost and implementation model selection — Prepare broad CAPEX and 3–5 year O&M estimates and select a preferred model such as Hybrid, Concession / LDOT, DBFOT or EPC + O&M.
  12. Draft RFP scope and obligations — Prepare bidder scope covering design, construction, stocking, operation, water-quality monitoring, fish health management, local employment, SHG / FFPO linkage and environmental safeguards.
  13. Bid evaluation framework — Define evaluation criteria covering aquaculture experience, technical capability, water-quality management plan, financial capacity, community integration, O&M plan and revenue proposal.

The following section lists the principal activities that may be undertaken by the selected contractor, developer, concessionaire, operator, or implementation agency after award of the project. These activities generally include detailed site investigations, engineering design, statutory submissions and approvals, equipment procurement, construction, commissioning, operational planning, staff training, and performance monitoring. The distinction between pre-RFP owner preparedness and post-award implementation responsibilities helps ensure an appropriate and efficient allocation of effort across the project development cycle.

After award, the selected concessionaire / contractor / O&M agency / aquaculture operator can take up the following activities in chronological order:

  1. Detailed site and water-body investigation — Undertake detailed bathymetric survey, drone mapping, bank-stability assessment, water-depth profiling, sediment testing and seasonal hydrology assessment.
  2. Detailed water quality and environmental plan — Prepare a water-quality monitoring plan, environmental management plan, fish-mortality response plan and pollution-control protocol in line with fisheries good practices.
  3. Final aquaculture master planning — Prepare the final master plan showing cage clusters, pond areas, nursery zones, landing platforms, processing areas, access routes, safety buffers and no-go zones.
  4. Detailed engineering and system design — Prepare detailed designs for cages, ponds, embankments, jetties, feed stores, aeration systems, water-testing points, harvesting platforms and safety barriers.
  5. Detailed Project Report (DPR) — The selected operator shall prepare the DPR covering technical design, species plan, stocking plan, carrying capacity, financial model, approvals plan, O&M plan and implementation schedule.
  6. Approvals, registration and insurance — Obtain required permissions from Fisheries Department / competent authority, local body clearances, insurance cover, and FSSAI registration / licence where processing or packaged fish sale is proposed. FSSAI licensing and registration regulations apply to food businesses, while fish and fish products have specific food-standard requirements.
  7. Procurement of seed, feed and equipment — Procure quality fish seed, feed, cage frames, nets, floats, anchors, aerators, testing kits, harvesting nets, ice boxes, PPE and safety equipment.
  8. Construction and installation — Develop ponds, cage units, landing platforms, fencing, signage, lighting, feed stores, storage space, water points and basic visitor / worker facilities.
  9. Preparation of operating procedures — Prepare SOPs for stocking, feeding, sampling, water testing, fish health checks, harvesting, disease response, mortality disposal, worker safety and emergency response.
  10. Recruitment and training — Recruit and train local youth, SHGs, fishers, FFPO members, technicians, guards, harvest workers and processing staff for aquaculture operations.
  11. Local livelihood integration — Onboard SHGs, fisher cooperatives, FFPOs, transporters, ice suppliers, vendors, processors and local entrepreneurs for fishery-linked income generation.
  12. Trial stocking and pilot operation — Begin with controlled stocking, monitor survival, feed conversion, growth, water quality and mortality before scaling to full commercial production.
  13. Processing, branding and market linkage — Establish cleaning, grading, icing, packaging, branding, cold-chain linkage, wholesale tie-ups, retail linkage and institutional buyer arrangements.
  14. Commissioning and commercial operation — Commission the aquaculture zone after successful pilot performance, water-quality compliance, staff readiness and market-linkage confirmation.
  15. Regular monitoring and phased expansion — Carry out routine O&M, water-quality testing, fish-health monitoring, yield tracking, safety audits, revenue reporting and phased expansion based on performance.

MONITORING GUIDANCE

Recommended models, scaling logic, key references, and abbreviations for aquaculture zone development on post-coal mine water bodies.

The following way forward summarizes the preferred development approach for the proposed repurposing option, based on the assessment presented in PRISM (Post Mining Repurposing Implementation and Strategy Models). It identifies the most suitable implementation models in order of preference, highlights key technical and due-diligence considerations, and outlines the recommended next steps and broad development logic. This section is intended to guide internal decision-making and support the project owner in progressing from concept-stage evaluation toward feasibility assessment, project structuring, regulatory approvals, and implementation planning.

Preferred Models, in order: (1) Hybrid Model, (2) Concession / Lease-Develop-Operate, (3) DBFOT

Why preferred: Hybrid is preferred because aquaculture in mine voids requires owner control over water bodies, safety and permissions while using fisheries expertise and local livelihood participation. The hybrid mix should include mine-owner water-body control + fisheries department / technical agency + SHG / FPO operation + private market-linkage partner.

Secondary option: EPC + O&M may be used for welfare-driven, owner-controlled pilot fishery projects.

Suggestable technical care: Water quality, sediment contamination, fish-food safety, bank stability, drowning risk, fisheries permission, water-use permission, stocking density, disease control and market access must be verified.

Recommended development logic: Start with a 1–5 ha pilot only after water and sediment safety clearance. Scale to 5–20 ha community aquaculture and 20 ha+ integrated aquaculture landscapes with cage culture, processing, training and market linkage.

Final recommendation: Hybrid Model is most suitable because aquaculture combines public safety, environmental control, technical fishery management and community livelihood.
